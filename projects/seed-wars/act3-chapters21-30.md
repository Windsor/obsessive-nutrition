## Chapter 21: "Elena Unchained"

The man strapped to the chair wouldn't stop screaming.

Elena Vasquez stood three feet away, her hand hovering in the air, not touching him. She didn't need to touch him anymore. After two months as Diego Salazar's weapon, she'd learned to feel the biological signatures from across the room—nerve clusters lighting up like constellations, blood vessels mapping themselves in her mind's eye, organs pulsing with borrowed time.

"Make him stop," Salazar said from behind her, voice flat. He was eating an orange, the citrus smell mixing with the copper stench of blood and piss. "I need names."

The man—Carlos, she'd heard them call him—was maybe twenty-five. A rival cartel's accountant. He knew where the money went. Salazar wanted it.

Elena had given him kidney stones. Three of them, jagged as broken glass, working their way through his ureter. She could feel them tearing tissue, nerves screaming. She could make them bigger. Make them multiply. Make them in his lungs, his brain, his heart.

"Please," Carlos sobbed. "Please, I have a daughter—"

Elena's hand dropped. "No."

The warehouse went quiet except for Carlos's ragged breathing. Salazar stopped mid-bite. "What did you say?"

"I said no." Elena turned to face him. Diego Salazar, king of Little Havana, the man who had her mother and brother Miguel locked in a bedroom somewhere in this compound. The man who'd made her dissolve a living person's bones two weeks ago while he screamed for seventeen minutes before going silent.

The man who, this morning, had brought her a ten-year-old boy.

"You're tired," Salazar said carefully. "Take a break. We'll bring your mother in, you can heal that cough she has—"

"You want me to hurt a child."

"I want you to make him tell me where his father keeps the shipping manifests." Salazar's voice hardened. "And I'm not asking."

Elena felt the room like a living map. Salazar's heartbeat, steady but elevated. His two guards flanking the door, hands on their guns. Carlos strapped to the chair, his body a symphony of pain she'd composed. And deeper in the building, beyond concrete walls: her mother's familiar rhythm, Miguel's faster pulse—the bird-quick heartbeat of a scared fifteen-year-old.

For two months she'd told herself she was protecting them. Doing terrible things so they could live. But this morning, looking at that little boy's face—the same age as Miguel—something had broken. Or maybe something had finally healed.

"Bring my family here," Elena said quietly. "Right now. Or I walk out that door."

Salazar smiled. It didn't reach his eyes. "You walk out, they die. You know this."

"No." Elena took a step toward him. "You kill them, I have no reason to hold back. And Diego—" She let her power unfurl, just enough to touch every living thing in the warehouse. Twenty-three men. She could feel them all. "—I can kill everyone in this building before you draw your gun."

The smile vanished. Salazar's hand moved toward his waistband.

Elena reached out with her mind and touched his prostate. Squeezed. Just a little.

Salazar gasped, doubling over. "You fucking—"

"Every disease I've ever healed," Elena said, voice shaking but steady, "I remember. Pancreatic cancer. Sepsis. Ebola. Flesh-eating bacteria. I kept tissue samples in my mind, Diego. Templates. I could give you all of them. Right now. All at once."

She could feel his terror. It tasted like copper and bile.

"Or you could bring me my family. Unharmed. And we all walk out of here alive."

For a long moment, the only sound was Carlos's whimpering and the wet sound of Salazar's orange hitting the floor.

"Boss?" One of the guards shifted, uncertain.

Salazar straightened slowly, one hand pressed to his stomach. His face was gray. "Get them."

---

Twenty minutes later, Elena stood in the warehouse parking lot with her mother's hand in hers and Miguel pressed against her side. Behind them, the building was silent. Not dead silent—she'd left them all alive, even Salazar, even the men who'd held guns to her family's heads.

But she'd given Salazar everything. Every malignancy, every infection, every disorder she'd pulled from dying patients over the years. Compressed it all into his body like stuffing a suitcase. His own men had stared in horror as tumors bloomed across his skin, as blood vessels burst in his eyes, as his organs began the slow cascade toward failure.

She'd left him conscious. Let him feel it.

"You can cure him," she'd told his second-in-command, a man named Raúl. "Get him to a hospital. They might save him. Might." She'd met his eyes. "But if anyone comes after my family—if I even think someone's watching us—I will do this to every single person you love. Do you understand?"

Raúl had nodded, pale and trembling.

Elena unlocked her car—still parked where they'd made her leave it two months ago—and helped her mother into the passenger seat. Miguel slid into the back, silent. He hadn't spoken since the guards brought him down.

As she started the engine, Elena caught her reflection in the rearview mirror. The spiral mark on her palm pulsed faintly, bioluminescent in the darkness. She'd saved so many people with this gift. Healed children, cured the dying, pulled people back from the edge of death.

She'd also learned she could kill with a thought. Twist flesh like clay. Unmake a human body while it still breathed.

The mark didn't care which she chose. The power was the same.

Her mother reached over and squeezed her knee. Didn't say anything. Didn't need to.

Elena drove them away from the warehouse, hands shaking on the wheel, the taste of bile in her throat. She'd won. They were free.

She'd also become the thing she'd sworn she'd never be.

Behind them, in the warehouse, Diego Salazar coughed blood and screamed for a doctor, his body eating itself from the inside. In the parking lot, Elena pressed her forehead against the steering wheel and tried not to think about how good it had felt.

The spiral mark burned cold against her skin.

## Chapter 22: "Contact"

The abandoned steel mill smelled like rust and pigeon shit. Marcus Cole crouched in the shadows of the old smelting floor, his mark hidden beneath a dirty sleeve, listening to the rhythm of footsteps echoing through the empty space.

One person. Light gait. Moving cautiously but not trying to hide.

His body was coiled, ready to absorb and redirect. He'd learned to keep a baseline charge now—ambient kinetic energy from his own heartbeat, the micro-movements of breathing. Enough to throw a punch that could crack concrete. Not enough to accidentally kill someone who startled him.

That had only happened once. It still woke him up at night.

The footsteps stopped. "Marcus Cole?"

A woman's voice. British accent, educated. Marcus stayed silent.

"My name is Amara Osei. I'm unarmed. I work for British intelligence—worked, technically. Past tense as of three days ago." A pause. "I know what you are. I know about Detroit. And I know you didn't mean to kill that man."

Marcus's jaw clenched. The memory: Jake Morrison's body hitting the concrete wall with enough force to shatter his spine. The look on his face before the light went out. Jake had been a good guy. Three kids.

"I'm alone," the woman continued. "If I wanted you captured, there'd be a tactical team. If I wanted you dead, there'd be a drone strike. I want to talk. That's all."

Against his better judgment, Marcus stood. Stepped into the weak light filtering through broken windows. "Hands where I can see them."

She emerged from behind a rusted girder. Forties, dark skin, sharp eyes, wearing jeans and a practical jacket. True to her word, her hands were empty and raised. "Thank you."

"You've got two minutes."

"The seeds were manufactured." Amara lowered her hands slowly, reaching into her jacket. Marcus tensed, but she pulled out a tablet, not a weapon. "Spectral analysis from classified ORCHARD files. These aren't meteorites. They're engineered biological technology. Whoever made them is centuries ahead of us. Maybe millennia."

She held up the tablet. Marcus didn't move closer, but he could see the graphs, the data. It looked legitimate. "So what? Aliens did this to us?"

"Someone did. Something. And they did it deliberately." Amara's voice was steady but urgent. "The meteor shower's trajectory wasn't natural. It was guided. The distribution pattern targeted population centers. These seeds were sent here, Marcus. Sent to bond with us. The question is why."

Marcus thought about the hallucinations during his bonding. Alien landscapes. Something vast and ancient watching him. He'd written it off as fever dreams. "You came all the way to Pennsylvania to tell me about aliens?"

"I came because the people who sent these are still out there. And I don't think they're done with us." Amara took a step closer. "ORCHARD has imprisoned innocent people. Senator Mercer is building a superhuman dictatorship. Trevor Haines has enslaved Portland. The world is tearing itself apart, and we're ignoring the bigger question: what happens when whoever sent the seeds comes back to check on their experiment?"

"Not my problem." Marcus turned to leave. "I've got my own shit to deal with."

"Your daughter."

He froze. Turned back slowly. "Don't."

"Lily Cole, eight years old, living with your ex-wife in Ann Arbor. I'm not threatening her—I'm telling you I know how to protect her." Amara's expression softened. "You're running. I get it. But you can't run forever. Eventually, Mercer's people or ORCHARD or some anti-Seeded militia will find you. And when they do, they'll use her to make you comply."

Marcus's hands clenched. The ambient energy in his body spiked. "If you touch her—"

"I won't. But they will." Amara held his gaze. "Work with me. I have resources, intel, contacts. I know where the black sites are. I know which Seeded are locked up and which are working for Mercer. I know things about the seeds that even ORCHARD doesn't."

"Why would you help me? You don't know me."

"Because you didn't run after Detroit. You called your ex-wife to make sure your daughter wouldn't see it on the news. You warned her." Amara gestured to the tablet. "I've been tracking you for weeks. You stopped three muggings, pulled a kid out of a burning car, and you didn't hurt anyone after Morrison. You're trying to control it. That makes you one of the good ones."

Marcus barked a bitter laugh. "Good ones. There's no such thing."

"Then prove me wrong." Amara lowered the tablet. "But I don't think you will. I think you were a good man before the seed, and you're trying to stay one after. That's more than most can say."

For a long moment, Marcus didn't speak. Outside, a train whistle echoed across the Pennsylvania hills. He thought about Lily's last text message, three weeks ago: *Dad are you okay? Mom won't tell me anything.*

He'd wanted to reply. Couldn't risk it.

"What do you want from me?" he finally asked.

"Help me find the others. The ones who aren't monsters or mercenaries. Build a network. Because when the truth comes out—when people find out the seeds were sent deliberately—the world is going to lose its mind. We need to be ready."

"Ready for what?"

Amara's expression went distant. "I don't know yet. But the seeds gave us powers for a reason. Enhanced us. Prepared us." She met his eyes. "What if we're not the endgame? What if we're just the opening move?"

Marcus felt something cold settle in his gut. The mark on his forearm pulsed once, faint and green-gold.

"Okay," he said quietly. "I'm listening."

Amara allowed herself a small smile. "Good. Because I've already found our third recruit. Former ER nurse, Miami. Cartel had her for two months before she escaped. She can manipulate biological matter."

"Healer?"

"And killer." Amara pulled up a file on her tablet. "Elena Vasquez. Left Diego Salazar dying of every disease known to man. Then disappeared with her family." She looked up. "I think we should find her before someone else does."

Marcus thought about Jake Morrison. About the power in his hands that could save or destroy. About his daughter growing up thinking her father was a monster.

About the fact that something out there had chosen him for this.

"All right," he said. "Let's go find a healer."

The steel mill groaned around them, rust flaking like dead skin. Two Seeded standing in the bones of American industry, planning to save a world that wanted them dead or registered or controlled.

It was a start.

## Chapter 23: "Rally"

Senator Jake Mercer—Special Coordinator for Enhanced Human Affairs, though he preferred just "Jake" when the cameras were rolling—stood backstage at the National Mall and felt half a million minds pressing against his consciousness like a living ocean.

They loved him. God, they *loved* him.

He could taste it in the air, that particular cocktail of adoration and hope and fear that only came from crowds this size. He'd learned to read it the way sommeliers read wine: the desperate edge of the unemployed, the righteous fury of the frightened, the zealotry of true believers. All of it flowing toward him, waiting to be shaped.

His aide, Jennifer Park—her mind smooth and compliant after six months of subtle reinforcement—touched his elbow. "Five minutes, sir. Sound check is green."

"Thank you, Jen." He gave her the smile. The one that made interns blush and senators hand over votes. She beamed back, her pupils dilating slightly. Pheromones were a hell of a drug, even before he'd been enhanced.

Mercer rolled up his sleeve, checking the spiral mark on his forearm. In the stage lights, it glowed faintly green-gold. Beautiful, really. A mark of divine selection, he'd called it in the *Times* interview. The public had eaten it up.

The truth was messier, of course. The seeds weren't divine—they were engineered by something with technology that made NASA look like cavemen banging rocks together. Dr. Tanaka's analysis had been clear on that. But "divine" played better than "alien experiment."

"Governor Reeves is in the front row," Jen said, scrolling through her tablet. "Also Secretary Valdez, three members of the Joint Chiefs, and—" She paused, frowning. "Sir, we're getting reports of a disturbance in Portland. Trevor Haines just—"

"Later." Mercer straightened his tie. Presidential blue, focus-grouped to perfection. "Portland's a problem for tomorrow. Today, we give them hope."

"But sir—"

He let just a whisper of persuasion curl through his voice. "Later, Jen."

Her frown smoothed out. "Of course. Sorry."

Mercer walked to the stage entrance, rolling his shoulders. Beyond the curtain, he could hear the warm-up speaker wrapping up: "—and now, the man who's given voice to all Seeded Americans, the man who's fought for unity in our darkest hour—"

Half a million people. The entire National Mall, from the Capitol steps to the Lincoln Memorial. Every major network carrying it live. Forty-three percent approval rating before Chicago, sixty-eight after his registration proposal. By tomorrow, he'd be at seventy-five.

"—Senator Jake Mercer!"

The roar hit him like a physical force. Mercer strode onto the stage, waving, smiling, letting the adoration wash over him. The morning sun caught his mark, making it gleam. Someone in the crowd screamed his name. A woman held up a sign: *MERCER 2028*.

He reached the podium and let the applause build. Ten seconds. Twenty. Thirty. At forty-five, he raised his hands for quiet. The silence that fell was instantaneous, profound. Half a million people holding their breath.

"Thank you," he said, voice carrying across the Mall through a hundred speakers. "Thank you for being here. For standing together. For refusing to let fear divide us."

He paused, letting his power seep outward—not control, nothing so crude. Just a gentle resonance, a psychic whisper that said *trust me, believe me, I am your champion*. The pheromones in his sweat did the rest, amplified by heat and proximity and the cameras broadcasting his face into a hundred million homes.

"One year ago, the sky broke open. We watched it together—that beautiful, terrible night. We didn't know what it meant. We didn't know what was coming." His voice dropped, intimate despite the massive crowd. "We didn't know we'd been chosen."

The word landed perfectly. *Chosen.* Not cursed, not infected. Chosen.

"Today, one hundred eighty-seven Americans carry the mark." He held up his arm. "We didn't ask for this gift. We didn't seek it out. But we received it. And now we face a choice: do we let fear control us, or do we embrace what we've become?"

Applause rippled through the crowd. He let it build, then cut it off with a gesture.

"Some people say the Seeded are dangerous. They point to Chicago. To Portland. To the incidents we all know about." His expression hardened. "And they're right. Some Seeded *are* dangerous. Trevor Haines has enslaved an American city. Dozens have died in powered conflicts. The threat is real."

He could feel the crowd's confusion, their uncertainty. Good.

"But I ask you this: were there no dangerous people before the seeds fell? Were there no murders, no tyrants, no cruelty?" His voice rose, passionate. "The seeds didn't create evil. They revealed it. They gave power to who we already were. And yes, some people were already monsters. But most—" He gestured to the crowd, sweeping. "—most were good. Most were decent. Most were *us*."

The applause came back, stronger. He rode it like a wave.

"I didn't choose this mark. But I choose what I do with it. I choose to serve. To protect. To build a future where the Seeded and the unseeded work together, where our gifts make us stronger, not divided." He leaned into the microphone. "I choose America."

The roar was deafening. Mercer let it wash over him, feeding on it. In the front row, Governor Reeves was on his feet, applauding. Secretary Valdez had tears in her eyes. The Joint Chiefs looked grave but approving.

And in forty-three million homes across America, people were nodding along, feeling in their hearts that this man—this honest, powerful, *chosen* man—was exactly what the country needed.

Mercer spoke for another twenty minutes. He outlined the Cooperation Act's provisions—voluntary registration, government funding for power control research, legal protections for compliant Seeded. He praised the heroes: the Seeded who'd saved lives, stopped disasters, used their gifts for good. He condemned the villains: Trevor Haines and his cult, the Chicago pyrokinetic, the unregistered who refused accountability.

He positioned himself perfectly in the middle: strong but compassionate, principled but practical. The reasonable voice in an unreasonable time.

When he finished, the standing ovation lasted four full minutes.

Backstage, Jen was waiting with his phone. "Sir, Governor Reeves wants to endorse you for—"

"Not yet." Mercer wiped sweat from his forehead. "Let him come to us. What about Portland?"

Jen's expression flickered—concern breaking through the haze of his influence. "Haines just mind-controlled an entire National Guard battalion. They turned around and went home. DOD is recommending a drone strike."

Mercer considered. Trevor Haines was a useful monster—proof that the Seeded needed oversight, that Mercer's measured approach was the only sane path. But if he was controlling military units now, he'd become too dangerous to leave operational.

"Tell DOD I support a limited strike. Minimize civilian casualties. And make sure my statement emphasizes that Haines is an outlier—not representative of the Seeded community."

"Yes, sir." Jen made notes. "Also, Dr. Tanaka called. He says the synthesis project is promising but needs more samples."

"Get him samples. Discreetly." Mercer accepted a bottle of water, drank deep. His mark pulsed faintly. "Anything else?"

"Marcus Cole and Amara Osei were spotted together in Pennsylvania. ORCHARD wants to move on them."

Mercer paused. Cole was dangerous—genuinely powerful, genuinely principled. Hard to control. But Osei was ex-intelligence, smart, connected. Together, they were the nucleus of a potential resistance.

"Tell ORCHARD to observe only. No engagement." He smiled slightly. "Let them build their little resistance. It'll make crushing them later more satisfying."

"And if they go after our black sites?"

"Then we'll have our villains." Mercer handed back the water bottle. "Every movement needs an enemy, Jen. Cole and Osei might just volunteer."

She nodded, already moving toward the phones. Mercer watched her go, feeling the shape of the future solidifying. In six months, he'd be indispensable. In a year, he'd be unstoppable. The President was already talking about creating a new Cabinet position—Secretary of Enhanced Affairs.

From there, it was a short road to the Oval Office.

He looked out at the dispersing crowd, half a million Americans who trusted him, believed in him, would follow him anywhere.

They had no idea they'd already begun.

The spiral mark on his arm burned warm and gold.

## Chapter 24: "War"

Trevor Haines stood at the floor-to-ceiling window of Portland's City Hall—*his* City Hall now—and felt the city's collective consciousness like a second skin. Eight hundred thousand minds, all humming in harmony with his will. It was beautiful. It was intoxicating.

It was starting to crack.

"Trevor."

He turned. Grace stood in the doorway, her empathic abilities making her glow in his psychic sight—a core of pure feeling in a sea of puppets. She was the only person in Portland whose mind he didn't control. The only one he didn't *want* to control.

Well. He wanted to. God, he wanted to. But he didn't. That had to count for something.

"I felt it," she said quietly. "Three more suicides this morning. The schoolteacher, the barista, and the man with the dog."

Trevor's jaw clenched. "They were weak."

"They were people whose minds you've been crushing for six months." Grace walked closer, her presence like a cool cloth on a fever. "You loosened your grip like I asked. Some of them woke up. And they couldn't live with what they'd done."

"They chose this." But the words felt hollow. Trevor remembered the schoolteacher—Marcus, he thought his name was—who'd beaten his wife under Trevor's influence. Broken three of her ribs and her nose. When the psychic pressure lifted, Marcus had hung himself from a shower rod.

The barista had drowned herself in the Willamette. The man with the dog had walked into traffic.

"They didn't choose anything," Grace said. "You chose for them."

Trevor felt the edges of his control fraying. The psychic lattice he'd built over Portland—invisible threads connecting every mind to his—was under strain. When he'd first taken the city, it had felt like conducting an orchestra. Now it felt like holding sand. The tighter he squeezed, the more slipped through.

"I can fix it," he muttered. "I just need to—"

The window exploded.

Trevor's mind screamed warning half a second before the glass shattered. He threw up a psychic barrier on instinct, freezing the shards in midair. Beyond the broken window, a helicopter hovered—military, bristling with weapons.

"This is the United States Army!" a voice boomed through loudspeakers. "Trevor Haines, you are under arrest for—"

Trevor reached out and crushed the pilot's mind like an eggshell.

The helicopter spun sideways, rotors screaming. It clipped the edge of the neighboring building and went down in a fireball. Trevor didn't watch it fall. He was already expanding his awareness, feeling for other minds, other threats.

There. Six blocks out: a battalion of National Guard troops, advancing in formation. Twelve hundred minds, all rigid with training and fear.

Trevor smiled. "Grace, get back."

"Trevor, don't—"

He unleashed his power like a tidal wave.

It swept outward from City Hall in concentric rings, invisible but devastating. The first National Guard soldiers went down screaming, clutching their heads. The second wave tried to fall back. The third wave turned and ran.

Trevor didn't let them run.

He dove into their minds—twelve hundred separate consciousnesses—and *rewrote* them. Not subtle whispers this time. Full psychic domination, brutal and total. He turned their fear into love, their orders into dreams, their purpose into *him*.

In forty-five seconds, it was over. Twelve hundred soldiers stood at attention, weapons lowered, eyes glazed and adoring.

"About face," Trevor whispered.

As one, they turned around.

"Go home. Forget why you came here. Remember only that you love your families and want to protect them."

The battalion marched away in perfect unison, humming the lullaby Trevor had planted in their heads. Behind them, the helicopter burned. Civilians were screaming. Grace was staring at him with something that looked like grief.

"I didn't kill them," Trevor said. His voice was shaking. "I could have. I didn't."

"You violated them." Grace's empathic gift let her feel everything—the soldiers' confusion, the civilians' terror, Trevor's own fractured psyche. "They'll go home to their families with holes in their memories. They'll wake up screaming from nightmares they can't explain. You've broken them, Trevor."

"I saved their lives!"

"You destroyed their minds to save their bodies." She stepped closer. "This is what I've been trying to tell you. Every time you do this, you leave scars. Psychic trauma that never heals. Those soldiers won't know why, but they'll spend the rest of their lives fighting depression, paranoia, dissociation. Some of them will kill themselves. And you'll tell yourself it was worth it."

Trevor felt something hot and shameful in his chest. "What was I supposed to do? Let them kill me? Kill the Awakened?"

"You could have run. Hidden. Asked for help." Grace's voice broke. "You could have done anything except what you're doing. Building an empire on enslaved minds. Calling yourself a prophet while you rape free will."

"I never—"

"You did." She gestured around them. "This whole city. Everyone in it. You've taken their consent, their autonomy, their *selves*. That's rape, Trevor. Psychic rape. And the worst part?" Tears streaked her face. "You know it is. I can feel your guilt. But you do it anyway."

Trevor's control wavered. For a moment—just a moment—the psychic lattice over Portland flickered. Eight hundred thousand minds gasped as his grip loosened, glimpsing freedom.

Then the panic hit. The horror. The memories of what they'd done under his control.

Trevor felt it like a punch to the gut: the collective realization, the mass trauma, the screaming existential crisis of a city waking up from a nightmare and finding it was real.

He couldn't let them have that. Couldn't let them feel that.

So he squeezed.

The psychic pressure came down like a hammer. Eight hundred thousand minds went silent again, docile, controlled. Safe from their own suffering. Trevor's mark blazed on his temple, the spiral glowing white-hot.

"I'm protecting them," he said hoarsely. "From themselves. From the government. From people who'd kill them for being different."

"You're protecting yourself," Grace said. "From having to face what you've become."

Before Trevor could respond, the air itself screamed. He looked up—drones, four of them, diving toward City Hall with missiles armed.

Trevor reached for the operators' minds and found them six hundred miles away in a Nevada bunker. Too far. He couldn't—

Grace grabbed his hand. Her empathic power amplified his, stretching his reach. Trevor felt the operators' minds snap into focus. He seized control, whispered a command.

The drones banked sharply, flying west. They crashed into the Pacific Ocean forty seconds later.

Trevor stared at Grace. "Why did you help me?"

"Because if you die, this city dies with you." She was still crying. "But don't mistake this for approval. I'm keeping you alive long enough to fix what you've broken. And if you won't—" Her voice hardened. "—then I'll find someone who can."

The city pulsed around them, eight hundred thousand minds singing Trevor's song. The helicopter burned in the street below. Somewhere, the twelve hundred soldiers marched home to families they'd forget the faces of in three days.

Trevor Haines, the Whisperer, the Prophet of the Awakened, looked at the only person whose mind he couldn't dominate and realized he was terrified of her.

Not because of what she could do.

Because of what she made him see.

"I can't let go," he whispered. "If I do, it all falls apart."

"It's already falling apart." Grace squeezed his hand once, then let go. "You're just too scared to watch."

She walked out, leaving him alone in a room full of broken glass and a city full of broken minds.

Trevor stood at the window and wondered when he'd become the monster.

The mark on his temple burned like a brand.

## Chapter 25: "Raid"

The ORCHARD black site in Nevada was a concrete bunker disguised as a water treatment facility, surrounded by twenty miles of desert and monitored by enough surveillance tech to make the NSA jealous. Marcus Cole crouched behind a maintenance shed half a klick out, watching the perimeter through binoculars, and thought: *This is a terrible idea.*

"Six guards visible," Amara Osei murmured beside him, tablet open with thermal imaging from a hacked satellite feed. "Another twelve inside. Power dampers in the detention wing—designed to disrupt seed bonding."

Marcus lowered the binoculars. "You're sure Elena can handle the guards non-lethally?"

"She stopped a man's heart and restarted it four times during the Miami extraction." Amara's expression was neutral. "She has control."

"Having control and using it are different things."

"Then let's hope she knows the difference." Amara glanced at her watch. "David and Zara are in position. We go in three minutes."

Marcus checked his mark—the spiral on his forearm glowing faintly in the darkness. He'd spent the last week learning to modulate his absorption: store kinetic energy, release it in controlled bursts. He could punch through a steel door now or redirect a bullet mid-flight. But the power dampers worried him. If they cut him off from his seed during the fight—

"Marcus." Elena Vasquez appeared from the shadows, moving silent despite the desert scrub. "You ready?"

He nodded. Elena had changed since Miami. Harder edges, darker circles under her eyes, a tension in her shoulders that never fully released. Whatever she'd done to Salazar had marked her in ways beyond the glowing spiral on her palm.

"Remember," Elena said, voice low. "We're here to free them, not start a war. Minimum necessary force."

"Says the woman who gave a cartel boss every disease known to man," Amara muttered.

Elena's expression went flat. "I'm aware of my capacity for violence, thanks. That's why I'm reminding myself."

Before the tension could escalate, a figure simply appeared among them—David Park, the phasing schoolteacher from Kansas, materializing out of solid air. "Perimeter is clear. Zara disabled the backup generators. When you cut the main power, we'll have ninety seconds before the dampers reboot."

"Ninety seconds." Marcus absorbed the information. "That's not a lot of time."

"Then we'd better move fast." David smiled slightly. "I can get us through the outer walls. After that, it's on you."

Amara tapped her tablet. "Countdown starts now. Good luck."

---

They moved.

David phased them through the perimeter fence first—a stomach-churning sensation like walking through ice water—then led them to the facility's north wall. Marcus could hear voices inside, the hum of electronics, the faint vibration of the power dampers that made his teeth ache.

"Ready?" David asked.

Elena and Marcus nodded. David placed his hands on the concrete, and his power rippled outward. The wall became translucent, ghost-like. "After you."

They stepped through solid concrete into a dimly lit corridor. Alarms immediately shrieked.

"So much for stealth," Marcus muttered.

Guards rounded the corner—four of them, automatic weapons raised. Marcus stepped forward, his body absorbing the kinetic energy of his own footsteps, the recoil of his heartbeat, the ambient vibrations of the building. By the time the first guard fired, Marcus was already moving.

He caught the bullets.

Not physically—his power absorbed the kinetic energy as they hit him, converting their momentum into stored force. The bullets clattered to the floor, harmless. Marcus punched the air in the guards' direction, releasing one percent of the stored energy.

The shockwave slammed into them like a battering ram. All four went down, unconscious but breathing.

"Detention wing is two floors down," Amara's voice crackled in Marcus's earpiece. "You've got seventy seconds before the dampers reboot."

They ran. David phased through doors ahead of them, unlocking them from the inside. Elena followed, her gift letting her feel every living body in the building—guards converging on their position, prisoners locked in cells below, something else deeper down that made her frown.

"There's someone in sub-level three," she said, breathing hard as they hit the stairs. "Heartbeat's wrong. Arrhythmic. Stressed."

"Prisoner or guard?"

## Chapter 26: "The Collective"

Trevor Haines stood in Pioneer Courthouse Square—Portland's living room, they'd called it before it became the beating heart of his empire—and felt the city breathe in unison with his will.

Eight hundred thousand minds. All singing his song.

He'd rebuilt after the cracks. After Grace's intervention. After the suicides and the helicopter and the drones. He'd reached deeper, woven his psychic lattice tighter, made the control absolute.

No more freedom. No more choice. No more suffering.

Portland was perfect now.

The crowds filled the square, faces upturned, waiting for him to speak. They wore the spiral mark—his mark—painted on their foreheads in gold. They chanted his name in perfect synchronization: "Trevor. Trevor. Trevor."

He could feel their love. Manufactured, yes. Psychically enforced. But wasn't all love just brain chemistry? Wasn't devotion just neural patterns? He'd simply chosen which patterns to amplify.

"My children," he said, and his voice carried through eight hundred thousand minds simultaneously. No loudspeakers needed. He was inside their heads. "Today, we make history."

"For too long, the unseeded have tried to control us. Register us. Imprison us. Turn us into weapons or lab rats." His power pulsed outward, reinforcing their anger, their fear. "Senator Mercer pretends to be our ally, but he's building cages with pretty names. ORCHARD hunts us like animals. The world looks at us and sees monsters."

"But we are not monsters. We are the *chosen*. The next step in human evolution. And I will not let them cage us. Control us. Destroy us." His voice rose, power crackling through the psychic lattice. "Today, Portland becomes the first Free City. Tomorrow, Seattle. Then San Francisco. Los Angeles. Every city on the West Coast will join the Collective."

"We will show them that the future belongs to the Seeded. That we are the inheritors of this world. And anyone who stands against us—" His eyes glowed, the spiral mark on his temple burning white. "—will kneel. Or burn."

He was invincible.

He'd noticed this morning—her psychic signature absent from the lattice, her apartment empty, a note on the kitchen table in her neat handwriting:

*I can't watch you do this anymore. I hope you find your way back. But I won't be there when you do. —G*

Trevor had crumpled the note, rage and grief warring in his chest. She'd been the only real thing. The only person who'd seen him and stayed.

Now she was gone.

He told himself it didn't matter. He had an entire city. Eight hundred thousand people who loved him, needed him, would die for him without question.

The National Guard tried one more time.

So he killed them.

Not with violence. With loyalty.

He reached into the minds of the battalion commanders and whispered: *Your enemy isn't Portland. It's the government that sent you. The politicians who don't care if you live or die. Your brothers and sisters in the Guard—they're your real family. Protect them.*

The commanders turned their guns on their superior officers.

The battalions fractured, confused orders contradicting Trevor's psychic directives. Some units turned back. Some kept advancing. Some opened fire on each other.

By noon, the National Guard had retreated, leaving forty-three dead and a hundred wounded in the chaos. Not one of Trevor's Collective had lifted a finger.

Translation: We can't stop him.

Trevor watched the broadcast from his penthouse—the mayor's residence, repurposed—and felt nothing. No triumph. No satisfaction. Just the vast, hollow ache of a power that couldn't be opposed.

He was twenty-two years old. He controlled a major American city. He could reshape minds with a thought.

And he had never felt more alone.

The message arrived three days later.

Not psychic—Trevor would have felt that. Physical. A courier walked into City Hall, mind blank and controlled, and handed him a tablet. On it, a video message.

"This is your warning," Marcus continued. "Free them. All of them. Walk away. Or we'll make you."

The video ended.

Trevor stared at the blank screen. For a long moment, he was silent.

Then he laughed.

It started low and built to something manic. They thought they could threaten him? They thought five Seeded could stand against his Collective?

"Bring them," he whispered to the empty room. "Bring your little band of heroes. Let them see what power really looks like."

The spiral on his temple pulsed, and eight hundred thousand minds pulsed with it.

War was coming to Portland.

Trevor couldn't wait.

## Chapter 27: "Alliance"

The safehouse was an old ranger station in the Gifford Pinchot National Forest, forty miles from Portland. Marcus Cole stood at the window, watching rain hammer the evergreens, and tried to calculate how many people were about to die because of the plan they'd just finalized.

Behind him, the team argued.

Zara was running on caffeine and spite. David had stress fractures from overusing his phasing. Dr. Tanaka was in hiding, analyzing seed data.

And Marcus himself? He hadn't slept more than three hours a night since Pennsylvania. Every time he closed his eyes, he saw Jake Morrison's body hitting the wall. Or Lily's face on a video call, asking when he was coming home.

"We're not ready for this," he said quietly.

Building infrastructure, synthesizing seeds, positioning himself for the presidency. Trevor's a wildfire. Mercer's a cancer."

"We might have to."

Amara spoke. "Marcus is right."

"Especially if he deserves it." Marcus's voice was hard. "Because the second we decide we get to kill people for being dangerous, we become Mercer. Or ORCHARD. Or Trevor." He touched his mark. "The seeds gave us power. They didn't give us the right to judge who lives and dies."

Four days to plan an assault on a psychic dictator in the middle of his own city, surrounded by an enslaved population. Marcus had run suicide missions in Kabul that looked safer.

Elena would handle casualties—both sides, no exceptions. Marcus would go head-to-head with Trevor, absorbing psychic energy and converting it to kinetic force.

But it was the plan they had.

"You don't think we can win," she said.

Elena nodded. They stood together in the rain, two damaged people with too much power, about to fight a war they couldn't win because it was the only war worth fighting.

Inside the cabin, Amara was coordinating. David was phasing supplies. Zara was doing push-ups, burning off nervous energy.

In four days, they'd march on Portland.

Marcus touched his mark and thought about Lily. If he died, she'd grow up thinking her father was a terrorist who attacked an American city.

But if he succeeded, maybe she'd grow up in a world where power didn't always corrupt. Where the strong protected the weak instead of enslaving them.

It was a long shot.

But it was the only shot that mattered.

## Chapter 28: "Siege of Portland"

They hit the city at 11:47 AM on a rain-soaked Sunday.

Marcus Cole rode in the back of a delivery van driven by a Seeded woman named Kira whose power was technopathy—she'd convinced every traffic camera and police scanner in Portland that they didn't exist. Beside him, Elena checked her med kit for the fourth time. David phased in and out of solid matter, nervous. Zara vibrated in place, burning energy.

Grace sat in the corner, eyes closed, preparing to face the man she'd loved.

"Five minutes to Pioneer Courthouse Square," Kira called from the front. "Trevor's already started. I can feel the psychic resonance through the city's network."

"Remember," Amara's voice crackled through earpieces. "We extract Trevor, not execute. Grace breaks the lattice. Get civilians clear. Minimize casualties."

"Easier said than done," Zara muttered.

The van stopped two blocks from the square. They piled out into an alley. Rain hammered down, washing the city clean. Or trying to. The psychic pollution was so thick Marcus could taste it—ozone and copper and something rotten underneath.

"Grace, you're up," Marcus said.

Grace opened her eyes. They were wet, but not from rain. "If I die—"

"You won't." Elena squeezed her shoulder. "I'll keep you alive. That's a promise."

They moved through the back streets, avoiding the main crowds flowing toward the square. Kira's technopathy kept them invisible to surveillance. David phased them through locked doors. They emerged on a rooftop overlooking Pioneer Courthouse Square.

Below, the crowd was a sea of humanity. Hundreds of thousands, all wearing gold spirals painted on their foreheads, all swaying in perfect synchronization. At the center, elevated on a platform, stood Trevor Haines.

He looked terrible. Gaunt, pale, the spiral on his temple glowing so bright it hurt to look at. When he spoke, his voice resonated through every mind in the city simultaneously.

"Today we are one," Trevor said. "One will. One purpose. One future. The unseeded tried to stop us. They failed. Mercer tries to control us. He will fail. We are inevitable. We are—"

Grace stepped to the edge of the roof and screamed.

It wasn't a physical sound. It was psychic—pure emotion weaponized, her empathic gift turned inside out. She broadcast every feeling Trevor had ever made her feel: grief, horror, betrayal, love twisted into chains.

The lattice shuddered.

Eight hundred thousand people gasped as Trevor's control flickered. For half a second, they were themselves again.

Then Trevor's head snapped up. His eyes found Grace across the city. Recognition. Fury. Heartbreak.

"You," he breathed, and the word echoed through Portland like thunder.

He reached for her mind—the one mind he'd never been able to dominate—and found her ready. Grace had spent six months learning his psychic signature, every nuance and frequency. She knew how he thought, how he attacked, how he controlled.

She met him in the space between minds and fought.

The crowd below convulsed. Half followed Trevor's commands. Half broke free, confusion and trauma flooding back. The psychic lattice was tearing.

"Now!" Marcus shouted.

Zara became a blur, racing into the square at inhuman speed. She grabbed civilians at the edge of the crowd—the ones waking up, screaming, about to be trampled—and pulled them to safety. Twenty people in fifteen seconds.

David phased Marcus and Elena into the square itself, materializing twenty feet from Trevor's platform. Guards—mind-controlled civilians with weapons—turned toward them.

"Non-lethal!" Elena commanded. She swept her hand out, feeling for nerve clusters, and a dozen guards dropped, unconscious but unharmed.

Marcus ran for the platform. Trevor saw him coming and lashed out psychically. The attack hit Marcus's mind like a sledgehammer—*kneel obey worship me love me fear me—*

Marcus absorbed it.

His power didn't distinguish between physical and psychic energy. Force was force. He took Trevor's psychic assault, converted it to kinetic potential, and punched the platform.

The shockwave shattered concrete. Trevor stumbled, his concentration breaking. The lattice tore further. More people woke up.

And started screaming.

Eight hundred thousand minds, free for the first time in months, remembering what they'd done. What had been done to them. The collective trauma was a tidal wave.

Grace felt it and nearly collapsed. Elena caught her, poured healing energy into her overtaxed nervous system. "Stay with me!"

Trevor recovered, rage and desperation twisting his face. "You don't understand! They need me! Without me, they're—"

"Elena!" Marcus roared.

Elena was already moving, her gift spreading outward, feeling for injuries. Broken bones, internal bleeding, concussions. She healed what she could, as fast as she could, but there were too many.

Zara blurred through the chaos, pulling the injured to safety. David phased between collapsing buildings, rescuing people trapped by debris.

And Grace—Grace was losing. Trevor's power was too strong, his desperation too complete. He was burning out his seed, pushing past safe limits, and he didn't care. If he couldn't have Portland, he'd drag everyone down with him.

Marcus made a choice.

He sprinted at Trevor, absorbing every step's kinetic energy, building charge. Trevor saw him coming and threw everything he had—psychic domination backed by six months of absolute power.

*KNEEL.*

Marcus knelt. He couldn't help it. The command was overwhelming.

Then he released all his stored energy through his legs, straight into the ground.

The resulting shockwave cratered the platform and launched Marcus upward. He caught Trevor in midair, both of them flying fifteen feet up, and wrapped his arms around the telepath's shoulders.

"Let them go," Marcus growled.

"I can't." Trevor's voice broke. "If I let go, they'll tear themselves apart. The trauma—"

"Grace will catch them. She'll ease the transition. But you have to let go."

"I can't!" Tears streamed down Trevor's face. "I'm all they have!"

Marcus looked into the eyes of a twenty-two-year-old who'd been given godlike power and no idea what to do with it. Who'd built an empire to hide from his own loneliness. Who'd enslaved hundreds of thousands because being loved—even falsely—was better than being invisible.

"You were never alone," Marcus said quietly. "You just never learned to see the people who actually cared."

They hit the ground. Marcus absorbed the impact. Trevor crumpled, psychically exhausted.

And the lattice broke.

---

Eight hundred thousand minds, suddenly free.

Grace was ready. She'd positioned herself at the psychic epicenter, and when the lattice shattered, she caught the pieces. Not controlling—cushioning. Broadcasting calm, acceptance, forgiveness. Giving them a soft place to land instead of the hard edge of trauma.

It wasn't perfect. Dozens collapsed, catatonic. Hundreds more screamed. But thousands found their feet, looked around, and saw each other—really saw each other—for the first time in months.

The silence that followed was profound.

Trevor lay on the broken platform, his mark dim, his power spent. He looked like a child. "What have I done?"

"What you thought you had to," Elena said, kneeling beside him. "Now you live with it."

Sirens wailed in the distance. ORCHARD teams, probably. Or Mercer's forces. The battle was over, but the consequences were just beginning.

"Trial, probably. Prison." Marcus's voice was tired. "Or Mercer disappears him into a black site. Either way, he's finished."

"He was just a kid," Grace whispered. "A scared, lonely kid who got too much power too fast."

"We all are," Elena said quietly, glowing mark on her palm pulsing. "The only difference is what we do with it."

They extracted before ORCHARD arrived, taking Trevor with them—unconscious, bound, no longer a god. Just a broken twenty-two-year-old who'd enslaved a city because he'd never learned to ask for what he needed.

Portland woke up slowly, painfully. Therapy centers were overwhelmed. Suicide hotlines lit up. But people were free.

That had to count for something.

## Chapter 29: "Aftermath"

Amara was quiet for a long moment. Then: "We get close to him. Someone he trusts. Someone he won't see coming." She met Marcus's eyes. "Someone like me."

"You?" Elena's voice rose. "Amara, he'll control you. His power—"

"Works in person. I know." Amara's expression was calm. "But his weakness is also in person. He needs proximity. If I can get close enough to plant evidence, hack his systems, record him—we'll have what we need."

"This is suicide," David said flatly.

"It's necessary." Amara stood. "I've already made contact with his people. Told them I want to come in from the cold. Provide intel on Marcus's team in exchange for amnesty."

Marcus felt cold. "When?"

"Three days ago. Right after Portland." Amara's voice was steady. "They believe me. I've given them low-level intelligence—locations you've already abandoned, Seeded you've already extracted. Enough to prove I'm turning. Mercer's people are arranging a meeting."

"You should have told us," Marcus growled.

"If I had, you'd have tried to stop me." Amara met his anger without flinching. "This is the play, Marcus. The only one that matters. We can rescue Seeded and blow up black sites forever. But if Mercer consolidates power, none of it matters. He becomes the architect of a new world order—Seeded at the top, everyone else underneath. We stop him now, or we fight his regime for generations."

The room was silent. Marcus wanted to argue. Wanted to forbid it. But Amara was right. Mercer was the cancer. Everything else was symptoms.

"What do you need from us?" Marcus finally asked.

"Time. Two weeks, maybe three. I get close, gather evidence, get out." Amara's voice softened slightly. "If I don't contact you by day twenty-one, assume I'm compromised. Don't come for me."

"Fuck that," Zara snapped. "We don't leave people behind."

"You do if rescuing them destroys the mission." Amara was already moving toward the door. "I'm not a soldier, Zara. I'm a spy. This is what spies do. We walk into the fire so others don't have to."

Elena stood. "At least let me give you a medical workup. If Mercer's using pheromones to control people, maybe I can—"

"You can't." Amara smiled sadly. "His power isn't biological. It's psycho-chemical. Supernatural. The seed doesn't care about my immune system." She paused at the door. "But thank you."

Marcus walked over, offered his hand. Amara took it. "You come back," he said. "That's an order."

"I'm not one of your rangers, Marcus."

"You are now."

They held the grip for a moment. Then Amara left, disappearing into the forest. Heading toward Mercer. Toward almost-certain domination or death.

The remaining team stood in silence.

"Three weeks," David finally said. "What do we do until then?"

Marcus thought about it. They had evidence of ORCHARD's crimes flooding the internet. They had forty-three freed Seeded who needed protection. They had a public that was terrified and angry and looking for someone to blame.

And in three weeks, they'd either have the evidence to destroy Mercer or they'd be burying Amara.

"We prepare," Marcus said. "We find more allies. We get ready for whatever comes next. Because when Amara pulls this off—and she will—Mercer's going to come at us with everything he has."

"And if she doesn't pull it off?" Zara asked.

Marcus's mark pulsed. "Then we burn his empire down anyway. Just louder."

That night, Marcus finally called Lily.

His ex-wife picked up, suspicious. "Marcus."

"I know. I'm sorry. Is she—?"

"Hold on."

Rustling. Then Lily's voice, small and hopeful: "Dad?"

Marcus's throat closed. "Hey, kiddo."

"Are you okay? Mom won't tell me anything. I saw you on the news—they said you fought a bad man in Portland."

"I did."

"Did you win?"

Marcus looked at his mark. Thought about Jake Morrison. Trevor Haines. The forty-seven dead in Portland. The eight hundred thousand saved.

"I don't know yet," he said honestly. "But I'm trying."

Lily was quiet for a moment. Then: "I drew you a picture. Want to see?"

"More than anything."

She held it up to the camera. A stick-figure family: mom, dad, daughter. The dad had a glowing spiral on his arm. Lily had labeled it: *My family. Dad is special.*

"I think your mark is pretty," she said. "Like you're magic."

Marcus pressed his hand to the screen. "I'm not magic, Lil. I'm just trying to do the right thing."

"That's what makes you magic, Dad."

They talked for ten more minutes before his ex-wife took the phone back, voice tight with emotion. "She needs you to come home, Marcus."

"I know." He did. God, he did. "But if I come home now, they'll use her to get to me. I have to finish this first."

"Finish what?"

"Making a world where she can be proud her dad has a glowing mark."

His ex-wife was quiet. Then: "Okay. But Marcus? Don't die. She couldn't take it."

"I won't," he lied.

He hung up and sat in the dark, Lily's drawing still on the screen. A family. Dad is special.

If he died fighting Mercer, she'd grow up without him. If he ran, she'd grow up in a world where power always corrupted and the strong always won.

There was no good choice.

So he chose the hard one.

## Chapter 30: "First Contact"

One year, two weeks, and three days after the meteor shower, the world had learned to live with the Seeded.

Registration had become normalized in most countries. Mercer's Unity Corps patrolled major cities, a mix of government authority and superpowered enforcement. ORCHARD's black sites had been exposed but not entirely dismantled—some just rebranded, moved, given prettier names.

Trevor Haines awaited trial in a federal supermax, his power suppressed by experimental dampers. Grace visited him weekly. He didn't deserve it. She came anyway.

Elena Vasquez ran a free clinic in New Mexico, healing the poor and trying to forget the feel of Salazar's organs rupturing under her touch. Most nights, she succeeded.

David Park taught high school physics in Kansas again. His students knew about his gift. Most thought it was cool. He phased through walls during demonstrations and tried not to think about Portland.

Zara Hassan ran interference for Marcus's underground railroad, moving unregistered Seeded to safety. She was the fastest person alive and still felt like she was always a step behind.

Dr. Tanaka had disappeared into the research community, sharing everything he'd learned about seed biology. Papers published anonymously. The scientific world buzzing with theories.

And Marcus Cole sat in a safehouse outside Ann Arbor, watching his daughter sleep through a window he couldn't enter because federal warrants and Mercer's hunters and the simple fact that getting close to her painted a target on her back.

Amara had been dark for seventeen days.

Four more days and they'd have to assume she was compromised. Marcus was already planning the extraction—stupid, suicidal, necessary. You didn't leave people behind. Even when the smart play was to cut losses and run.

His phone buzzed. Unknown number.

Marcus answered. "Yeah."

"It's me." Amara's voice, hushed and urgent. "I'm in. Close to Mercer. Closer than I thought possible. He trusts me."

Relief flooded through him. "Evidence?"

"So much evidence. Recordings of him using his power to manipulate Congress. Financial ties to ORCHARD's synthetic seed program. Plans to position himself for the presidency with a cabinet full of dominated officials." Her voice dropped. "Marcus, it's worse than we thought. He's not building a Seeded regime. He's building a dictatorship with himself as the only real Seeded in power. Everyone else is a tool."

"Can you get out?"

"Not yet. There's something bigger. He's nervous about something. Checking the sky. Having Dr. Tanaka's research pulled and classified. He knows something we don't." Static crackled. "I'll get what I can and extract in seventy-two hours. Be ready."

"Amara—"

The line went dead.

Marcus sat in the dark, his mark pulsing. Seventy-two hours. Then they'd have everything they needed to burn Mercer's empire down.

He should have felt triumph. Instead, he felt a cold certainty that something was wrong.

Why was Mercer looking at the sky?

---

Somewhere in a bunker beneath the New Mexico desert, Senator Jake Mercer stood in front of a classified feed from the James Webb Space Telescope and felt genuine fear for the first time since his seed had bonded.

The object had appeared six hours ago. Massive. Organic. Moving at point-two-seven lightspeed toward Earth.

ETA: forty-eight hours.

NASA was panicking quietly. The military was mobilizing uselessly. The President—whom Mercer had been carefully positioning for removal—was demanding answers Mercer didn't have.

Behind him, Dr. Tanaka's voice was shaking. "It's not a ship. It's... I don't know what it is. The structure is biological. Semi-crystalline. Radiating energy in frequencies that match the seeds' spectral signature."

"They're coming back," Mercer said quietly. "Whoever sent the seeds. They're coming to check on their experiment."

"What do we do?"

Mercer thought about his Unity Corps. His synthetic seed program. The empire he'd built in twelve months. All of it meaningless if the beings who'd created the seeds decided humanity had failed.

"We prepare," he said. "And we pray they like what they see."

---

In Portland, Grace woke from a nightmare she couldn't remember and felt every Seeded on Earth simultaneously gasp. Something was pulling at the seeds. Waking them up. Making them *ready*.

She grabbed her phone and called Marcus. "Something's happening. The seeds are activating. All of them. I can feel—"

The connection dropped.

---

Elena stepped out of her clinic to find the night sky wrong. Stars were disappearing. Not setting—being blocked. She looked up and saw a shadow crossing the moon. Not an eclipse. Something in front of it.

Something enormous.

Her mark blazed white-hot. She gasped, dropped to her knees. The pain was exquisite—her seed rewriting itself, upgrading, preparing for something.

Every Seeded on Earth felt it. Powers that had plateaued suddenly spiked. Dormant abilities came online. The marks burned.

---

Trevor Haines sat in his cell and felt the dampers fail as his seed roared to life. He could feel minds again—millions of them, all screaming the same question: *What's happening?*

And beyond them, vast and alien and utterly other: billions of minds that had never been human. That had never been anything like human.

Coming down from the stars.

---

Marcus was halfway to his car when the mark on his arm erupted in light. He stumbled, power flooding his body. Not just kinetic absorption now. He could feel force itself—gravity, momentum, the rotation of the Earth. Everything was energy. Everything was his.

The sky lit up.

Marcus looked up and saw it: a shape descending through the atmosphere, wreathed in fire and light. It stretched from horizon to horizon—organic, wrong, beautiful. Like a jellyfish made of stars and flesh and geometries that hurt to perceive.

His phone rang. Lily's number.

"Dad!" Her voice was terrified. "Dad, what's in the sky? What's happening?"

"I don't know, baby." Marcus couldn't look away from the descending shape. "But I'm going to find out. I'm going to keep you safe."

"Promise?"

The thing in the sky opened. Thousands of smaller shapes—pods, ships, *something*—began to separate from the main body. Falling toward Earth like a second meteor shower.

Marcus touched his mark. Felt the power burning in his veins. Felt every other Seeded on the planet feeling the same thing: the seeds fully activating, giving them everything they'd been designed for.

Weapons. They were weapons. Engineered by something ancient and vast to fight something worse.

"I promise," Marcus said.

The first pod hit the ocean off the coast of California. Then another in the Sahara. Another in Beijing. They were landing everywhere.

And the sky was full of things that had been traveling for centuries, following the seeds, drawn by the same signal that had woken every Seeded on Earth.

Amara's voice crackled through his earpiece, breaking up but audible: "Marcus. They're here. The Swarm. The invasion. The seeds were just the opening move. We're the—"

Static.

Marcus looked up at the sky full of descending horrors and the marks blazing on his skin and thought: *We're the defense.*

The seeds had never been about power. They'd been about preparation.

Earth had one year to get ready for a war it didn't know was coming.

Now the war was here.

Marcus Cole started the car, drove toward his daughter, and felt the future collapsing into a single burning point:

Survive.

Behind him, the sky burned. The Swarm descended. Every Seeded on Earth felt their power surge to levels that would have killed them twelve months ago.

Ready or not.

Chosen or not.

The war for humanity's survival had begun.

And in the space between worlds, vast and patient and utterly alien, The Wardens watched their inoculation take effect and hoped—as they'd hoped for thousands of doomed worlds—that this one might be different.

That this one might survive.

The spiral marks blazed like stars against human skin.

And the Swarm, ancient and endless and hungry, came down from the dark between galaxies to feed.

# CRUEL OBSESSION

### The Obsession Duet, Book 1

---

## Prologue

### ALEKSANDR

*Three years ago. Moscow.*

The last time I watched a man beg, I felt nothing.

He was on his knees in a warehouse on the outskirts of Krasnogorsk, blood pooling beneath the fluorescent lights, and all I could think about was the photograph in my coat pocket. Not of him. Not of the debt he owed. Of *her*.

A woman I'd never met. A woman who didn't know I existed.

The photograph was taken outside a gallery in Lisbon — a candid shot, slightly blurred, like the photographer had been walking when something stopped him. She was standing in a doorway with the afternoon light hitting her dark hair, one hand raised to push it from her face, mouth half-open as though she'd just been laughing. She wore a white blouse with paint smeared on the cuff.

I'd been given the photograph as part of a dossier. Her father's dossier. Ricardo Marques — a man who owed my family more than money. A man whose debts had roots in blood.

But I didn't look at the father's section of the file. I kept coming back to the daughter.

Sofia Marques. Twenty-five years old. Art history degree from the Universidade de Lisboa. Junior curator at the Galeria Saudade in Chiado. No criminal record. No significant debts of her own. Clean. Bright. Building something from the rubble her father left behind.

I should have closed the file.

Instead, I bought the gallery.

The man on his knees was still begging. I tucked the photograph back into my pocket and nodded to Marco.

"Enough," I said.

Not because I had mercy. Because I had somewhere to be.

I had a flight to Lisbon.

## Chapter 1

### SOFIA

The morning light in Lisbon is a liar.

It pours through the gallery windows like honey, making everything look warm, gilded, *safe* — as though the world outside isn't a machine designed to grind you into dust. I stand in the main exhibition hall of Galeria Saudade and let the light wash over Ana Beatriz Fonseca's paintings, and for one perfect, suspended moment, I believe the lie.

The exhibition opens tonight. Twelve pieces by an emerging Portuguese artist whose work makes me feel like someone has cracked open my ribs and poured color directly into my chest. Abstract expressionism with a Lusophone soul — sweeping gestures of cobalt and burnt sienna, textures that make you want to press your palms flat against the canvas.

I've spent three months curating this show. Three months of negotiations, late nights, grant applications, and arguments with my director about whether "emerging" meant "unprofitable." Tonight is the opening. Tonight, if everything goes right, we'll sell enough pieces to keep the gallery funded through the autumn.

If everything goes right.

My phone buzzes. I glance down.

**Helena Dias (Director):** *Come to my office. Now.*

No "good morning." No pleasantries. Helena's messages are always clipped, but this one feels like a door slamming.

I take one last look at Ana's centerpiece — a massive canvas called *Saudade em Azul*, all oceanic blues and fractured gold leaf — and head for the stairs.

Helena's office is on the second floor, overlooking the Rua Garrett. It's a beautiful space that she's managed to make feel like a dentist's waiting room — beige walls, a desk so clean it looks sterilized, and a single orchid that I'm convinced is artificial.

She doesn't look up when I enter.

"Close the door."

I close it. Sit in the chair across from her. Wait.

Helena Dias is fifty-three, whip-thin, with silver hair cropped close to her skull and the kind of face that looks like it was designed for delivering bad news. She's been the director of Galeria Saudade for eleven years. She gave me my first job when I was a twenty-two-year-old with a fresh degree, no connections, and a surname that made people in the art world wrinkle their noses.

She's the closest thing I have to a mentor.

Which is why the expression on her face makes my stomach drop.

"The gallery is being sold," she says.

The words land like stones.

"What?"

"The holding company that owns the building — Vortex Capital — has decided to liquidate the property. We have sixty days to vacate."

I stare at her. The morning light is still streaming through the window, still doing its golden, lying thing, and I want to reach out and snap the blinds shut.

"Sixty days," I repeat. "Helena, the Fonseca exhibition opens *tonight*."

"I'm aware."

"We have bookings through October. The Mendes retrospective, the photography collective, the —"

"Sofia." Her voice is a scalpel. Clean. Final. "I've spent the last forty-eight hours on the phone with lawyers. The decision is made. Vortex Capital owns the building, the name, and the collection license. When they sell, we cease to exist."

I feel the chair beneath me, the hard edge of the armrest under my fingers. I feel my lungs pulling air. These are facts. The rest of this doesn't feel like facts.

"Who's behind Vortex Capital?"

Helena's mouth tightens. "That's the question, isn't it? It's a shell company registered in Luxembourg. The beneficial owner is hidden behind three layers of corporate structure. My lawyer has been trying to trace it for months."

"Months?" I lean forward. "You've known about this for *months*?"

Something shifts in her expression. Not guilt, exactly. Helena doesn't do guilt. But something close.

"I've known it was a possibility. The holding company has been making... adjustments. Reducing our operating budget. Blocking grant applications. It's been a slow suffocation, Sofia. I didn't want to alarm you until I was certain."

A slow suffocation. I think about the rejected grant proposals, the maintenance requests that went unanswered, the heating system that broke in January and took six weeks to fix. I thought it was bureaucracy. Incompetence. The usual grind of working in a Portuguese institution that runs on fumes and stubbornness.

It wasn't incompetence. It was deliberate.

Someone has been killing this gallery. Slowly. Methodically. And now they're moving in for the final stroke.

"What do we do?" I ask.

Helena looks at me for a long time. Then she opens her desk drawer and pulls out a business card. Slides it across the desk.

I pick it up. Heavy card stock, cream-colored, with embossed lettering.

**ALEKSANDR VOLKOV**
*Volkov Industries*

A Lisbon phone number. Nothing else. No title, no email, no address. Just a name and a number, like a dare.

"Who is he?"

"A man with more money than God and fewer scruples than the devil." Helena folds her hands. "He's also the only person I know who has the resources and the connections to challenge Vortex Capital's sale. Or to buy the gallery himself and keep it running."

"Why would he do that?"

"That," Helena says, "is what you're going to find out. He's requested a meeting. With you. Specifically."

The name on the card seems to pulse under my fingers.

"With me? Why not with you? You're the director."

"I asked the same question. His people said he's interested in the curatorial vision of the gallery, and that he wants to discuss it with the person responsible." She pauses. "That's you, Sofia."

I look at the card again. Aleksandr Volkov. I've heard the name — everyone in Lisbon has, if they pay attention to real estate, finance, or the society pages of *Público*. Russian-Portuguese. Obscenely wealthy. Bought half of the Parque das Nações waterfront last year. There are rumors about his past that range from "old money" to "blood money," depending on who's telling the story.

"When?" I ask.

"Tonight. After the opening."

Of course. Because today wasn't already going to be the longest day of my life.

I stand. I put the card in my pocket. I look at Helena, and I see something in her eyes that I've never seen before.

Fear.

"Be careful with him," she says. "He's not like the men you're used to dealing with."

I want to ask what that means. But my phone is buzzing again — the caterers, the lighting technician, the journalist from *Time Out Lisboa* who wants a preview — and the day is already pulling me under.

I close Helena's door behind me. I press my back against the wall of the corridor and close my eyes and breathe.

Sixty days.

One name on a card.

And a feeling in my chest like standing on the edge of something very high, looking down.

## Chapter 2

### ALEKSANDR

I watch her through the gallery window for eleven minutes before I go inside.

She's wearing a black dress — simple, fitted, ending just above her knee — and her dark hair is pulled up, exposing the line of her neck. She moves through the crowd like water, pausing to speak with a collector here, redirecting a journalist there, one hand always gesturing, always in motion.

She's nervous. I can see it in the way she touches the base of her throat when she thinks no one is looking. In the way she holds her champagne glass without drinking from it.

She's also magnificent.

Three years of watching from a distance — through reports, photographs, financial statements, the careful architecture of ownership and control — and nothing prepared me for the reality of her. The photograph in my pocket was a shadow. This woman is the thing that cast it.

Marco stands beside me on the cobblestones of Rua Garrett, patient as a wall.

"The security team is in position," he says. "Two inside, one on the street. The car is around the corner on Rua do Alecrim."

"Fine."

"You could still cancel. Send a representative."

I turn to look at him. Marco Vieira has worked for me for seven years. He is the only person alive who speaks to me like this — with the careful, measured tone of a man who knows exactly how far he can push and pushes anyway.

"No," I say.

He nods. He knew the answer before he asked.

I straighten my cuffs. I don't need to — my suit is impeccable, charcoal Brioni, tailored in Milan — but the gesture settles something in me. A preparation. An alignment.

I've built an empire on control. On the understanding that every interaction is a negotiation, every room a chessboard, every person a piece to be positioned. I've never walked into a room without knowing exactly what I wanted and exactly how to take it.

Tonight, for the first time, I'm not entirely sure what will happen.

Because Sofia Marques is not a chess piece. She's the board on fire.

I cross the street and enter the gallery.

The exhibition is impressive. I say this not as a compliment but as an assessment — I've been inside the world's finest galleries, from the Tretyakov to the Uffizi, and I know the difference between curation and decoration. Sofia has created something with intention. The paintings are arranged not chronologically or by size but by emotional arc — you enter through chaos, move through fragmentation, and arrive at something that feels, against all odds, like hope.

She did this. This woman who grew up in a two-bedroom flat in Amadora, whose father gambled away everything, whose mother worked double shifts at a hospital until her heart gave out. She took the wreckage of her life and built something beautiful.

It makes what I'm about to do more complicated.

Not complicated enough to stop me.

I take a glass of champagne from a passing tray and position myself near the centerpiece — the large blue canvas. It's the strongest piece in the collection. Sofia will come back to it eventually. Everyone orbits the thing they love.

It takes four minutes.

She's mid-conversation with a man in a linen jacket — a collector, judging by the possessive way he looks at the paintings — when her gaze drifts to the centerpiece and catches on me.

She goes still.

I don't smile. I don't look away. I hold her gaze across the room and let the silence between us thicken into something tangible.

She excuses herself from the collector. Crosses the room. Each step measured, deliberate, her chin lifted like a woman walking into a headwind.

Good. I didn't want someone who'd be easy.

"Senhor Volkov?"

Her voice is lower than I expected. Warm. With a slight rasp at the edges, like smoke.

"Miss Marques."

"You're early. Helena said you wanted to meet after the opening."

"I wanted to see the exhibition. I prefer to understand what I'm discussing before I discuss it."

Her eyes narrow slightly. They're dark — nearly black in this light — and sharp. Assessing. She's reading me the way she reads a painting, looking for composition, intent, the thing beneath the surface.

"And? What do you think?"

I glance at the canvas beside us. *Saudade em Azul.* Up close, the texture is extraordinary — layers of paint built up and scraped back, revealing glimpses of color beneath, like geological strata. Like the painting has a history it's trying to hide.

"I think you've arranged a conversation about loss," I say. "Not mourning — something more active. The way we carry the things we've lost, build around them, build *with* them." I pause. "The title helps. Saudade is a useful word."

Something changes in her face. Not softening, exactly. But the defensive architecture shifts, opens a crack.

"Most people just say they like the colors."

"I'm not most people."

"No," she says quietly. "I don't imagine you are."

A beat of silence. The gallery hums around us — voices, laughter, the clink of glasses — but it feels distant. Irrelevant.

"You know why I'm here," I say.

"You want to talk about the gallery."

"I want to talk about saving it."

Her jaw tightens. "Helena told me about Vortex Capital. About the sale."

"What else did she tell you?"

"That you might be the only person who can stop it. And that I should be careful."

A smile, thin and controlled. "Helena is a wise woman."

"She also said you asked for me specifically. Why?"

Because I've been watching you for three years. Because I bought this gallery to get closer to you. Because your father destroyed something that belonged to my family, and I planned to use you as leverage, and then I saw your face in a photograph and the plan changed into something I don't fully understand.

"Because you're the one who makes this gallery worth saving," I say. "The building is real estate. The art is inventory. *You* are the vision. Without you, there's nothing to invest in."

She searches my face. I let her look. I've spent years perfecting the art of showing people exactly what I want them to see, but with her, I find myself wanting to hold still. To let her look past the surface.

Dangerous.

"Let's talk after the opening," she says. "There's a bar on the Rua da Rosa — Pensão Amor. Do you know it?"

"I know it."

"Meet me at eleven."

She turns and walks back into the crowd without waiting for my answer. Without looking back.

I watch her go. I raise the champagne to my lips and drink for the first time tonight.

She chose the venue. She set the time. She walked away first.

She thinks she's in control.

I let her think that. For now.

## Chapter 3

### SOFIA

Pensão Amor is exactly the kind of place you bring a man you want to unsettle.

A former brothel in Cais do Sodré, converted into a bar that still wears its history like perfume — vintage erotic art on the walls, velvet booths, a library stuffed with erotica, and a burlesque stage where, on weekends, performers in feathered corsets make the tourists blush. It's theatrical and unapologetic, and I chose it because I needed to meet Aleksandr Volkov somewhere that wasn't his territory.

I need to remind myself that I'm not afraid of powerful men.

Even if my hands haven't stopped shaking since he looked at me across the gallery.

I arrive first. Take a booth in the back, near the library, where the light is low and the velvet is the color of old wine. I order a gin tónico — Lisbon style, with a massive glass and too much botanicals — and press my palms flat against the table.

*Think, Sofia. What do you know?*

I know Aleksandr Volkov is thirty-four years old. Born in Moscow, mother Portuguese, father Russian — Grigory Volkov, a name that appears in international finance articles the way sharks appear in ocean footage. Briefly. Ominously. Grigory died eight years ago. Causes vary depending on the source: heart attack, according to the official record. Something uglier, according to the whispers.

Aleksandr inherited Volkov Industries at twenty-six and tripled its value in five years. Real estate, energy, shipping. He moved the company's European headquarters to Lisbon three years ago. Since then, he's been acquiring property, influence, and a reputation for being absolutely ruthless in negotiation and absolutely impenetrable in private.

No public relationships. No scandals. No social media presence. The man is a ghost who happens to own buildings.

And tonight, in the gallery, he looked at a painting and understood it better than most critics I've worked with.

That's the part that frightens me most.

The door opens. He walks in. And the room — the whole room, not just my corner of it — seems to recalibrate around him.

He's tall. I noticed that at the gallery, but here, in a space designed for intimacy, it's more pronounced. He moves with an economy that borders on stillness, like a man who learned long ago that the most powerful thing you can do in a room is take up less space than you're entitled to.

He sees me. Crosses the room. Sits across from me in the booth without asking permission.

"You've been researching me," he says.

It's not a question. I don't insult him by denying it.

"You've been researching me too," I say. "You knew about the exhibition. You knew about my curatorial approach. You asked for me by name."

"I did."

"So let's skip the performance. What do you want?"

He leans back. Studies me. In the amber light, his eyes are the color of Baltic sea glass — pale, cold, with something moving beneath the surface.

"I want to buy the gallery," he says. "Outright. From Vortex Capital. I have the resources, the lawyers, and the leverage to force the sale."

"And what do you want in return?"

"In return for saving your gallery, your artists, and your career?"

"Nothing is free, Senhor Volkov. Especially not from men who can afford to give it away."

A flash of something in those eyes. Amusement? Respect?

"Aleksandr," he says. "If we're going to do this, use my name."

"We're not doing anything yet. Answer the question."

He reaches into his jacket. Pulls out a slim leather folder. Places it on the table between us. I don't touch it.

"Inside is a proposal," he says. "I buy the gallery. I fund its operations for five years — full budget, no restrictions. You become the sole creative director. Complete artistic control. No board, no committee, no Helena looking over your shoulder."

My heart is hammering. Complete artistic control. Five years of funding. It's everything I've ever wanted, handed to me in a leather folder across a table in an old brothel.

Which means the price is going to be obscene.

"And?" I say.

"And you spend thirty days with me."

The words land in the space between us like a match on gasoline. I feel them catch. Flare.

"Excuse me?"

"Thirty days," he repeats. "You accompany me. To events, meetings, dinners. You let me show you my world. At the end of the thirty days, the gallery is yours, free and clear, regardless of what happens between us."

"What happens between us," I repeat slowly. "What exactly do you think is going to happen between us?"

He leans forward. The distance between us shrinks to something dangerous.

"I think," he says, his voice low enough that I have to lean in to hear it, and I hate myself for leaning in, "that you've been performing independence for so long you've forgotten what it feels like to let someone else hold the weight. I think you're exhausted. I think you're brilliant and fierce and *tired*, and the idea of surrendering — not your career, not your identity, but that white-knuckled grip on control — terrifies you. And attracts you."

My mouth is dry. My pulse is a drumline.

"You don't know me."

"I know you ordered gin because it's what you drink when you're nervous. I know you chose this bar because it's sexual enough to feel like armor — you thought it would make me uncomfortable. I know you've touched the base of your throat four times since I sat down, which means your heart rate is elevated." He pauses. "And I know you haven't opened the folder, because if you open it, this becomes real."

I stare at him. The bar noise has receded to a low hum. The velvet booth feels like a confessional.

"Thirty days," I say. "And then what? You let me go?"

Something shifts in his expression. For a fraction of a second — less than a heartbeat — I see something that doesn't match the rest of him. Something raw and unguarded.

"That depends," he says, "on whether you want to be let go."

I should stand up. I should walk out. I should go home to my apartment in Alfama, pour a glass of wine, and spend the night updating my CV.

Instead, I open the folder.

## Chapter 4

### ALEKSANDR

She opens the folder.

I watch her eyes move across the document — quickly at first, then slowing as the details register. The financial terms. The operational guarantees. The clause about creative control. She reads like she curates: with precision, attention, and an instinct for what's being hidden.

She'll find the hidden thing. I made sure of it.

"This gives me veto power over acquisitions," she says, looking up.

"Yes."

"And final say on exhibition programming."

"Yes."

"And an independent budget that you can't touch."

"Yes."

"Why?"

Because I need you to trust the offer enough to accept it. Because the thirty days are what matters, and the gallery is the price I'm willing to pay.

"Because a gallery without artistic independence is a showroom," I say. "I don't invest in showrooms."

She closes the folder. Presses her fingers against it, like she's feeling for the pulse of the thing.

"I need to think about it."

"Of course."

"And I want my own lawyer to review the contract."

"I'd be disappointed if you didn't."

She looks at me. In the low light of Pensão Amor, surrounded by the ghosts of a thousand transactions far more explicit than ours, she looks like a woman standing at the edge of a decision that will change the shape of her life.

I've been that person. I know the vertigo.

"Why me?" she asks. And this time, the question is different. Not *why did you ask for me* but something deeper. *Why do you want me?*

I could lie. I'm exceptional at lying — it's a language I learned before I learned kindness. I could tell her it's business, strategy, the intersection of investment and opportunity.

Instead, I tell her a truth. Not the whole truth. But enough.

"Because you're the first person I've met who looks at the world and sees what it could be instead of what it is. And I've spent my entire life surrounded by people who only see what they can take."

She holds my gaze. Doesn't blink. Doesn't look away.

"That's either the most honest thing you've ever said," she murmurs, "or the most manipulative."

"Perhaps it's both."

A pause. Then, impossibly, she almost smiles.

"I'll give you my answer in forty-eight hours," she says. She slides out of the booth. Stands. Looks down at me. "Goodnight, Aleksandr."

She walks out of Pensão Amor with the leather folder under her arm, and I sit in the booth and finish my drink and feel something I haven't felt in years.

Uncertainty.

Marco materializes from the shadows near the bar. He always does this — appears exactly when needed, like a well-dressed haunting.

"She took the folder," he observes.

"She did."

"You didn't tell her about Vortex."

That I own Vortex Capital. That the company threatening her gallery is mine. That I created the problem I'm now offering to solve.

"No."

Marco is quiet for a moment. "She's going to find out."

"Eventually."

"And when she does?"

I set down my glass. Stand. Button my jacket.

"Then things will get interesting."

## Chapter 5

### SOFIA

I don't sleep.

I sit on the tiled floor of my apartment in Alfama with the contract spread around me like a crime scene, and I read every clause, every sub-clause, every carefully worded provision while the sounds of the neighborhood filter through my open window — a fado singer rehearsing three doors down, someone's television, the clatter of dishes, the distant hum of the Tejo.

My apartment is small — a converted attic in a crumbling eighteenth-century building on the Rua de São Miguel. The tiles are original azulejos, blue and white, cracked in places. The ceiling slopes so dramatically that I can only stand upright in the center of the room. The rent is cheap because the landlord is ancient and forgetful and doesn't know what the neighborhood is worth now.

I love this apartment. It was the first thing that was truly mine.

The contract is thorough. Whoever drafted it — and it has the fingerprints of an expensive firm, probably Morais Leitão or one of the big Lisbon outfits — understood exactly how to make an offer that a person in my position couldn't refuse. Five years of funding. Complete creative control. A salary that's triple what I currently make.

And the thirty days.

The clause is carefully vague. *The Recipient agrees to accompany the Benefactor to social, professional, and private engagements for a period of thirty (30) calendar days, beginning on a mutually agreed date.* There's language about travel, accommodation, wardrobe expenses — all covered. There's a confidentiality clause. There's a termination provision that allows either party to end the arrangement at any time, no penalty, with the gallery deal remaining intact.

That's the part I keep reading. *The gallery deal remaining intact.* Even if I walk away on day one, the gallery is saved.

Which means the thirty days aren't a transaction. They're not payment.

They're a seduction.

He's not buying me. He's daring me.

I press my palms against the cool tiles and close my eyes. I think about his voice in the bar — low, certain, threading through me like a current. *I think you've been performing independence for so long you've forgotten what it feels like to let someone else hold the weight.*

He's wrong. He has to be wrong. My independence isn't a performance — it's the bone structure of who I am. I built it from nothing, from the ashes of my father's gambling debts and my mother's slow, quiet death and the years of scraping and fighting and refusing, *refusing*, to be defined by what was taken from me.

But.

There's a but. And I hate the but.

*But* sometimes, in the dark, in the honest hours between two and four a.m., I am so tired of holding everything that my hands shake.

My phone lights up. A message from my best friend, Inês.

**Inês:** *How was the opening? Did you sell anything? Did that guy show up?*

I told Inês about the meeting. I tell Inês everything, mostly because she'd find out anyway — she's a journalist at *Observador* with a nose for secrets.

**Me:** *He showed up. He wants to buy the gallery and fund it for five years.*

**Inês:** *?????? That's amazing???*

**Me:** *There's a catch.*

**Inês:** *There's always a catch with billionaires. That's literally their whole thing.*

**Me:** *He wants me to spend 30 days with him. Accompanying him to events, dinners. Basically being... available.*

A long pause. Then:

**Inês:** *Sofia. Is this man trying to Pretty Woman you?*

I almost laugh. Almost.

**Me:** *The contract says the gallery deal goes through either way. Even if I end the 30 days early.*

**Inês:** *Then what's in it for him?*

**Me:** *I don't know.*

**Inês:** *You do know. You just don't want to say it.*

I put the phone down. She's right. I do know.

He wants me to fall.

Not into his bed — though that's part of it, certainly, the current between us was so strong I could practically hear it hum. He wants me to fall into *him*. Into the orbit of a man who radiates gravity. He wants to prove that my independence is a fortress with a door, and that he has the key.

And the worst part — the part that keeps me awake on the floor of my Alfama apartment at three in the morning — is that a small, furious, honest part of me wants to see if he's right.

I pick up the phone.

**Me:** *I'm going to do it.*

**Inês:** *Estás maluca.*

Maybe I am crazy. But the gallery is my life. And thirty days is just thirty days.

Right?

## Chapter 6

### ALEKSANDR

She calls at exactly forty-eight hours. Not forty-seven. Not forty-nine. Precisely on the deadline she set.

"I accept," she says. No preamble. "With conditions."

I'm standing at the window of my office in the Vasco da Gama Tower, watching the Tejo catch the morning light. The river is a sheet of hammered silver. A container ship moves slowly toward the Ponte 25 de Abril, small as a toy from this height.

"Name them."

"First. My lawyer reviews and approves the final contract. Non-negotiable."

"Agreed."

"Second. I keep my apartment. I go home when I want. I'm not moving into some penthouse like a kept woman."

"You'll keep your apartment. Though you may find yourself using it less than you expect."

A beat of silence. I hear her breathing. I catalog it — slightly quickened, controlled.

"Third. The thirty days start next Monday. That gives me a week to brief my assistant on the gallery operations."

"Monday works."

"Fourth." A pause. "Whatever this is — whatever you think is going to happen between us — I'm not sleeping with you as part of the deal. If anything... *happens*... it's because I choose it. Not because it's expected."

"Sofia." I let her name sit in my mouth. A deliberate pleasure. "If I wanted someone in my bed without the complication of choice, I could have that with a phone call. That's not what interests me."

"What interests you?"

"The moment when a woman who has every reason to say no says yes. Not because she has to. Because she *wants* to. Because the desire is so consuming that her pride, her resistance, her carefully constructed walls — all of it — becomes less important than the need to be touched."

Silence. The kind that has texture.

"You're very confident," she says finally.

"I'm very patient."

"Those aren't the same thing."

"No. They're not. But in my experience, the combination is... effective."

Another silence. Then: "Monday, then. Send me the details."

She hangs up.

I lower the phone. Look out at the river.

Monday. Five days from now. Five days to prepare.

I turn to my desk. Open my laptop. Begin the first of several arrangements: a car, a wardrobe consultation at the Avenida da Liberdade Dior boutique (she'll refuse — I'll enjoy that), reservations at Belcanto (she'll appreciate the food and resent the implication), and a private viewing at the Museu Nacional de Arte Antiga (she'll forget to resent anything at all).

I know her. Not the way a lover knows — not yet. But the way an architect knows a building he intends to redesign. I know the load-bearing walls. I know where the light comes in. I know which rooms she keeps locked.

The locked rooms are what interest me most.

Marco knocks. Enters.

"The Vortex Capital paperwork is ready," he says. "The sale can be triggered within twenty-four hours of her signing."

"Good."

"Aleksandr." He says my name the way he always does when he's about to say something I don't want to hear. "The background on Ricardo Marques. The connection to your father —"

"Is not relevant to this conversation."

"It's relevant to her. If she finds out that your father and hers —"

"She won't. Not yet."

"And when she does?"

I close the laptop. Look at Marco.

"When she does, I'll deal with it. I always do."

Afterwards, we lie tangled together. Breathing hard. Her head on my chest, my hand in her hair, and the silence between us is not empty but full — full of everything we said and didn't say, everything we did and survived, the wreckage and the repair.

I press my lips to the top of her head. She presses her palm flat against my heart.

"Aleksandr," she murmurs, half-asleep.

"Yes?"

"If you ever lie to me again, I will destroy you."

"I believe you."

"Good." A pause. "Goodnight."

"Goodnight, Sofia."

She sleeps. I hold her. And for the first time in twenty years, I don't plan what comes next.

I just let it come.

## Epilogue

### SOFIA

Day thirty.

I wake to sunlight and an empty bed and a note on the pillow.

*Rooftop. Come up.*

I pull on a sweater and take the stairs — not my rooftop, but his. He's brought me to the building on Rua da Atalaia, the one he bought for the view. Paulo must have driven us here while I was still half-asleep. I push open the door to the roof and step into the Lisbon morning.

He's there. Standing at the railing. The city is spread out below us — terracotta and white and blue, the river silver, the bridge red, the sky enormous and clear and achingly bright.

On the concrete beside him is a table. On the table is coffee, pastéis de nata, and a single file folder.

"What's this?" I ask.

"The last secret."

I open the folder. Inside is a document — a corporate dissolution order. Vortex Capital, the shell company, the holding structure, all of it. Being permanently dissolved.

And beneath it, a letter. Handwritten.

*To Sofia Marques,*

*I built Vortex Capital to control you. I'm destroying it because you taught me that control is the opposite of love.*

*The gallery is yours. It was always supposed to be yours. Not because I gave it to you — because you earned it. Every late night, every rejected grant you fought through, every time you refused to let the bastards (including me) grind you down.*

*I don't know what happens next. I don't have a plan. For the first time in my life, I don't want one.*

*I just want to drink coffee with you on a rooftop and see what the day brings.*

*— A*

I look up. He's watching me. The morning light is doing its golden, lying thing, but today I don't mind. Today the light feels honest.

"You dissolved the company," I say.

"I don't need it anymore."

"Because you have what you wanted?"

"Because what I wanted changed." He steps toward me. "I came to Lisbon for revenge. I found you. And you were — are — better than anything I was looking for."

I hold the letter. I look at this man — this complicated, dangerous, broken, brilliant man who dismantled his own empire and stood in a gallery with a bag of tiles and told the truth when it would have been so much easier to lie.

I don't forgive him. Not completely. Not yet. That will take time — real time, unglamorous time, the kind measured in arguments and difficult conversations and slow, hard-won trust.

But I'm here. On a rooftop, in the morning, in Lisbon. With him.

I pour the coffee. Take a pastel de nata. Sit on the concrete ledge and look out at the city that gave me everything.

"Sit down," I say. "Drink your coffee."

He sits. He drinks. Our shoulders touch.

Below us, Lisbon wakes up. The trams rattle through Bairro Alto. The Tejo catches the sun and scatters it into a million fragments of light. Somewhere in Alfama, a fado singer is rehearsing. Somewhere in Chiado, my gallery is opening its doors.

"Aleksandr."

"Yes?"

"This doesn't mean I trust you."

"I know."

"It means I'm willing to try."

He takes my hand. I let him.

"That's enough," he says. "That's more than enough."

We sit on the rooftop, and we watch the city, and we begin.

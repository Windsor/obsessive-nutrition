# ENCANTADA — Book 1: Chapters 1–10

---

## Chapter One
### MAYA

The GPS betrays me somewhere outside Ponte de Lima.

One moment, the friendly British voice is guiding me along a perfectly reasonable two-lane highway lined with vineyards and granite walls. The next, she's instructing me to turn right onto what is clearly a goat path. I squint through the windshield of my rental Fiat — a car so small I feel like I'm wearing it rather than driving it — and try to reconcile the cheerful blue arrow on my phone with the narrow dirt lane that disappears into a tunnel of overhanging chestnut trees.

"Recalculating," the GPS announces, which is code for *you're on your own, idiot.*

I take the turn anyway, because what's the worst that can happen? I've already lost my research grant, my boyfriend, and my apartment in Boston. Dying in a Portuguese ditch would at least give my obituary some flair. *Maya Chen, 28, marine biologist, eaten by countryside. Survived by no one who will return her Tupperware.*

The Fiat bounces over a rut that makes my teeth click together, and I grip the steering wheel with both hands. The road — I'm using the term generously — winds uphill through terraced vineyards heavy with late-summer green. The vines hang over granite walls so old they look like they grew out of the hillside. Every few hundred meters, there's a crumbling stone cross or a shrine to some saint I don't recognize, their ceramic faces weathered smooth by Atlantic rain.

It's beautiful. Stupidly, heartbreakingly beautiful. The kind of landscape that makes you understand why people believed in enchantment.

I don't believe in enchantment. I believe in peer-reviewed studies, dissolved oxygen levels, and the bioluminescent mechanisms of *Gaussia princeps*. But even my aggressively rational brain has to admit: the Minho region of northern Portugal looks like it was designed by someone who took the concept of "pastoral charm" and cranked it to eleven.

The road levels out, and I see it.

The quinta.

My grandmother's quinta.

*My* quinta, technically, though the word feels absurd in my mouth. I'm a Chinese-American woman from Dorchester who eats ramen standing over the sink. I don't own a Portuguese farmhouse. That's a thing that happens to characters in Nancy Meyers movies, women with linen pants and emotional availability.

And yet.

I pull the Fiat to a stop in front of a low granite wall, half-collapsed and drowning in ivy. Beyond it, the house rises — two stories of weathered stone with a red clay tile roof, at least a third of which seems to be actively composting. The façade is covered in climbing jasmine, the white flowers so thick that the whole place smells like someone's dream of summer. A massive fig tree leans against the eastern wall, its branches heavy with fruit that nobody's picked in what looks like years.

It's beautiful. It's also clearly trying to return to the earth from which it was built. The shutters hang at drunken angles. The front door is a slab of oak so warped it looks like a funhouse mirror. The courtyard is a riot of wildflowers pushing through cracked flagstone, and somewhere behind the house, I can hear the persistent trickle of water — a spring, maybe, or a very determined leak.

I get out of the car and just stand there for a minute, letting the July heat settle on my shoulders like a hand. The air smells like jasmine and warm stone and, underneath it all, something else — something mineral and green, like wet granite after rain. Cicadas scream from the fig tree. A rooster crows from somewhere downhill, which feels aggressively on-brand.

"Okay, Avó Lúcia," I say to nobody. "What the hell was this about?"

My grandmother left Boston when I was seven. I have exactly four memories of her: the smell of cinnamon on her hands, a lullaby in Portuguese I can't quite remember, the way she'd pull my braids and call me *ratinha* — little mouse — and the fight. The last one. Mom screaming, Avó silent, the front door closing with a click that sounded like a bone breaking.

Mom never talked about her after that. When I asked, she'd get the face — the one that meant the conversation was a locked door and I didn't have the key. All I knew was that Avó Lúcia went back to Portugal and never came home.

Three months ago, a lawyer in Braga sent me a letter. My grandmother had died — peacefully, in her sleep, at eighty-nine — and left me everything. The quinta, the land, the contents of the house. My mother, notably, got nothing. The lawyer's letter included a single handwritten line from Avó's will:

*Para a minha ratinha. Vem ver o que eu guardei para ti.*

*For my little mouse. Come see what I kept for you.*

I cried for an hour in my empty apartment, surrounded by boxes from the breakup, and then I booked a flight.

Now I'm here, sweating in a sundress, about to enter a house that probably has structural opinions about my presence.

The front door requires shoulder and profanity in roughly equal measure. When it finally gives, it swings open onto a foyer that smells like dust and dried lavender and old paper. The ceiling is high — wooden beams, dark with age — and the floor is worn terracotta tile. To the left, a kitchen with a massive stone hearth. To the right, a sitting room where someone has draped white sheets over the furniture like the world's saddest ghost convention.

I drop my suitcase by the door and start exploring.

The kitchen is the heart of the place. A long granite counter, a farmhouse sink the size of a bathtub, copper pots hanging from hooks that have left green ghosts on the whitewashed wall. The hearth is big enough to stand in, and above it, someone has carved a pattern into the stone — a sinuous, coiling shape that might be a vine or might be a serpent, depending on how much wine you've had. There's a gas stove from approximately 1987, and when I turn the knob, it clicks three times before producing a flame the color of a bruise. Still works. Small miracles.

Upstairs, two bedrooms. The master is clearly where Avó lived — the bed is neatly made with a crocheted blanket, and on the nightstand, a pair of reading glasses and a book of Pessoa poems, spine cracked to a page about the sea. The second bedroom is smaller, with a window overlooking the valley. From here, I can see the terraced vineyards cascading down the hillside to a river that glints silver in the distance. The Cávado, I think. Or the Lima. I should probably know which.

I find the journal in the closet of the master bedroom, tucked behind a stack of folded linen that smells like mothballs and regret. It's a leather-bound notebook, the kind you find in stationery shops that are trying too hard, but the leather is soft with age and the pages are thick, cream-colored, covered in my grandmother's spidery handwriting.

I sit on the bed and open it.

The first few pages are what I'd expect — notes about the quinta, records of repairs, a list of what's planted in the garden (*tomates, courgettes, alecrim, hortelã*). Then the drawings start.

Serpents.

Page after page of serpents. But not like any snake I've seen — and I've seen a lot, because marine biology means you encounter every conceivable creature that the ocean has stress-tested into existence. These are sinuous, almost architectural, coiling around standing stones and through cave systems drawn in meticulous cross-section. Some of the serpents have human faces. Some have crowns. In one drawing, a massive serpentine figure coils around a glowing stone, and underneath, Avó has written:

*A pedra guarda. O encantado guarda a pedra.*

*The stone guards. The enchanted one guards the stone.*

I turn the pages faster. More drawings — maps of tunnel systems, symbols I don't recognize, what looks like a family tree with names going back centuries. Some entries are in Portuguese, some in a language I've never seen — angular, sharp, like someone tried to write with a knife.

The last entry is dated three years ago. Just one line:

*Ela virá. O sangue lembra.*

*She will come. The blood remembers.*

"Okay, Avó," I say to the empty room. "That's not creepy at all."

I close the journal and press it to my chest, feeling the leather warm against my skin. My grandmother was many things — stubborn, secretive, possibly the reason my mother is the emotional equivalent of a sealed vault — but she wasn't crazy. If she spent decades drawing serpent creatures in a secret journal, she had a reason.

Probably folklore. The Minho region is apparently lousy with it — enchanted Mouros, serpent queens, treasures hidden under dolmens. My grandmother grew up here. She was probably recording local legends. The biologist in me wants to categorize this, file it under "cultural anthropology, amateur, see also: retirement hobbies."

The other part of me — the part that booked a flight to Portugal three weeks after her funeral, the part that feels a pull in my chest when I look out the window at the green hills — that part isn't so sure.

I spend the rest of the afternoon cleaning. Not the deep kind — more the "remove enough dust to breathe without developing an origin story" kind. I sweep the kitchen, wipe down the counter, figure out the plumbing (cold water only, but it runs clear after a minute of alarming brown), and discover that the fridge works if you lean something heavy against its left side. Inside, someone has left — recently, it seems — a bottle of vinho verde, a wedge of São Jorge cheese, and a note that says *Bem-vinda* in handwriting that isn't my grandmother's.

I decide not to question this. In Portugal, I'm learning, food appears. You don't interrogate miracles.

Dinner is cheese, bread I bought at a gas station on the drive up, and half the bottle of vinho verde, which is crisp and bright and tastes like biting into a green apple at the exact moment a wave breaks over your feet. I eat on the front step, watching the sun sink behind the hills in a gradient of gold and bruised violet that makes me want to call someone. I don't call anyone. There's no one I want to tell about this who would understand why it matters.

The stars come out. I've never seen this many stars. Boston's light pollution has been gaslighting me about the sky my entire life.

I'm halfway to sleep on the step, wine-warm and road-tired, when I hear it.

A hum.

Low. Deep. Not mechanical — there's no steady rhythm, no electrical buzz. It's more like a vibration felt in the bones, a resonance that seems to come from the ground itself. From *below.*

I press my hand to the flagstone. It's warm.

The hum pulses once, like a heartbeat, and then stops.

I stay very still for a long time, my hand on the warm stone, my grandmother's journal upstairs full of serpents, the stars wheeling overhead like they know something I don't.

"Pipes," I say firmly. "Old pipes."

I go inside. I lock the door. I do not go to the basement.

But I dream of warm stone and golden eyes, and in the dream, something is humming my name.

## Chapter Two
### TRISTÃO

Sixty-three years.

I have guarded the ward-stone beneath this quinta for sixty-three years, and in that time, the world above has changed in ways I find both fascinating and profoundly irritating. They have put a man on the moon and invented a device that allows them to argue with strangers across the ocean. They have discovered the structure of their own blood and yet remain incapable of building a road to this property that does not threaten the suspension of every vehicle that attempts it.

Sixty-three years of silence. Of stone and dark and the steady pulse of the ward beneath my coils. Of feeling the seasons change through the tremor of tree roots and the shift of underground water. Of listening to Lúcia's footsteps above me — first quick and purposeful, then slower, then achingly slow — and never speaking to her, because that was the agreement. I guard. She keeps the house. The blood stays close to the stone. The ward holds.

The arrangement was Lúcia's idea. She was practical, that woman — terrifyingly so. When she returned from America, fifty years old and furious about something she never explained, she stood at the entrance to my cavern and said, "I know what you are. I know what's down there. I'll keep the house if you keep the ward. Don't talk to me, don't show yourself, and don't you dare die before I do."

I agreed. What else could I do? The ward-stone requires old blood nearby to maintain its resonance, and Lúcia carried the strongest old blood I'd encountered in two centuries. Her grandmother before her had been the same — a fierce, silent woman who brought me honey cakes on the solstice and never once looked directly at my true form.

The women of this bloodline are not easily impressed.

Lúcia is gone now. Three months dead, her heartbeat absent from the rhythm of the house for the first time since I began my vigil. The silence she left is a wound. Not because I loved her — we were not that to each other — but because she was the last thread connecting this ward-stone to the blood that feeds it. Without old blood nearby, the stone weakens. I can feel it — a thinning, like fabric worn too long. A tremor in the deep that was not there before.

I was beginning to calculate how long I had. Months, perhaps. A year if the subterranean fault line held steady. I was composing messages to Casa da Serpente, swallowing the bitter wine of asking my father's court for help after six decades of self-imposed exile, when—

She arrives.

I feel her before I hear the miserable little car grinding up the road. A shift in the ward-stone's pulse, a brightening, like someone opening a window in a dark room. Old blood. *Strong* old blood. Stronger than Lúcia's, which should be impossible, because the old blood dilutes with every generation that lives away from the stone.

Unless it doesn't. Unless the blood, like the stone, has been waiting.

I am deep in the cavern when the resonance hits — I've been inspecting the northern fault line where the ward's influence is thinnest, my true form uncoiled along the passage, scales scraping granite. The tremor reaches me like a wave. My coils seize. My scales flush — not the usual deep emerald but *gold*, bright burning gold, a color I have only seen described in the oldest texts of my house.

Gold is the color of the Encanto.

*No.*

I press my palms flat against the cavern wall and breathe. The Encanto is a fairy tale. A story the elders tell to give meaning to marriages that are, in truth, political arrangements. A magical resonance between fated mates — it is romantic, certainly. It is also, in my 287 years of experience, fictional.

My scales pulse gold again. My blood is *singing*. There is no other word for it — a harmonic has entered my bloodstream, a frequency that matches the ward-stone's hum but pitched to a note I have never heard. A note that says *her, her, her* with every beat of my ancient heart.

I shift to human form. It's an act of will — pulling the serpentine mass of my lower body into the compressed architecture of human legs, retracting scales into skin that feels too thin, too fragile, too *small*. I've worn this form thousands of times. Tonight, for the first time, it feels like a lie.

In human form, I'm tall. Angular. Brown-skinned, dark-haired, amber-eyed — close enough to pass for Portuguese, which I am, in the sense that I was here before there was a Portugal. The eyes are the problem. They catch light wrong, the pupils narrowing to slits when I'm not careful. Lúcia told me once that my human face was "unsettling, like a painting where the eyes follow you." She did not intend this as a compliment.

I climb. Through the cavern, up the passage that runs beneath the quinta's foundation, to the gap in the basement wall where the mortar crumbled decades ago and I never bothered to repair it. I press my face to the gap.

And I see her.

She's in the kitchen. The light is wrong — she's turned on that anemic bulb Lúcia installed ten years ago, the one that makes everything look like it's being viewed through old honey — but I can see her clearly. My kind sees well in dim light. It's one of the few advantages of being a creature designed for the underground.

She's young. Mid-twenties, perhaps. Chinese features — Lúcia married a Chinese man in America, I remember, and the granddaughter shows it in the shape of her eyes, the black silk of her hair pulled into a knot that's collapsing slowly as she moves. But the bones are Lúcia's. The stubborn set of the jaw. The way she stands with her weight slightly forward, like she's preparing to argue with the universe.

She's eating cheese off a knife and drinking vinho verde directly from the bottle. She's wearing a sundress with a stain on the hem. She's talking to herself — something about pipes, I think, though my Portuguese-tuned ear is still adjusting to her American English.

She is, without question, the most beautiful thing I have seen in sixty-three years of darkness.

The Encanto *roars.*

My hands slam against the stone wall. I feel my nails elongate, claws scratching granite, and wrench them back with an effort that makes my vision blur. My scales are trying to surface — I can feel them rippling beneath the thin disguise of human skin, desperate to emerge, to display, to show her what I am.

*Hers*, they say. *We are hers.*

I am the heir to Casa da Serpente. I am the son of the Rei das Pedras. I am 287 years old, I have killed in battle and negotiated treaties and survived the political crucible of a court that would make the Medicis look like children playing at intrigue. I do not lose control.

I am losing control.

I force myself away from the wall. Back down the passage. Into the cavern where the ward-stone pulses its eternal blue-white light, and I coil around it the way I have for decades, wrapping myself in its steady rhythm until the roaring in my blood subsides to something merely deafening.

I need to think.

The old blood has returned — stronger than expected, in a vessel I was not prepared for. The ward-stone responded to her arrival. My blood responded to her arrival. The Encanto, which I have spent my entire life dismissing as mythology, is apparently very real and very specifically interested in Lúcia's granddaughter.

This is, strategically speaking, both a gift and a catastrophe.

The gift: old blood near the stone means the ward strengthens. If she stays, the weakening I've felt these past months will reverse. The tremors from the deep — the stirring of what lies beneath the Atlantic — may calm. Time. She gives me time.

The catastrophe: everything else.

The Encanto is not a subtle magic. It is a claiming — a bond that, once triggered, drives both parties toward completion with the patience of a river wearing through stone. I will want her. Every instinct I possess will orient toward her like iron filings toward a lodestone. I will know her heartbeat, her moods, her location. I will be unable to think of anything else with the same clarity. If she leaves, the bond will remain — an open wound that never heals, the saudade my people sing about in our lowest, oldest songs.

And she does not know. She does not know about the Mouros, the ward-stone, the Encanto, or the fact that a 287-year-old serpent creature is currently having an existential crisis in the cavern beneath her bedroom because she ate cheese in a way that made his ancient heart try to climb out of his chest.

She will think she is alone.

I press my forehead to the ward-stone and feel its cool pulse against my overheated skin. I need a plan. I need to approach carefully, to earn her trust, to explain the old blood without terrifying her, to reveal the ward-stone's nature in a way that her rational mind can process.

I also need to not shift involuntarily and eat her furniture in a territorial display, which is apparently something my body is now considering.

Above me, she moves through the house. I can feel her footsteps through the stone — quick, purposeful, the cadence of a woman who is cleaning and cursing simultaneously. She finds things. I hear her voice, muffled but clear to my ears: "Jesus, Avó, how many crocheted blankets does one woman need?" and later, quieter, "Oh. Oh, okay. That's a lot of snakes."

She found the journal.

Good. The journal was supposed to be found — Lúcia told me she'd leave a record. "The girl won't believe you," she said, in one of the few times she addressed me directly, near the end. "She's like me. She needs evidence. Give her the book first. The monster later."

It stung, the word *monster*. It always does, no matter how many centuries pass. But Lúcia was right. She was almost always right.

The hours pass. The house settles. She eats on the front step — I feel the vibration of her weight on the flagstone, and my blood responds to even this, this tiny communion of her body and the stone I guard. It's absurd. It's humiliating. I have guarded an ancient ward against the stirring of a primordial giant beneath the sea, and I am undone by the knowledge that a woman is sitting on my doorstep drinking wine.

Then she hears the hum.

I feel the ward-stone pulse — it does this sometimes, a deep resonance that escapes the cavern and vibrates through the foundation. Normally, no one hears it. Lúcia never mentioned it, and her blood was strong. But this woman, this granddaughter with the impossible blood, presses her hand to the flagstone, and the ward-stone responds.

It *reaches* for her.

I have never seen it do this. In 287 years, in sixty-three years of intimate proximity with this particular stone, I have never felt it reach for a human. It pulses once, warm and bright, and I feel the echo in my own chest — a swell of something that might be hope, if hope is a thing that burns.

She pulls her hand away. Goes inside. Locks the door.

I wait in the dark, my scales fading from gold to their usual emerald, the Encanto settling from a roar to a steady, unrelenting hum that I know, with the certainty of stone, will never be silent again.

I need to see her.

Not through a gap in the mortar. Not through the mediated vibration of her footsteps on my ceiling. I need to *see* her. To be near her. To learn whether this impossible resonance in my blood is what the old texts promise — a bond that, once formed, makes all the centuries before it feel like waiting.

I have been waiting.

I think, perhaps, I have been waiting for a very long time.

Tomorrow. I will find a way. Human form, a plausible excuse, a careful approach. I will be patient. I will be controlled. I will not terrify Lúcia's granddaughter on her first full day in a country she doesn't know, in a house she doesn't understand, above a world she doesn't believe exists.

I can be patient.

The ward-stone pulses, and somewhere above me, she turns in her sleep, and my blood sings her name — a name I don't know yet — in a language older than the stones.

I can be patient.

I may not survive it, but I can be patient.

## Chapter Three
### MAYA

I wake up to sunlight and roosters and the absolute certainty that something is wrong with this house.

Not wrong in a "the roof is caving in" way — though, honestly, give it six months — but wrong in a "the floor is warm when it shouldn't be" way. I lie in my grandmother's bed, in my grandmother's sheets that smell like lavender and dust, and press my bare feet to the terracotta tile. It's warm. Not sun-warmed — the bedroom faces west, and it's barely seven a.m. Warm from below.

*Hot water pipes under the floor,* I tell myself. *Geothermal heating. This is a Mediterranean climate. Stone retains heat.*

These are all reasonable explanations. I am a reasonable person.

I am also a person who dreamed about golden eyes all night, which is significantly less reasonable, so I'm going to make coffee and ignore everything below the foundation of this house until I've had caffeine.

The gas stove takes three tries and produces a flame that smells faintly of brimstone. I find a *cafeteira* — one of those stovetop espresso makers that every Portuguese household apparently owns on a molecular level — and a tin of coffee that, miraculously, is not stale. It brews with a sound like a cat purring through gravel, and the first sip is so strong it makes my back teeth ache.

Perfect. I could weep.

Armed with coffee and the kind of reckless optimism that only jet lag and denial can produce, I set out to explore the property.

The quinta is larger than I realized. The house is the centerpiece, but the land stretches back into terraced levels held up by granite retaining walls. There's an overgrown vegetable garden where tomato plants have gone feral and are waging territorial war against an equally aggressive colony of courgettes. A stone outbuilding that might have been a stable — or a wine press, judging by the massive granite basin inside that still smells faintly of fermented grapes. An ancient stone well with a rusted hand-pump and water so cold it numbs my fingers.

Behind the stable, a path leads into a grove of chestnut trees, their canopy so thick it turns the July morning into a green twilight. At the far edge of the grove, the land drops sharply, and I find myself looking down into a valley so lush it looks rendered. Vineyards. A village — terracotta roofs and a white church with a bell tower. The river. Mist rising off it like the earth is breathing.

I take a photo and send it to Jade with the caption: *Guess I live here now. The roosters are relentless.*

She responds in seconds: *GIRL. That view is ILLEGAL. Are there hot farmers?? Is this Mamma Mia 3?? DETAILS.*

I smile, pocket my phone, and keep walking.

The path circles back to the house, and that's when I notice the woman standing at my front gate.

She's small — maybe five feet in sensible shoes, which she's not wearing. She's wearing actual *chinelos de quarto* — house slippers, the kind with the little heel — and a floral housecoat that suggests she left her own kitchen in a hurry. Her hair is white, cut short and practical, and her face is a topographic map of a life well-lived. She's holding a ceramic dish covered with a tea towel and looking at me with the bright, assessing eyes of someone who has already formed seventeen opinions and is deciding which one to share first.

"Bom dia!" she calls. "Tu és a neta da Lúcia?"

My Portuguese is functional but rusty — Mom made sure I learned it, even after Avó left, one of the few acknowledgments that the other half of my heritage existed. "Sim," I say. "Sou a Maya."

The woman's face breaks into a smile so wide it could power a solar panel. She bustles through the gate, thrusts the dish into my hands, and starts talking at a speed that makes my language processing center file for bankruptcy.

I catch fragments: *coitadinha* (poor little thing), something about being too thin, a reference to Lúcia that involves the word *saudade*, and what I'm fairly certain is a comprehensive critique of my rental car.

"Desculpe," I manage. "O meu português é—"

"Terrible," she finishes in English, with a heavy accent and zero remorse. "But you have the face. Lúcia's face. Same stubborn chin." She taps my chin with a finger that feels like it's made of steel cable. "I am Celeste. Dona Celeste. I live there." She points vaguely downhill. "Your grandmother was my friend for forty years, and she told me you would come, and she told me to feed you. Eat."

She pulls the towel off the dish. Inside: golden pastéis de nata, still warm, their tops blistered and caramelized. The smell hits me like a religious experience.

"Oh my God," I say.

"Sim," says Dona Celeste, as if this is a perfectly reasonable response to pastry.

We end up in the kitchen — her talking, me eating, the cafeteira working overtime — and within thirty minutes, I know the following: Dona Celeste is eighty-three, widowed twice ("the first was handsome, the second was useful"), has four children she describes as "disappointing but alive," and considers herself the unofficial guardian of this stretch of hillside.

She also knew my grandmother better than anyone.

"Lúcia was..." Celeste pauses, stirring her coffee with the focus of a woman disarming a bomb. "Complicated. Like this land. Beautiful, but there were things underneath. Things she didn't talk about." She looks at me over the rim of her cup. "You found the book, yes? The journal?"

I blink. "How did you know about the journal?"

Celeste waves a hand. "She told me. 'When the girl comes, she'll find the book. Make sure she eats first.' Your grandmother had priorities."

"What's in it?" I ask. "The drawings — the serpents, the symbols—"

"Stories," Celeste says, a little too quickly. "Old stories. This region, you know, it's full of them. The Mouros Encantados — you've heard of them?"

I shake my head.

Celeste settles into her chair like a woman preparing to hold court. "Before the Christians came, before the Romans even, there were the Mouros. Not the Moors from Africa — different. Older. They lived in the hills, in the caves, under the stones. They were..." She makes a gesture that encompasses something vast and indefinable. "Encantados. Enchanted. Not human, not animal. Something between. The stories say they were serpents who could wear the shape of people. Beautiful. Dangerous."

She sips her coffee.

"When the Christians conquered, the Mouros went underground. Literally. Beneath the castros, the dolmens, the old places. The stories say they're still there. Waiting. And sometimes" — she leans forward — "sometimes, the women who live near the old places, they feel things. A humming in the ground. Warmth where there should be cold. Dreams of golden eyes."

The coffee in my cup ripples.

"That's—" I start.

"Folklore," Celeste finishes, smiling in a way that doesn't reach her eyes. "Just folklore. Don't worry, menina. The snakes under the hill are just stories." She stands, brushes pastry crumbs from her housecoat. "Now. Show me the house. I want to see what needs fixing before it falls on your pretty head."

The tour reveals exactly how much trouble I'm in. Dona Celeste catalogs structural problems with the efficiency of a FEMA inspector who's seen some things: the roof needs re-tiling (half the terra-cotta is cracked or missing), the second-floor bathroom has a leak that's been conducting a slow-motion assassination of the ceiling below, the electrical wiring is "artistic" (her word for "liable to burn the house down"), and the plumbing is a theological question.

But she saves the basement for last.

"Your grandmother kept this locked," Celeste says, standing at the top of the stone steps that descend from the kitchen. "Always. Even from me."

The stairs go down into cool darkness. I pull out my phone and turn on the flashlight. The beam cuts through air that smells of damp stone and something else — something metallic, like copper. Or blood. The steps are worn smooth in the center, the granite hollowed by centuries of feet.

At the bottom: a room. Low-ceilinged, stone-walled, with that particular quality of underground silence that feels like being held in a fist. It's mostly empty — some wine racks (bare), a few wooden crates of the kind that contained nothing exciting, a shelf of dusty preserves that I am not brave enough to open.

And at the far wall: a door.

Not a wooden door. Stone. A slab of granite fitted into the wall so precisely that the seam is barely visible — I only notice it because the stone is a slightly different color, darker, with a faint pattern carved into its surface. The same pattern from Avó's journal. The same coiling, serpentine design.

I press my hand to it.

The stone is warm.

"Huh," I say.

I push. Nothing. I lean my full weight against it. It doesn't budge — not in the way that a stuck door doesn't budge, but in the way that a mountain doesn't budge. It's not a door that's stuck. It's a door that is *refusing.*

"It never opened for Lúcia either," Celeste says from the top of the stairs, her voice carefully neutral. "She said it was sealed. An old cellar, she told me. Nothing behind it."

I press my ear to the stone. And I hear it — the hum. The same low vibration from last night, clearer now, resonating through the stone and into my skull like a tuning fork pressed to bone.

"There's something behind this," I say.

"There is always something behind closed doors," says Dona Celeste. "The question is whether you want to open them."

She feeds me lunch — caldo verde, the iconic Portuguese green soup, which she somehow produces from my empty kitchen through what I can only assume is witchcraft. We eat in companionable near-silence, the kind that comes from a shared understanding that sometimes food is more important than conversation.

When she leaves, she holds my face in both hands and looks at me with an intensity that makes my sternum ache.

"Your grandmother wanted you here," she says. "Remember that. Whatever you find — she wanted you here."

"That's ominous," I say.

"It's Portuguese," she corrects, and leaves.

I spend the afternoon with Avó's journal, sitting cross-legged on the bed with my laptop open, trying to cross-reference her drawings with actual archaeological records. The serpent motifs match Castreja culture — the pre-Roman Celtic-influenced civilization that occupied this region. The standing stones she's drawn look like real dolmens I can find on Google Maps, within walking distance of the quinta. The symbol on the basement door appears in her journal at least thirty times, always labeled *a guarda* — the ward, or the guard.

The family tree is where it gets interesting. Avó traced our bloodline back twelve generations to a woman named Inês, born in 1640, described as *filha do sangue antigo* — daughter of the old blood. Whatever "old blood" means.

By evening, I've convinced myself of two things:

One: My grandmother was doing serious amateur ethnographic work, documenting local legends with a rigor that would make a folklorist weep.

Two: Something is humming beneath this house, and it is not the pipes.

I eat cereal for dinner — I found a box of Chocapic in a bag of groceries that the ghost of Portuguese hospitality apparently left in the pantry. I eat it dry, standing at the kitchen window, watching the light drain from the valley like water from a tub.

The hum is louder tonight. Or maybe I'm just listening harder.

I go to bed. I lock the bedroom door, which is absurd, because I am alone in a house on a hill in a country where the most dangerous animal is probably a wild boar, and I am a grown woman with a PhD who does not believe in enchanted serpents living under old farmhouses.

I dream of golden eyes again. But this time, the eyes are closer. This time, they're watching me from the dark with an expression that I can only describe, against every instinct I have, as *hungry.*

I wake at 3 a.m. with my hand pressed flat against the wall, and the wall is warm, and the hum is steady as a heartbeat, and somewhere deep beneath me, something is awake.

## Chapter Four
### TRISTÃO

She argues with furniture.

I did not know this was something humans did, but Maya Chen — her name is Maya; I learned it when the old woman Celeste came and said it aloud, and the sound of it hit my blood like a stone dropped into still water — Maya Chen argues with furniture.

"You are a cabinet," she says to the wardrobe in the master bedroom, which has swollen with humidity and refuses to open. "You are an inanimate object. You do not have *opinions.* Open."

The wardrobe does not open.

"Fine," she says. "We'll do this the hard way." She braces her foot against the wall and heaves, and the door comes free with a shriek of wood that makes me flinch in the passage below. She stumbles back, catches herself on the bed, and says, "Ha. Who's inanimate *now,* you piece of—"

The wardrobe door falls off its hinges.

She stares at it for a long moment. Then she picks it up, leans it against the wall with great dignity, and says, "We'll discuss this later."

I am in the narrow space between the basement wall and the cavern entrance, coiled in a position that would be uncomfortable if comfort were something I still had the capacity to notice. I do not. Every particle of my awareness is oriented upward, toward the footsteps, the voice, the heartbeat that has become the axis around which my existence turns.

It has been two days since her arrival, and the Encanto has not settled. If anything, it has deepened. I know her rhythms now — she wakes early, brews coffee with the fervor of a religious rite, explores the house with the methodical intensity of a woman cataloging a new ecosystem. She eats badly and irregularly. She talks to herself constantly, a running monologue of observations, complaints, and occasional bursts of singing (off-key, enthusiastic, songs I don't recognize — she favors one about a woman named Jolene who is apparently a threat of some kind).

She has spent hours with Lúcia's journal. I can feel her concentration through the stone — a focused heat, like a lens gathering light. The ward-stone responds to it, pulsing brighter when she reads, as though it recognizes the quality of her attention. Old blood, yes, but more than that. Old *mind*. The kind of intelligence that looks at mystery and sees not danger but a problem to solve.

This will make things more difficult.

A woman who feared the unknown might be gently guided away from the truth, kept safe in the comfortable fiction that the hum is pipes and the warm stone is geothermal. But Maya Chen does not fear the unknown. She *investigates* the unknown. She has already been back to the basement door three times. Each time, she presses her hand to the stone, tilts her head, and listens. Each time, the ward-stone reaches for her, and I have to physically restrain it — pressing my will against the ancient magic like a hand over a mouth — to keep it from answering.

She cannot know. Not yet. Not like this — a mysterious door that opens to reveal a cavern full of ancient magic and, incidentally, a serpent creature in the grip of a mating bond that has essentially turned his higher cognitive functions into a single sustained note of *want.*

I need a plan.

The plan, such as it is, comes to me on the second evening, while I'm watching her eat cereal from a box. Dry. Without milk. Standing at the kitchen window. She eats it the way a bird eats — quickly, distractedly, her gaze on the valley outside while her hand moves between box and mouth with mechanical indifference.

She has no one. This is the thing that strikes me, the thing that cracks through the roar of the Encanto and touches something older, quieter, deeper. She is alone. Not in the way I am alone — I chose my solitude, or at least I tell myself I did. She has been *left* alone. I hear it in her phone calls — one friend, the woman called Jade, who makes her laugh but is an ocean away. No family calls. No lover's voice. She ended one conversation with "Yeah, Mom, it's fine, I'll send pictures" in a tone that suggested she would rather have her scales pulled out one by one than continue the exchange.

She is alone, and the quinta is crumbling around her, and she eats cereal for dinner because there is no one to cook for and no one to scold her for not cooking.

I know what loneliness looks like. I've worn it for sixty-three years.

The plan crystallizes: I will approach her in human form. A neighbor. Someone who knows the property, who can help with the repairs she obviously needs, who can bridge the gap between her solitary arrival and the truth she is not yet ready to hear. I will be patient. Careful. I will earn her trust before I shatter her understanding of reality.

I spend the night preparing.

Human form requires concentration under the best of circumstances. The body must be constructed precisely — the proportions, the skin tone, the subtle asymmetries that make a face read as real rather than *almost* real, which is worse, because humans are exquisitely attuned to the almost-real. The uncanny valley, they call it. I have crossed it from the wrong direction enough times to know its terrain.

The Encanto complicates everything. My scales want to surface. My eyes want to blaze. My body wants to be *seen* — not the careful human replica, but the truth of me, the emerald and gold, the coiled power, the ancient form that has not been looked upon with anything other than fear in longer than I care to remember.

I wrestle it all down. Layer by layer. Skin over scales. Blunt teeth over fangs. Round pupils over slits. I check my reflection in the underground river and see a man who might pass for Portuguese — tall, sharp-featured, amber eyes that catch the light a little strangely, but nothing that would register as inhuman in broad daylight.

I practice speaking. English — I learned it decades ago from BBC radio broadcasts, then updated it through the thin walls of the quinta when Lúcia watched American television. My accent is an archaeological artifact: British foundation, Portuguese substrate, with occasional flourishes that belong to no era currently occupied. Maya will notice. She notices everything.

I choose clothing from the cache I keep for surface visits — dark trousers, a linen shirt, boots that can pass for modern if no one looks too closely. I look, I decide, like a man who might reasonably live in this village. Perhaps a man who works with his hands. The kind of man a woman alone on a remote property might accept help from without excessive suspicion.

The kind of man who absolutely is not a 287-year-old serpent creature experiencing a mating bond of such intensity that the ground trembles when he thinks about her eating cereal.

Dawn comes. I wait. In sixty-three years, I have become very good at waiting, but this waiting is different. Before, I waited with the patience of stone — endlessly, without expectation, because there was nothing to expect. Now I wait like a held breath. Like the moment before a wave breaks.

She wakes. Coffee. The cafeteira hisses. She sings — the Jolene song again, and I make a mental note to investigate this Jolene person, because Maya seems genuinely distressed about her.

She goes outside. This is my signal.

I climb. Through the passage, out through the gap in the stable wall that I've used for years when I needed to visit the surface. The morning light hits me like a slap — not painful, exactly, but disorienting after decades underground. My skin tingles. My eyes water. The sun is not my enemy, but it is not my friend either; an hour or two of exposure dims my strength in ways that take hours underground to recover.

I circle the property. Approach from the road, as a neighbor would. Walk up the dirt path with my hands in my pockets and my pulse hammering in a way that has nothing to do with the exertion of walking uphill.

She's in the garden. Kneeling in the dirt, her hands black to the wrists, pulling weeds from around the tomato plants with the focused aggression of someone who has found an enemy she can actually fight.

"Bom dia," I say.

She startles. Turns. Looks up at me.

I am not prepared.

The journal drawings, the footsteps through stone, the voice overhead — none of it prepared me for her face, turned toward me, smeared with dirt, squinting in the morning sun with an expression of such immediate and thorough suspicion that I nearly laugh.

"Hi," she says, in a tone that communicates several things simultaneously: *who are you, why are you here, I have a trowel and I'm not afraid to use it.*

"I'm sorry to startle you," I say. "I am Tristão. I live nearby." This is technically true. "I knew your grandmother." Also technically true, in the way that a creature living beneath someone's house for six decades constitutes knowing them. "I wanted to welcome you and offer help. The property — it needs work."

She stands. She's shorter than I expected — the top of her head would reach my collarbone, and in true form, she'd barely reach my sternum. She wipes her hands on her shorts and tilts her chin up to look at me properly, and the Encanto surges so violently that I feel my pupils contract and have to look away, pretending to study the fig tree.

"You knew my grandmother," she says flatly.

"Yes. She was—"

"Everyone in this village apparently knew my grandmother better than I did." She crosses her arms. "Are you the one who left the cheese in the fridge?"

"No," I say honestly. That was probably Celeste, or one of the village women who constituted Lúcia's informal support network. "But I can bring cheese, if that would help."

Something shifts in her expression. Not softening, exactly — more like the suspicion rearranging itself to make room for reluctant amusement. "It wouldn't hurt. Do you know anything about roofs?"

"Some."

"Do you know anything about stone doors that won't open?"

My heart stops. Restarts. "I know many things about stone," I say carefully. "This region is built on granite. The old houses often have features that—"

"Yeah, great, you can workshop the history lecture later. The roof leaks in three places, the bathroom ceiling is actively decomposing, and there's a door in the basement that I'm pretty sure is sealed with something stronger than mortar." She looks at me with those sharp, dark eyes. "Are you actually offering to help, or is this a Portuguese courtesy thing where you offer and I'm supposed to politely decline?"

"I am actually offering."

"Cool." She turns back to the garden. "I'm Maya. Don't steal anything."

She extends her hand for me to shake.

This is the moment. I know it's coming and I still am not prepared, because nothing in 287 years has prepared me for the simple, devastating act of a human woman offering her hand to a monster.

I take it.

Her fingers are warm and dirt-smudged and small in mine, and the instant our skin touches, the Encanto detonates. Heat floods from the point of contact up my arm and into my chest, and I feel my scales surge beneath my skin, pressing against the barrier of human form like something trying to hatch. My vision sharpens. My breath catches.

She feels it too. I see it — the slight widening of her eyes, the way her lips part, the quick inhalation. Her hand tightens in mine, involuntary, and then releases.

"Static shock," she says, shaking her hand. "The dry air, probably."

It is July in the Minho. The air is so humid that mushrooms are growing on the windowsills.

"Probably," I agree.

She nods. Turns away. Goes back to pulling weeds.

I stand in the morning light, my hand burning where she touched it, my blood singing a song I've waited 287 years to hear, and think: *I am in terrible, terrible trouble.*

## Chapter Five
### MAYA

Here's the thing about Tristão: he's wrong.

Not wrong as in incorrect about anything specific — his knowledge of Portuguese construction techniques is frankly alarming in its depth, and his assessment of the roof was accurate down to the individual cracked tile. He's wrong the way a special effect is wrong. Too detailed to be real. Something about him sits slightly off-center in reality, like a photograph that's been shifted two degrees and you can't tell what's different but your brain knows.

He's too tall. Not freakishly — maybe six-four — but he carries it strangely, like his body is a language he's fluent in but not native to. His movements are fluid in a way that bypasses "graceful" and lands somewhere in "uncanny." He never fidgets. He never stumbles. When he tilts his head to listen, there's a quality of absolute stillness to it that reminds me of nature documentaries. Specifically, the part of nature documentaries right before something gets eaten.

His face is beautiful and I hate that I notice this. Angular, severe, the kind of bone structure that makes you wonder if God has a favorite. Warm brown skin. Dark hair that falls slightly too long over eyes that are — and I keep coming back to this — amber. Not brown-amber. *Amber* amber. Like actual fossilized tree resin. When the light catches them at certain angles, the pupils seem to narrow, and then I blink and they're perfectly round and normal and I feel like I'm losing my mind.

He showed up three days ago and hasn't really left.

I didn't ask him to stay. He just... kept appearing. The morning after our introduction, he was at the gate at seven a.m. with a tool bag and an expression of grim purpose. "The bathroom ceiling," he said, as if this explained everything. And then he was in my house, and I was watching a stranger rip out water-damaged plaster with the controlled efficiency of a man who takes structural decay personally.

I should have said no. I should have said "thank you, I'll hire someone," like a reasonable person with appropriate boundaries. But here's the other thing about Tristão: he's useful. Like, absurdly useful. He knows things about this house that I haven't even discovered yet — where the hidden cistern is, which shutters need to be rehung at a specific angle to catch the cross-breeze, that the fig tree drops fruit onto the stable roof every August and you have to clear it or the tiles crack.

"How do you know all this?" I asked on the second day, while he was pointing out a stress fracture in the kitchen wall that I would never have noticed.

"I've lived in this area a long time," he said.

"How long?"

A pause. The kind of pause that's shaped like a decision. "Long enough."

Now it's day four of Tristão, and we've settled into a rhythm that I am choosing not to examine too closely. He arrives in the morning, works on whatever urgent repair he's identified, and leaves in the early afternoon. He brings things — not cheese, as I suggested, but better: a bag of oranges from "a neighbor's tree," bread that's still warm from the padaria in the village, a bottle of olive oil so green it looks like liquid emerald.

He doesn't eat with me. He deflects lunch invitations with a grace that suggests long practice at declining things. He drinks water — never coffee, never wine — and when I asked if he wanted tea, he looked at me with an expression I can only describe as mild existential confusion, as if the concept of tea had briefly destabilized his understanding of the universe.

We talk. Or rather, I talk, and he listens with an intensity that would be flattering if it weren't slightly unnerving. He asks questions — about my work, my life in Boston, my grandmother. The questions are precise, specific, like he's constructing a model of me from data points. When I told him about my PhD research on bioluminescence, he said, "Light-producing organisms," and then went quiet for so long I thought I'd bored him, until he said, "There are things beneath the earth that glow. The deep places have their own light." And the way he said it — not like a fun fact, but like a memory — made the back of my neck prickle.

Today he's grouting the bathroom tiles. I'm in the doorway, eating an orange, watching him work, and trying to figure out what's bothering me.

It's not that he's here. It's that I *want* him here. Three days of a stranger in my house, and his absence in the afternoon feels like a room with the lights turned off. When he's in the house, I'm aware of his location the way you're aware of a fire — not looking at it directly, but feeling its heat. This morning, reaching past him in the kitchen for the coffee, my arm brushed his and I felt a jolt of warmth so acute it made me gasp.

He felt it too. I saw his hand clench around the counter's edge, the knuckles going white, his jaw locking in a way that looked less like surprise and more like agony. Then he stepped away, very deliberately, and said, "Excuse me," in a voice that sounded like it was being strained through gravel.

"Static," I said, for the fourth time this week.

"Of course," he said, for the fourth time this week.

It is not static. I know what static discharge feels like. This felt like grabbing a live wire, if live wires were warm and made your heart beat in your throat.

"You're staring," he says now, from the bathroom floor, without looking up.

"I'm supervising."

"You're eating an orange and watching me grout tile. This is supervision?"

"In America, we call it project management."

He makes a sound that might be a laugh, quickly suppressed. His mouth does something complicated — the corner twitches up, then is forcibly returned to neutral. I'm becoming an expert in the micro-expressions of Tristão's face, the way the control occasionally cracks and something warmer shows through, like light through a door left slightly ajar.

"Tell me about Dona Celeste," I say, because I've been thinking about her since our conversation. "How well did she know my grandmother?"

He pauses, trowel in hand. "Celeste has been here longer than almost anyone. She knows the old stories."

"The Mouros Encantados stories?"

Another pause. "Yes."

"She told me about them. Enchanted serpent people living underground. Pre-Roman, went into hiding during the Christian conquest." I lean against the doorframe. "My grandmother drew them obsessively. The journal is full of serpent imagery, maps of tunnel systems, references to something called 'sangue antigo.' Old blood."

His hand has stopped moving entirely. He's staring at the grout line with the fixed attention of a man trying very hard not to react.

"What do you know about it?" I ask.

"About what?"

"The folklore. The enchanted Mouros. You said you've lived here a long time."

He puts down the trowel. Stands. In the small bathroom, this has the effect of reducing the available space by approximately half — he fills the room the way a storm fills a valley, all contained force and displaced air.

"I know," he says slowly, "that the stories of this region are older than most people realize. And that some stories survive because they are useful, and some survive because they are true, and the difficulty is knowing which is which."

"That's very cryptic."

"I'm told it's a personal failing."

I snort. He almost smiles.

"The church bells," I say, because I've been saving this. "When the bells ring in the village, you flinch."

His expression shutters. "I have sensitive hearing."

"And you never stay past early afternoon. You're always gone before the sun gets high. And yesterday, when I opened the curtains in the kitchen, you moved away from the window so fast I thought there was a wasp."

He is very still. That predatory stillness, the one that makes me think of deep-sea creatures holding their position in a current.

"You're observant," he says.

"I'm a scientist. Observation is literally what I do." I eat the last segment of my orange. "I'm not accusing you of anything. I just notice things."

"What do you notice?"

"That you're not from around here. I mean — you speak Portuguese like it's your first language, and you clearly know this property inside and out. But something about you is... off. I can't explain it. You move differently. You react to things differently. You—" I hesitate, because this is the part that sounds insane. "You feel different. When you're nearby, it's like — I don't know. Like standing near a generator. There's a hum."

The word hangs in the air between us. *Hum.* The same hum from the basement. The same hum from the stone beneath the house.

Something passes behind his eyes — something vast and conflicted and old. Then it's gone, and he's reaching for his tool bag with careful, controlled movements.

"Perhaps the house is affecting you," he says. "Old houses have their own energy. Stone holds memory."

"That's not a scientific statement."

"Not everything is."

He leaves earlier than usual that day. I stand in the doorway and watch him walk down the road, his long stride covering ground with that fluid, inhuman grace, and I think about the way the light caught his eyes when I said the word *hum* — the way the amber seemed to *blaze*, just for an instant, before he looked away.

That night, I sit on the bed with Avó's journal and my laptop and a glass of vinho verde that I'm pretending is a research tool. I search: *Mouros Encantados, Minho, serpent legends, Portuguese folklore, old blood.*

The internet gives me fragments. Wikipedia entries about mythological Mouros who guard treasure under hills. Academic papers on Celtic-Iberian serpent worship. A blog post from a Portuguese folklore enthusiast who writes: *The Mouros Encantados are unique in European mythology in that they are not presented as evil or demonic — they are enchanted, cursed, waiting. Their stories are love stories as often as they are cautionary tales.*

Love stories.

I close the laptop. I drink my wine. I think about amber eyes and warm hands and a man who knows my house better than I do, who flinches at church bells and avoids the sun and speaks about the things beneath the earth as if he's met them personally.

"You're being ridiculous," I say to the empty room. "He's just a weird, hot neighbor."

The house hums beneath me. The stone is warm. And somewhere in the back of my mind, behind the fortress of reason and evidence and peer-reviewed certainty, a small, irrational voice says: *Is he, though?*

## Chapter Six
### TRISTÃO

I am grouting tiles.

Let me be clear about what this means. I am Tristão das Pedras, son of the Rei das Pedras, heir to Casa da Serpente, the oldest and most powerful of the seven noble houses of Sob a Pedra. My bloodline stretches back to before the Romans set foot on this peninsula. I have fought in wars that history has forgotten. I have navigated political intrigues that would reduce a Machiavelli to tears. I have killed a cave bear with my bare hands — my *clawed* hands, admittedly, but the principle stands.

And I am on my hands and knees in a bathroom, grouting tiles, because Maya Chen's shower leaks.

The humiliation is not in the task itself. Manual labor is dignified; the Mouros have never shared the human aristocracy's contempt for craft. The humiliation is in *why* I'm doing it — which is that I cannot think of a better excuse to be near her, and the Encanto has made it physically painful to be far.

I tried. The first afternoon, when I forced myself to leave and descend to the cavern, the bond protested with a ferocity that left me coiled around the ward-stone, scales flickering gold, jaw clenched against a sound that wanted very badly to be a howl. The stone pulsed in sympathy. Or possibly in mockery. After 287 years, I am no longer certain the ward-stone doesn't have a sense of humor.

So I return each morning. I find things to repair. The quinta is generous in this regard — sixty years of minimal maintenance has left a catalog of problems so extensive that I could work here daily for a year and not exhaust them. The bathroom ceiling. The kitchen wiring. The warped shutters. The cracked tiles. Each one a reason to be in her space, to breathe her air, to exist in the impossible luxury of her presence.

She smells like citrus and coffee and, underneath, something that is purely *her* — a warm, clean scent that my hindbrain categorizes as *mate* with a certainty that makes rational thought difficult.

Maintaining human form is becoming harder. Not the physical maintenance — I've worn this body enough to hold it by reflex. It's the Encanto that undermines me. When she passes close, my scales fight to surface. When she laughs — and she laughs often, at herself, at the house, at me — my fangs press against the blunt human teeth that contain them. When she accidentally touches me, which she does with increasing frequency because she is apparently a person who *gestures while talking* and *reaches across people for things* and *once put her hand on my shoulder to lean past me and look at a pipe fitting*, I have to channel every scrap of 287 years of self-discipline into not shifting.

If I shift, the deception ends. She will see what I am. And what I am — in this form, in this context — is a monster who has inserted himself into a vulnerable woman's life because his ancient blood has decided she belongs to him.

This is not how I wanted it.

In the old stories, the Encanto is romantic. The Mouro meets his fated mate, their blood sings in harmony, he reveals his true form, she accepts him, they seal the bond and live beneath the stone in eternal devotion. It's a beautiful narrative. It also, I suspect, was largely written by Mouros who had never actually experienced the Encanto and were romanticizing what is, in practice, an exercise in sustained psychological torment.

Because what the old stories leave out is *this*: the wanting.

Not the desire — though that is present, a constant low fire that I manage through sheer force of will and occasional retreats to the underground river, where the cold water reminds my body that it has functions beyond orienting toward Maya Chen's heartbeat. I mean the wanting that goes deeper. The wanting to be *known*. To shed the careful disguise and stand before her as I am — all of me, scales and coils and ancient history — and have her see it without fear.

She would fear it. She should fear it. I am not the neighbor I pretend to be. I am something out of her grandmother's journal, and if I showed her the truth of that right now, I would lose the tentative, suspicious, beautiful trust she's beginning to extend.

So I grout tiles. I repair shutters. I point out which herbs in the garden are still alive and how to revive them. I am, God help me, *useful*.

She watches me. She thinks I don't notice — she leans in doorways, eats fruit, asks questions with the precision of a woman building a case. She notices things. The church bells. The sunlight. The way I move. She's an investigator by nature, a scientist, and I am the most interesting anomaly in her immediate environment.

I should be more careful. I should moderate my reactions, perform humanity more convincingly. But the Encanto makes subtlety nearly impossible. When the bells ring, the iron in them resonates at a frequency that feels like being struck, and I cannot control the flinch. When the sun hits me directly, my skin protests — not burning, nothing so dramatic, but a thinning of the human façade, a warning from the deep part of me that says *underground, go underground, you don't belong in the light.*

And the touches. Dear stones, the touches.

Yesterday she reached past me for a coffee cup and her arm brushed mine and the Encanto surged like a tide. I felt the scales ripple along my forearms, pressing against human skin from underneath. My vision sharpened — the kitchen suddenly rendered in predator-resolution, every detail crisp, every shadow deep, and Maya, *Maya*, in the center of it all, glowing with old blood like a lantern in the dark.

I gripped the counter so hard the granite creaked.

"Static," she said.

Static. Yes. The mystical resonance of an ancient mating bond that has been woven into the fabric of my being since before her species invented *language* — static. Certainly.

"Of course," I said.

Today she asks me about the church bells, and the sunlight, and the hum, and I feel the lies stack up like stones in a wall, each one adding weight, each one making the inevitable collapse more destructive.

She is so close to the truth. She has the journal. She has Celeste, who knows more than she should and says less than she could. She has her own instincts, which are remarkable — her observation about the *hum*, the way she connected my reactions to the same frequency that emanates from the ward-stone below, was so precisely correct that I nearly told her everything right there in the bathroom.

I didn't. I deflected, offered vague platitudes about old houses and stone memory, and watched the skepticism in her eyes harden into something that looked uncomfortably like disappointment.

She is beginning not to trust me. Not because I've done anything wrong — I have been, by any reasonable measure, an exemplary neighbor — but because she can feel the gap between what I am and what I'm presenting, and the gap insults her intelligence.

I leave earlier than usual. I need distance. I need to think.

The passage to the cavern is cool and dark and *mine* in a way that the surface never is. I shed human form with a relief so profound it's nearly physical pleasure — the scales emerging along my spine and forearms, my lower body elongating, the tail uncurling as vertebrae multiply and muscle masses rearrange. The shift takes seconds. The relief takes longer, rolling through me in waves as the compressed energy of maintaining human form dissipates into the open air of the cavern.

I coil around the ward-stone. Its pulse is steady tonight — stronger than it was before Maya's arrival, which confirms what I already knew: her proximity feeds the ward. The old blood is doing what old blood does. Simply by sleeping above this stone, by living in this house, by pressing her curious, stubborn hands to the basement door, she is strengthening a magic that has held for a thousand years.

And weakening me.

Because every day I spend in her presence, the Encanto deepens. The bond is not a static thing — it grows, feeds on proximity, on shared experience, on the small accumulated intimacies of two people occupying the same space. I know her coffee order now (strong, black, no sugar, the correct way to drink coffee and I will die on this hill). I know she hums when she reads. I know the sound she makes when she discovers something in the journal that excites her — a sharp intake of breath, then a rapid-fire monologue directed at the book as if it can hear her.

I know the way her eyes look in the morning light when she's not guarded yet, before the suspicion settles in — soft and dark and wondering, taking in the valley below the quinta as if she can't quite believe she's here.

I know I would burn cities for that expression.

This is the danger. Not Iago and his political maneuvering, not the weakening wards, not Adamastor stirring in the deep. The danger is that I am falling in love with a woman who does not know what I am, and every day I wait to tell her, the fall gets steeper and the landing gets harder.

The tremor comes at midnight. Small — barely a shiver in the stone, the kind that humans above would attribute to a passing truck. But I feel it in my bones, in the scales pressed against the ward-stone, and I know what it means.

The deep is shifting. The prison beneath the Atlantic is straining against bonds that have held for millennia. The ward-stone — this ward-stone, the one I've dedicated sixty-three years of my existence to protecting — is one of seven that maintains the seal. If it weakens further, the cascade effect could compromise the others.

I need Maya's blood. Not literally — the old blood strengthens the ward through proximity, through touch, through the resonance of her living body with the ancient magic embedded in the stone. But I need her *closer*. I need her to understand what she is, what the quinta is, what lies beneath.

I need her to not run.

Lúcia ran. Not immediately — she stayed for years, kept the house, maintained the proximity. But she never accepted the full truth of what she was. She kept the ward-stone at arm's length, a duty rather than a bond, and in the end, the distance between what she knew and what she was willing to embrace left cracks in the magic that I've been patching for decades.

Maya is different. She has to be different.

She will be different, because I will be honest with her. Not tonight, not tomorrow, but soon. Before the tremors get worse. Before the lies calcify into a wall she can't see past.

I press my forehead to the ward-stone and feel its ancient pulse against my scales — steady, patient, enduring. It has waited a thousand years. It can wait a little longer.

I am less certain about myself.

In the house above, Maya sleeps. I feel her heartbeat through the stone — slow, steady, trusting. She doesn't know I'm here. She doesn't know I can feel her pulse from sixty feet below. She doesn't know that the warmth in the floor that puzzles her each morning is me — my body heat rising through the stone because I spend the nights coiled directly beneath her bedroom like the world's most pathetic supernatural heating system.

The Encanto hums. The ward-stone hums. Maya's heartbeat hums.

And I — heir to the oldest house, guardian of the oldest stone, the most powerful Mouro in my generation — lie in the dark and think about the way she eats oranges, methodically, section by section, juice on her chin, and I know, with a certainty that predates language, that I am doomed.

## Chapter Seven
### MAYA

I'm starting to think my grandmother was onto something.

Not the "enchanted serpent people living under the hills" something — I haven't completely lost my grip on reality, thank you — but the "there's more going on here than meets the eye" something. The journal is extensive. Avó wasn't doodling; she was documenting. And the more I read, the more I realize this wasn't a casual hobby.

It was a life's work.

I've spread the journal across the kitchen table alongside my laptop, a map of the region, and a bottle of vinho verde that I'm pretending is optional. It's day eight at the quinta, and I've fallen into the kind of hyperfocused research spiral that my PhD advisor used to call "productive mania" and my ex used to call "you're doing it again."

The journal entries span decades — the earliest dated 1976, the year Avó first returned to Portugal, and the most recent just three years ago. The early entries are tentative, almost embarrassed: *The old people in the village speak of encantados. Enchanted ones beneath the hills. I think it's folklore, but the ward-stone pulses when I touch it, and I don't have an explanation for that.*

Ward-stone. She names it explicitly. Not "the stone in the basement" or "the door I can't open." *Ward-stone.* As if she knew exactly what it was.

By the 1980s, the entries are more confident. She's mapped the tunnel system beneath the quinta in meticulous cross-section, labeling chambers and passages with names I don't recognize — *A Sala do Eco* (The Echo Chamber), *O Rio Prateado* (The Silver River), *A Guarda* (The Ward). Her serpent drawings have evolved from sketches into detailed anatomical studies — scales rendered with a naturalist's precision, measurements noted in the margins, alongside a lexicon of terms in a language that isn't Portuguese.

The language. This is what keeps pulling me in. It appears in scattered entries throughout the journal — angular symbols, sharp consonants, nothing I can match to any known European script. I've tried: Latin, Celtiberian, Lusitanian, even proto-Celtic. Nothing fits. Avó has provided translations for some terms:

*Encanto* — the bond. The resonance between two matched souls. (Her translation, not mine. I would never unironically write "matched souls.")

*Sangue antigo* — old blood. The bloodline that connects certain humans to the encantados.

*Sob a Pedra* — beneath the stone. The hidden place.

*O encantado* — the enchanted one. Singular. As in: there is one. Under this house.

I read that entry three times and then pour more wine.

The most disturbing section is near the middle of the journal, dated 1989. Avó writes:

*He guards the stone. I don't see him — that was our agreement. But I feel him. The warmth in the floor. The hum at night. Sometimes I hear him move — a sound like scales on granite, very far below. He is patient. He is waiting. I think he has been waiting for a very long time.*

*I asked Celeste what happens when the waiting ends. She said: "You'll know. The blood will know." She wouldn't say more.*

*I think the old blood is mine. I think it was always mine. And I think the reason my mother left Portugal, and her mother before her, is because the blood calls you back to the stone and some women are too afraid to answer.*

*I'm not afraid. But I'm not brave enough either. Not for what the stone is asking.*

*Maybe the next one will be.*

I put the journal down and sit in the silence of the kitchen for a long time.

The next one. She meant me. She meant the entry in her will, the handwritten line: *Come see what I kept for you.* She kept the house. She kept the stone. She kept whatever's underneath.

And she left it all to me.

I'm processing this — or more accurately, failing to process this, because my brain keeps returning a 404 error every time I try to reconcile "my grandmother's journal describes a creature living beneath this house" with "I am a scientist and this is not how reality works" — when Tristão knocks on the front door.

He's been coming every day. I should put a stop to it. I should say, "Thank you for your help, I'll hire a contractor, please stop being in my house." But I don't, because I'm lonely and he's useful and also because — and I hate admitting this — I like him. Not just the obvious way, although let's not pretend the man isn't offensively attractive in a way that feels almost aggressive, like beauty as a weapon. I like the way he talks. Careful, precise, with a dry humor that surfaces unexpectedly, like a fish jumping. I like the way he listens — completely, with his whole body, as if my words are a substance he can absorb through his skin.

I like the way he makes the house feel less empty.

But I also don't trust him, and the distrust is growing at the same rate as the attraction, which is creating a deeply uncomfortable internal weather system.

"Good morning," he says, and I notice immediately that he looks worse than yesterday. Not unwell — he's too perfect for that, in the uncanny way that everything about him is too perfect — but strained. There are shadows under his amber eyes, and he holds himself with a rigidity that suggests something hurts.

"You look terrible," I say.

"Thank you."

"Seriously. Did you sleep?"

A pause. "Sleep and I have a complicated relationship."

I let him in because this is what I do now, apparently — let the mysterious handsome stranger into my dead grandmother's house so he can fix things and be weird. He goes straight to the kitchen and begins examining the wiring behind the stove, which he identified yesterday as a fire hazard.

I watch him from the table, journal open in front of me.

"Tristão."

"Yes?"

"What does 'encantado' mean to you?"

His hands go still inside the wall. "Enchanted. It's a common word."

"In folklore. What does it mean in the context of the folklore? The Mouros Encantados?"

He withdraws from the wall slowly. Turns to face me. His expression is very controlled — so controlled it's almost a confession, because nobody controls their face that carefully unless there's something behind it that needs controlling.

"In the old stories," he says, "the encantados are beings who were enchanted — cursed, some say — to live beneath the earth. They can take human form, but their true nature is something else. Something older."

"Serpentine."

"Yes."

"And the 'encanto' — the bond — is what, exactly?"

"A resonance. Between an encantado and a human with the old blood. The stories describe it as... recognition. The blood of one calls to the blood of the other."

"Sounds like a mating call."

Something flickers in his eyes — gold, bright, there and gone. "That is one interpretation."

"My grandmother wrote about this," I say, pushing the journal toward him. "Extensively. She wrote about a ward-stone under this house. She wrote about something guarding it. She wrote that the old blood is in our family."

He doesn't look at the journal. He's looking at me, and his expression has shifted into something I can't read — open and closed at the same time, like a door held ajar by a chain.

"Maya—"

"I'm not saying I believe in enchanted serpent people," I say quickly. "I'm a biologist. I believe in things I can measure, observe, and replicate. But *something* is under this house. Something is humming. Something makes the floor warm. And you" — I point at him — "are not a normal neighbor."

The silence is so thick I could spread it on bread.

"You avoid sunlight," I continue, counting on my fingers. "Church bells hurt you. You know this property better than someone who lives 'nearby' should. You move like nothing I've ever seen — and I've studied marine predators, so my baseline for 'weird movement patterns' is already pretty high. When we touch, there's a—" I falter. "A reaction. That is not static electricity."

He's breathing carefully. In and out, measured, like someone managing pain.

"What are you?" I ask.

The question hangs in the air. He opens his mouth. Closes it. His hands, hanging at his sides, clench and release.

"I am your neighbor," he says quietly. "And I am someone who knew your grandmother. And I promise you, Maya, that when the time comes, I will tell you everything. But I am asking you — *please* — to give me a little more time."

It's the *please* that gets me. He says it like the word costs him something. Like asking for things is a skill he hasn't practiced in a very long time.

"How much time?" I ask.

"Days. Not weeks."

"And you'll explain everything? The hum, the door, the—" I gesture vaguely at his entire person. "All of it?"

"All of it."

I should push. Every instinct says push — he's hiding something, it's probably important, and I've been burned too many times by men who say "trust me" when what they mean is "don't ask questions." But there's something in his face that isn't deception. It's closer to dread. He looks like a man standing at the edge of a cliff, trying to calculate the distance to the bottom.

"Fine," I say. "Days. But I want a straight answer to one question right now."

"Ask."

"The thing under the house. The hum. Is it dangerous?"

He considers this with the weight of someone who takes the question seriously. "Not to you," he says finally. "Nothing under this house would ever harm you."

The way he says *nothing under this house* — with a specificity that feels personal, protective, almost possessive — sends a shiver down my spine that has absolutely nothing to do with fear.

"Okay," I say. "Days."

He nods. Returns to the wiring. And I sit at the table with my grandmother's journal and my wine and my increasingly untenable theory that the most interesting thing in this village is just a handsome guy who's good with his hands.

The church bell rings at noon. He flinches — a full-body twitch, quickly suppressed, but not quickly enough. And in the instant of the flinch, I see something impossible: a ripple along his forearm, as if the skin itself is shifting, and underneath, for just a fraction of a second, a flash of green.

I blink. It's gone. His arm is normal — brown skin, dark hair, human.

"Sensitive hearing," he says, without looking at me.

"Uh-huh," I say.

And I think: *Days, Tristão. You've got days.*

## Chapter Eight
### TRISTÃO

The ward-stone is failing.

Not dramatically — there is no cracking, no visible decay, no sudden darkening of the light that has pulsed from its core for a millennium. It is a subtler diminishment, the kind that would be imperceptible to anyone who hasn't spent sixty-three years in its company. But I have, and I feel it the way you feel the seasons — not as a single moment of change but as a gradual, irreversible shift in the quality of things.

The pulse is slower. The light is dimmer. The resonance, which once filled the cavern with a steady harmonic that vibrated in my chest like a second heartbeat, has thinned to something fragile. Where once I could feel the ward's influence extending outward — through the granite, the soil, the roots of the fig tree, the foundation of the quinta — now it barely reaches the cavern walls.

And the tremors are increasing.

Three in the past week. Small, each one, barely enough to rattle a glass on a shelf. But the source is deep — not the normal tectonic adjustments of the Iberian plate, which I know as well as I know my own coils, but something *beneath* the plate. Something pressing upward. Something testing the barrier between its prison and the world.

Adamastor.

I do not use the name lightly. Among my kind, it is spoken with the weight of other species' apocalypse myths, because that is precisely what it is. The Adamastor is not a metaphor. It is not a cautionary tale. It is a being of such magnitude that the Mouros of antiquity — a civilization that reshaped the underground world to their will — could not destroy it. Only contain it. Seven ward-stones, seven seals, seven bloodlines bound to the stone by magic older than language.

My stone. My bloodline. My responsibility.

And it is failing because I have been alone too long, and the old blood that feeds it has been absent too long, and the world above has forgotten what lies beneath.

Maya is helping. She doesn't know it — she doesn't know that simply sleeping above the stone strengthens it, that her heartbeat resonating through sixty feet of granite reaches the ward-stone and brightens its pulse, that the blood in her veins carries a frequency that matches the stone's own harmonic and reinforces it the way a voice added to a choir fills the space between notes.

But she is one woman, unaware, and the ward-stone needs more than passive proximity. It needs active engagement — the old blood pressed to the stone with intent, the resonance amplified by will. It needs a keeper who knows what she is and chooses to serve.

It needs Maya to choose, and she cannot choose what she does not know.

I am running out of time to be gentle.

The fourth tremor comes while I'm in the cavern, inspecting the northern fault line that has been my primary concern for the past decade. It's stronger than the others — not by much, but enough that dust sifts from the ceiling and the underground river shivers. I press my hands to the cavern wall and feel the vibration in my scales, my bones, the deep architecture of my body that was designed for this — for reading the earth, for interpreting the language of stone and seismic shift.

The language is clear: the ward is thinning. The prison below is straining. Whatever is down there is not asleep anymore.

I have weeks, perhaps. A month, if the fault line holds. After that, the ward-stone's sphere of influence will contract to the stone itself, and the cascading effect on the other six wards will begin, and the Rei das Pedras — my father, fading in the deep halls of Sob a Pedra — will know that his son has failed in the only task that mattered.

I cannot let that happen.

I climb to the passage beneath the quinta and press my ear to the stone. Above me, Maya is reading. I can hear the turn of pages, the occasional scratch of a pen — she's been annotating the journal, cross-referencing it with things she finds on her computer, building a framework of understanding that approaches the truth from every angle except the one that matters: belief.

She is so close. The conversation today — when she confronted me with her observations, her data, her meticulously assembled case that I am not what I claim to be — was simultaneously the most terrifying and the most exhilarating exchange I've had in decades. She sees me. Not the human mask, not the helpful neighbor — *me*, the anomaly, the thing that doesn't fit. And rather than looking away, as most humans would, she leans in.

I asked for days. She gave them. But the days I asked for are borrowed against a debt the earth itself is calling due, and the price of patience is rising.

The fifth tremor comes at midnight.

This one is not small. The cavern shakes. Stalactites crack. The underground river surges, white-capped and sudden, before settling. The ward-stone flares — bright, desperate — and I feel the resonance waver like a plucked string about to snap.

I wrap myself around the stone. Coils tight, scales pressed flat, every ounce of my own power channeled into the ward. This is what I've been doing for sixty-three years — supplementing the stone's waning strength with my own life force, a stopgap measure that was never meant to be permanent. It's like plugging a dam with your body. It works until it doesn't.

The tremor subsides. The stone steadies. But the light is dimmer than before, and I can feel the cost in my own bones — a weariness that runs deeper than physical exhaustion, a thinning of the reserves I've been burning for decades.

I'm not enough. I was never enough alone. The ward-stone was designed to be tended by a bonded pair — Mouro and old blood, together, their combined resonance feeding the stone in a loop of mutual reinforcement. For sixty-three years, I've been running this system on half power. It's a testament to the ancients' engineering that it's lasted this long.

It will not last much longer.

I need to tell Maya. Not in days — tomorrow. I need to show her the ward-stone, explain the old blood, reveal myself. I need to do it in a way that doesn't send her running back to Boston on the first available flight, because if she runs, the ward fails, and if the ward fails—

I cannot think about that. Focus on the immediate problem. The human problem. The problem of a woman who requires evidence, who processes the world through the framework of science and reason, who will look at a 287-year-old serpent creature and demand to see peer-reviewed studies.

I almost smile. Even now, even with the earth groaning beneath me and the ward-stone flickering like a candle in a draft, the thought of Maya's reaction makes something warm and desperate bloom in my chest.

She'll be furious. She'll be terrified. She'll throw something at me — she has a particular relationship with thrown objects that I've observed and cataloged with wary admiration. And then, if I'm fortunate, if the old blood in her is as strong as I believe, she'll stop throwing things and start asking questions.

I can answer questions. I have been preparing answers for a thousand years.

The night passes. I do not sleep — sleep has become an unreliable ally since the Encanto, my body too attuned to Maya's rhythms to rest when she rests, too alert when she stirs. I feel her turn in her sleep. I feel the moment she wakes, gasping, from what I suspect is another dream of golden eyes.

The dreams are the Encanto's doing. The bond reaches across the stone between us and shows her fragments of me — the real me, the form I'm hiding, the truth her blood already recognizes. She'll be dreaming of scales and warm coils and a darkness that isn't darkness but the particular quality of light that exists underground, where stone glows and water shines and the world is not dark at all, merely lit by different suns.

She doesn't understand the dreams yet. She will.

Dawn comes. I prepare myself as I have every morning — the careful construction of a human body, the compression of scales into skin, the effort of will that makes me less so that I can be near her without being myself. But today it feels different. Today it feels like the last time.

Because tomorrow, one way or another, Maya Chen will see what I am.

The earth trembles beneath me, a reminder. The ward-stone pulses, fading.

I am out of time.

## Chapter Nine
### MAYA

The earthquake wakes me at 4:17 a.m.

I know the exact time because I'm staring at my phone when it happens — insomnia, courtesy of yet another dream about golden eyes and warm scales and a voice saying my name in a language that doesn't exist. I've been lying in bed trying to convince myself that the dreams are stress-related, that the perfectly reasonable explanation for dreaming about a serpent creature every single night for a week is "you're reading too much of your grandmother's weird journal before bed, you absolute walnut," when the house shakes.

Not a big shake. Not a disaster-movie, things-falling-off-shelves shake. More like the house takes a deep breath and shudders on the exhale. The bed vibrates. The reading glasses on the nightstand rattle. The terracotta tile under my feet gives a single, pronounced *thrum* — felt more than heard, a bass note that resonates in my teeth and the base of my skull.

And then, from below, a sound. Not the usual hum. A *crack*. Like stone breaking.

I'm out of bed before I've consciously decided to move. Bare feet on warm tile, phone flashlight on, down the stairs to the kitchen, across to the basement steps. I'm aware, in the back of my mind, that this is the beginning of every horror movie I've ever yelled at — *don't go in the basement, don't go in the basement* — but I am a scientist, and the sound came from below, and whatever's been humming under this house for the past nine days has just changed pitch.

Something is different.

The basement stairs are cold for the first time — the perpetual warmth I've felt since arriving is gone, replaced by a chill that raises goosebumps on my arms. The air smells different too: sharper, more mineral, like the moment before lightning strikes.

My flashlight sweeps the familiar space — wine racks, crates, preserves — and finds the stone door.

The door is cracked.

Not open. Cracked. A fissure runs from the top left corner to the bottom right, a jagged line that wasn't there yesterday, and through it — through the impossible, immovable, *refusing* door — light. Faint, blue-white, pulsing. The same rhythm as the hum, but visible now. A heartbeat of light.

"Okay," I whisper. "Okay. That's new."

The scientist in me is screaming *bioluminescence, geologic phosphorescence, naturally occurring mineral light, there is an explanation for this.* The rest of me — the part that's been dreaming of golden eyes, that felt the jolt when Tristão touched my hand, that heard the hum on her first night and pressed her palm to warm stone — the rest of me is saying: *you already know.*

I don't know. That's the thing. I don't *know*. I have data points, observations, hypotheses — I have my grandmother's journal full of serpent drawings and a stranger who flinches at church bells and a hum that's been vibrating in my bones since I walked through the front door of this house. But I don't have *knowledge*. Knowledge is confirmed, verified, reproducible. Knowledge is what you get when you stop being afraid of the answer and ask the question.

I put my hand on the cracked door and push.

It gives. Not like a stuck door finally moving — like a held breath finally released. The stone slides with a grinding, tectonic reluctance, as if it's doing this under protest, and the crack widens into a gap, and the gap widens into an opening, and the blue-white light floods through and hits me like walking into the sun.

Behind the door: stairs. Not the rough-cut basement steps but carved stairs, spiraling downward into the earth, each step worn smooth by... by what? Time? Feet? Something else entirely? The walls are granite, but the granite is *wrong* — patterned with the same serpentine carvings from Avó's journal, and the carvings are glowing. Not lit from outside — glowing from within, as if the stone itself is luminous.

"Bioluminescence," I say out loud, because saying it makes it feel more manageable. "Bioluminescent minerals. It's rare but documented. This is not magic. This is geology."

The stairs spiral down. I follow them. I'm wearing pajama shorts and a Boston College T-shirt and no shoes, and I am descending into an unknown geological formation beneath my dead grandmother's house at four in the morning, and if this is how I die, Jade is going to kill me.

The descent is longer than it should be. The quinta sits on a hillside, and I'd estimate the basement is maybe three meters below ground level. The stairs take me at least ten meters down, maybe more. The air changes as I descend — warmer, damper, with a mineral sweetness that reminds me of deep-ocean vents, that particular quality of air that's been processed through stone and water for a very long time.

The hum is everywhere now. Not below me or around me — in me. Vibrating in my ribs, my teeth, the small bones of my inner ear. It's the frequency I've been hearing since I arrived, but amplified, clarified, as if I've been listening to a radio through a wall and now I've walked into the room with the speakers.

The stairs end. I step into—

A cavern.

I stop breathing.

The cavern is vast. That's the first thing — the ceiling arches thirty feet overhead, a natural dome of granite ribbed with veins of quartz that catch the blue-white light and scatter it like stars. An underground river runs along the far wall, its water so clear it looks like liquid glass, and the light reflecting off its surface casts rippling patterns across the stone ceiling that make the whole space look like the inside of a living thing. A heartbeat of light and water and stone.

And in the center, on a natural pedestal of granite, the ward-stone.

I know what it is before I consciously identify it. My grandmother's drawings didn't do it justice. It's about four feet tall, roughly cylindrical, the surface covered in carvings so dense they look like text — spiraling patterns, serpentine forms, symbols in the same angular script from Avó's journal. The stone itself is dark granite, but the carvings glow with a blue-white light that pulses in rhythm with the hum, and the pulse is — I'm not making this up — syncing with my heartbeat.

Or my heartbeat is syncing with it. I can't tell which came first.

"Oh," I say. It's not adequate, but it's what I have.

The marine biologist in me is going absolutely feral. The light patterns suggest bioluminescent organisms embedded in the mineral structure — not impossible, not unprecedented, there are deep-cave ecosystems on every continent that harbor extremophile organisms producing light through chemiluminescent reactions. The river could support an entire ecosystem. The mineral composition of the stone could—

But that's not what this is.

I know that's not what this is.

I walk toward the ward-stone. My bare feet are warm on the cavern floor, and with each step, the hum intensifies. Not louder — *closer*. Like the source of the hum is matching its frequency to mine, or mine to it, a mutual tuning that pulls us into resonance.

My hand rises. I don't decide to raise it. My body moves toward the stone the way iron moves toward a magnet — not by choice but by nature. The old blood. The *sangue antigo.* My grandmother wrote about it, and I thought it was folklore, and it's not folklore. It's in my veins. It's singing.

I touch the ward-stone.

Light.

Not the blue-white pulse — something else entirely. Gold. Bright, burning, living gold that erupts from the point of contact and floods through me like electricity, like hot water, like the jolt I felt when Tristão touched my hand but magnified a thousandfold. It's in my blood. It's in my bones. It's in the architecture of my cells, rearranging something fundamental, waking something that was always there but sleeping.

The hum becomes a roar. The cavern trembles. The underground river surges, white-capped, luminous. The carvings on the ward-stone blaze, and for one impossible moment, I can *read* them — not the language, but the intention. Ward. Protect. Hold. Seal. Keep. A thousand years of desperate, beautiful purpose, distilled into stone and light and a magic that my rational mind is screaming cannot exist even as my blood says *yes, yes, I know you, I've always known you.*

My grandmother's voice, from the journal: *The blood remembers.*

The blood remembers.

And then the light is too much. It floods my vision, my nervous system, the circuits of consciousness that keep a person upright and functioning. I feel my knees buckle. I feel the cavern floor rush up. I feel, in the last instant before the golden light swallows everything—

Arms.

Someone catches me. Strong arms, impossibly warm, and against the bare skin of my arms I feel something that is not skin — something smooth and hard and scaled, like armor, like the belly of a snake, and it's so warm, and the hum is a heartbeat, and the heartbeat is his.

I know it's his.

"Tristão," I say, or think I say, as the gold takes everything.

And then there's nothing but warmth and the hum and the dark.

## Chapter Ten
### TRISTÃO

I catch her.

Of course I catch her. I have been beneath this house for sixty-three years, waiting for precisely this — not this exact scenario, which involves a woman in sleeping clothes touching the ward-stone at four in the morning and channeling enough old-blood resonance to light up the cavern like a sun, but this in the broader sense. The moment the old blood returns to the stone. The moment the ward takes notice. The moment everything changes.

I wasn't expecting her to find the cavern tonight. I was going to tell her tomorrow — I'd made the decision, prepared the words, rehearsed the approach with the meticulous care of a man who knows he gets one chance. But the tremor at midnight cracked the basement door, and the door was never going to hold against her once it showed a gap, because Maya Chen does not leave gaps unexamined.

So I was already in the cavern when I felt her on the stairs. The ward-stone felt her first — it blazed, brightening to a luminosity I haven't seen in decades, the old magic surging toward her blood the way a river surges toward the sea. I shifted to my true form involuntarily — scales erupting, coils unfurling, my body responding to the combined resonance of ward-stone and old blood and Encanto with a surrender of control so complete I didn't even fight it.

I watched from the shadows of the cavern's edge as she entered. As she saw the ward-stone. As her face — half-illuminated by the blue-white light, bare feet on stone, her hair loose and dark around her shoulders — shifted from shock to wonder to the expression I've come to love above all others: the scientist's face. The face that says *I don't understand this but I am going to.*

And then she touched the stone, and the world went gold.

The ward-stone has not responded like this in my lifetime. The resonance she triggered — the old blood meeting the ancient magic, the circuit completing for the first time in over a century — was so powerful that I felt it in my own blood, the Encanto amplifying the ward's surge until my scales blazed gold and the cavern trembled and for one searing instant, I felt *her*. Not just the heartbeat I've been tracking through sixty feet of stone. *Her.* The architecture of her mind — sharp, skeptical, hungry for truth. The warmth of her body. The fear and wonder braided together in her chest. The bone-deep recognition that something she'd been searching for without knowing it was searching back.

She said my name as she fell.

She *knew.* In that last instant of consciousness, with the old blood alight in her veins and the ward-stone's magic flooding through her neural pathways and the last shred of rationality dissolving in a tide of ancient resonance — she knew it was me. Not the human mask. Me.

I caught her before she hit the ground. Bare arms against my scales, her body limp and warm and impossibly small in my coils. Because I am in my true form — I couldn't shift back if I tried, not with the Encanto roaring and the ward-stone blazing and her skin against my scales — and my true form is not the careful six-foot-four human I've been wearing like a costume. It's eight feet of serpentine power, the coil alone twice her length, and the woman I'm holding weighs nothing to me, nothing at all, a warm and fragile weight that I would guard against the collapse of the earth itself.

The ward-stone's light subsides to a steady, strong pulse. Stronger than it's been in years. Stronger than I've felt it since Lúcia was young and vital and pressed her hands to the stone on the solstice as her grandmother taught her.

Maya's heartbeat is steady. I check it three times — once with my hand on her wrist, once with my ear against her chest, once through the Encanto, which provides a level of biometric data that I suspect would make her science brain very excited if she were conscious to process it. Her breathing is deep and even. Her temperature is elevated — a degree, maybe two — but not dangerously so. The old blood activation raises body temperature temporarily. I know this from the texts.

She's fine.

She's unconscious, barefoot, lying in the coils of a serpent creature in a cave beneath her house, and she's *fine*. She touched the ward-stone and survived. More than survived — she activated a resonance that the stone hasn't produced in over a century.

Strong old blood. Stronger than Lúcia's. Stronger than anyone I've encountered.

The blood remembers, Lúcia wrote, and she was right.

I carry her upstairs. The passage from the cavern to the basement is narrow for my true form — I compress the coils, move sideways, scraping scales against granite with a sound that I usually find comforting and now find merely embarrassing, because the woman in my arms might wake at any moment and the first thing she'll see is a giant serpent attempting to navigate a staircase.

In the basement, I force the shift. It's agonizing — the Encanto protests, the old magic protests, every instinct screams to stay in my true form, to let her see, to be *known*. But she's unconscious. She didn't choose this. When she sees me — if she lets me show her — it needs to be her choice, conscious and clear-eyed, the way she does everything.

Human form assembles around me like armor. Skin over scales. The compression of coils into legs, which after 287 years still feels like folding a river into a glass. I adjust. I settle. I'm Tristão again — the neighbor, the helpful stranger, the lie that's served its purpose and is about to be retired.

I carry her up the stairs, through the kitchen, to the bedroom. Lay her on the bed. Pull the crocheted blanket — one of the dozens her grandmother made, because Lúcia expressed anxiety through textile production — over her body.

And then I sit by the window and wait.

The moonlight comes in at an angle that catches my eyes, and I know — if she were to wake now and look at me — that they wouldn't look human. The Encanto has stripped my control to its foundations. My pupils are slit. The amber blazes. I can feel my scales pressing against the skin of my forearms, visible as a faint pattern if the light were brighter. The disguise is thinner than it's ever been.

Because the disguise is almost done.

Below us, the ward-stone pulses — strong, steady, brighter than it's been in decades. Maya's touch recharged it. The tremors have stopped — for now. The deep prison holds. One touch, one moment of old-blood contact, has bought us time that I'd been losing for years.

But it's temporary. The ward-stone needs sustained engagement — regular contact, intentional resonance, a keeper who knows what she is and feeds the magic with purpose. One unconscious touch in the middle of the night is a bandage on a wound that needs surgery.

I need her to understand. All of it. The ward-stone, the old blood, the Mouros, the thing beneath the Atlantic that her bloodline has been helping contain since before her family had a surname. I need her to understand that the quinta is not just a house — it's a keystone, and she's not just an inheritor — she's a guardian.

And I need her to understand me.

Not the helpful neighbor. Not the lie I've worn for a week like an ill-fitting suit. Me. Tristão das Pedras, heir to Casa da Serpente, 287 years old, serpentine and scaled and ancient and terrified — not of the dark or the deep or the thing stirring beneath the ocean, but of a twenty-eight-year-old marine biologist from Boston who eats cereal for dinner and argues with furniture and has, in nine days, dismantled the careful isolation I spent sixty-three years constructing.

She moves in her sleep. Turns toward me. Her hand slides across the pillow, reaching for something that isn't there, and her fingers flex — open, close, open — as if grasping at a dream.

"Tristão," she murmurs.

My chest constricts. The Encanto surges, warm and fierce, and I grip the arms of the chair to keep myself from going to her, from taking her hand, from pressing my forehead to hers and letting the bond show her everything — every year of waiting, every night of listening to her grandmother's footsteps above, every hour of the past week when her voice or her laugh or the particular way she tilts her head when she's thinking made the centuries before her feel like a single long intake of breath.

Saudade. The word my people use for the longing that has no cure. I have felt saudade for 287 years — for a mate I'd never met, for a life I'd never lived, for a version of myself that existed in relation to another person rather than in isolation from everything. And now the saudade has a face. It has a name. It sleeps three feet away from me, and I cannot touch it, because it hasn't chosen me yet.

The moon moves. An hour passes. Two. The light shifts from silver to the deep blue of predawn, and I watch the shadows change on her face and think about all the ways this can end.

She can run. People do — Lúcia did, for twenty years, before coming back. The old blood pulls, but it doesn't compel. Maya is stubborn enough to resist anything, including her own nature.

She can stay and reject me. Accept the ward-stone, accept the old blood, but refuse the Encanto, refuse the mating bond, refuse the serpent creature who's been lying to her since the moment they met. I would survive this — technically. The Sem Luz, the Without Light, the slow diminishment of an unmatched Mouro. I would survive and I would continue to guard the stone and I would do it in a darkness that made the previous sixty-three years look like a summer afternoon.

Or she can choose. Consciously, deliberately, the way she does everything — with data and analysis and probably several pointed questions and at least one thrown object. She can look at what I am and decide it's not a monster, or decide it is and want it anyway.

I have no right to expect this. No right to ask for it. The Encanto is not a contract — it's an offer, written in blood and magic, and the offer can be declined.

But dear stones, I want her to accept.

A tremor rolls through the house. Not from below — from the deep. Distant, but clear. The ward-stone, newly charged, absorbs most of the vibration, but I feel the remainder in my bones, a reminder that the thing beneath the Atlantic is not sleeping, merely pausing.

Time is not on my side. It never has been, but the pretense of patience has become impossible to maintain. The basement door is open. The cavern is accessible. Maya touched the ward-stone and survived, and when she wakes, she will have questions that I cannot answer with evasions and half-truths and vague promises about *days*.

The days are up.

She stirs. The predawn light catches her face — soft, unguarded, the wariness stripped away by sleep. Her eyelids flutter. Her breath changes rhythm.

I stay in the chair. I keep my hands on the armrests. I hold the human form as steady as I can, even though the scales are pressing against my skin like something trying to hatch, even though my eyes are burning gold in the dim room, even though every cell in my ancient body is screaming *she is waking, she is here, she is YOURS—*

She is not mine. Not yet. Perhaps not ever. That is not my choice to make.

She opens her eyes.

She sees me.

And in the blue-gray light before dawn, sitting by the window of her grandmother's bedroom in a house built above a world she doesn't know, Maya Chen looks at me — really looks, with those sharp dark eyes that miss nothing, that categorize and analyze and refuse to accept anything less than truth — and I see the moment she registers that my eyes are wrong. Gold. Slit-pupiled. Inhuman.

"So," she says, her voice rough with sleep and the aftermath of channeling a millennium of ancient magic through her unshielded nervous system. "I think we need to have that conversation now."

"Yes," I say. "We do."

She sits up. Pulls the blanket around her shoulders. Looks at me the way she looks at everything — as a problem to be solved, a specimen to be studied, a truth to be extracted.

"Start from the beginning," she says.

And because I have been waiting 287 years for someone to ask, I do.

I tell her about the Mouros. The old civilization, the curse, the retreat underground. I tell her about Sob a Pedra — the hidden kingdom, the seven houses, the bioluminescent caverns and silver rivers and a world beneath the world that has endured for a thousand years. I tell her about the ward-stones — seven seals maintaining a prison beneath the Atlantic, containing something ancient and terrible, something that her bloodline has been helping to contain since before her family knew they carried the old blood.

I tell her about her grandmother. The agreement. The vigil. Sixty-three years of silence below while Lúcia tended the house above.

I tell her about the old blood in her veins — the *sangue antigo*, the genetic inheritance that connects her to the ward-stone, that allowed her to touch it tonight and survive, that has been pulling her toward this house, this land, this stone since the moment it was in her blood.

I do not tell her about the Encanto. One crisis at a time.

She listens. She doesn't interrupt — which I was not expecting, because Maya interrupts everything and everyone, including, on one memorable occasion, a ringing telephone. She listens with the focused stillness of a woman holding each piece of information at arm's length, examining it, deciding whether it fits.

When I finish, the room is quiet. Dawn has arrived while I was speaking — pink light on the granite walls, birdsong through the open window, the distant sound of Dona Celeste's rooster declaring sovereignty over the morning.

"Show me," Maya says.

"Show you what?"

"What you are."

The words land in my chest like a stone. "Maya—"

"You said 'the beginning.' This is the beginning. You said the Mouros can shift between forms. You're one of them." She pulls the blanket tighter, and I see her hands trembling — the only sign of fear she'll permit herself. "I touched the ward-stone. I passed out. I woke up in my bed with you sitting by the window with snake eyes. I'm not stupid, Tristão. I've been putting this together for a week. Now show me."

I stand. The chair scrapes back. My heart — my ancient, patient, terrified heart — beats so hard I can feel it in my teeth.

"It will be... alarming," I say.

"I'm already alarmed. Rip the Band-Aid."

I have no idea what this expression means, but the intent is clear.

I let go.

The shift is not gradual. It has been held back too long, compressed by will and necessity and the desperate hope that she'd never have to see it under these conditions — in a bedroom, at dawn, with no preparation and no context beyond a story she has every reason to disbelieve. The human form doesn't dissolve; it *fractures*, like ice breaking on a river, and what emerges is—

Me.

Eight feet of scaled serpentine power. My lower body extends from the waist into the coil — twelve feet of emerald and gold, the scales catching the dawn light in a way that makes them look almost metallic. My upper body remains humanoid but larger, broader, the scales running along my spine and forearms and ribs in patterns that echo the carvings on the ward-stone. My hands are clawed. My eyes blaze gold. My teeth—

She screams.

Fair. Entirely fair. The sound rips through the morning quiet, and the rooster downstairs responds with an indignant crow, and for one terrible instant, I think she's going to run. I watch her scramble back on the bed until she hits the headboard, eyes wide, chest heaving, hands gripping the crocheted blanket like a lifeline.

I don't move. I don't speak. I coil myself as small as I can — which is still enormous in this bedroom, my tail curling against the far wall, my head nearly touching the ceiling — and I wait.

She stares at me. I stare at her. The dawn light falls between us like a curtain, pink and gold, and in it, her face cycles through every emotion I feared: terror, disbelief, something close to grief. And then — and this is the moment I will remember if I live another 287 years — her eyes narrow. Her chin lifts. The scientist surfaces through the shock like a diver through dark water.

"Holy shit," Maya Chen says, with the dazed clarity of a woman whose worldview has just undergone catastrophic remodeling. "You're real."

"I'm real," I say, and my voice in this form is deeper, resonant, vibrating in the stone walls.

She doesn't run.

She stares at me — all of me, scales and coils and claws and golden eyes — and she doesn't run. Her hands are still shaking. Her heartbeat, which I can feel through the Encanto like a drum in my chest, is fast and frightened. But she's looking at me the way she looks at everything: like a problem to be solved. Like a mystery to be understood.

"The Encanto," she says slowly. "You left something out."

My blood freezes.

"My grandmother's journal," she continues. "She wrote about the bond. The resonance between matched souls. She said the encantado beneath the house had been waiting." Her voice cracks, just slightly, on the word *waiting*. "Is that what this is? The jolt when we touch. The hum. The dreams."

I cannot lie. Not now, not in this form, not with her looking at me with those terrible, brilliant, beautiful eyes.

"Yes," I say.

She closes her eyes. Opens them.

"How long?" she whispers. "How long have you been waiting?"

"Two hundred and eighty-seven years," I say. "For anyone. Nine days — for you."

Something moves across her face that I can't categorize. Not fear. Not anger. Something rawer, something that looks almost like the saudade I've carried for centuries reflected back at me from a human face.

The house trembles. A tremor — small, but real, rolling up from the deep, through the ward-stone, through the foundation, into the bedroom where a serpent creature and a marine biologist are staring at each other across a divide that is wider and narrower than anything I've ever known.

"That tremor," she says. "That's what the ward-stone holds back?"

"Yes."

"And it's failing."

"Yes."

"And I... my blood... I can help fix it."

"Yes."

She presses her palms to her face. Takes a breath. Drops her hands.

"Okay," she says. "Okay. I have about nine hundred questions, and at least half of them are going to sound insane, and I reserve the right to freak out about this for approximately the next decade. But right now — right now, I need you to promise me one thing."

"Anything."

"No more lies."

The word settles between us like a ward-stone of its own — heavy, foundational, built to hold.

"No more lies," I say.

She nods. Wraps the blanket tighter around her shoulders. Looks at me — the full eight feet of me, coiled in her grandmother's bedroom, golden-eyed and fanged and trembling with the effort of holding still.

"So," she says, and I hear, beneath the fear and the shock and the nine hundred questions, the thing that made my blood sing the moment she walked through the door of this house. Curiosity. Fierce, stubborn, unkillable curiosity.

"Tell me more."

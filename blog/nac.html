NAC (N-Acetyl Cysteine): The Glutathione Precursor That Does a Lot More Than You Think

Used in hospital emergency rooms for paracetamol overdoses, investigated for OCD, addiction, and PCOS, and quietly sitting in thousands of supplement stacks — NAC may be the most underrated compound on the shelf.

March 2026 • 11 min read

What NAC Actually Is

N-Acetyl Cysteine is a stable, bioavailable form of the amino acid cysteine. It's not found naturally in food in meaningful amounts — it's a modified version of cysteine (itself found in chicken, eggs, dairy, and legumes). In supplement form, NAC is taken orally and metabolised to cysteine, which then drives one of the most important reactions in human biochemistry: the synthesis of glutathione.

Glutathione is often called the body's "master antioxidant." Unlike vitamin C or vitamin E — which quench individual free radicals — glutathione operates throughout the intracellular environment, neutralising reactive oxygen species, regenerating other antioxidants, supporting immune function, and driving phase II liver detoxification. Your cells make it continuously. Your ability to make it declines with age, stress, alcohol consumption, and chronic illness.

NAC's main job is to supply the rate-limiting building block for that process. Cysteine is scarce. NAC delivers it reliably.

The Glutathione-NAC Relationship: Why Not Just Take Glutathione?

You can buy glutathione capsules. The problem: oral glutathione is poorly absorbed. The tripeptide (glutamate-cysteine-glycine) is broken down in the gut before it reaches circulation in meaningful amounts. Liposomal glutathione improves delivery somewhat, but the research is limited.

NAC solves this differently. It doesn't supply glutathione directly — it supplies cysteine, which crosses intestinal membranes efficiently, enters cells, and is used there to synthesise glutathione where it's actually needed.

Studies consistently show that oral NAC raises intracellular glutathione levels. A 2000 review in Free Radical Biology and Medicine confirmed significant elevation in blood glutathione following NAC supplementation across multiple clinical populations, including HIV-positive patients, elderly individuals, and healthy controls.

The practical upshot: if you want to raise glutathione — for liver support, oxidative stress, or any other reason — NAC is the most evidence-backed way to do it orally.

The Emergency Room Application

NAC has a well-established clinical use that most people don't know about: it's the standard treatment for paracetamol (acetaminophen) overdose. When paracetamol is taken in excessive doses, it overwhelms the liver's glucuronidation pathway and produces a toxic metabolite (NAPQI) that depletes glutathione and causes fatal liver damage within 72 hours.

Intravenous NAC, given within 8–10 hours of overdose, replenishes glutathione fast enough to neutralise NAPQI and prevent liver failure. It's one of the clearest demonstrations of NAC's mechanism in action — and it has saved hundreds of thousands of lives since the 1970s.

This clinical use is also why NAC has been flagged by the FDA as a pharmaceutical ingredient — complicating its supplement status in the US and triggering Amazon's 2021 removal of some NAC products (later reversed). The UK, EU, and most other markets treat it straightforwardly as a supplement.

Liver Protection: The Evidence

Beyond acute overdose, NAC has documented protective effects on the liver in chronic settings:

Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD): A 2019 RCT in Hepatology International (n=60, 12 weeks, 600mg NAC twice daily) found significant reductions in liver enzymes (ALT, AST) and improvements in ultrasound-assessed liver steatosis compared to placebo.

Alcohol-associated liver disease: NAC is a well-established adjunct in clinical settings. By replenishing glutathione depleted by alcohol metabolism (via the CYP2E1 pathway, which generates substantial reactive oxygen species), NAC reduces hepatocellular oxidative damage. A 2020 meta-analysis in Medicine found NAC significantly reduced oxidative markers and inflammatory cytokines in alcoholic liver disease patients.

Drug-induced liver injury: Beyond paracetamol, NAC has been studied (with more mixed results) for gentamicin, isoniazid (TB antibiotic), and other hepatotoxic agents.

Practical note: if you drink alcohol regularly, train intensively, take medications with hepatic processing, or are simply ageing (glutathione synthesis declines from the third decade onwards), a daily NAC supplement has a reasonable evidence base for liver support.

Respiratory Benefits: Mucus and COPD

NAC has been used as a mucolytic (mucus-thinning agent) in respiratory medicine since the 1960s. The mechanism: NAC breaks disulfide bonds in the mucin glycoproteins that give mucus its viscosity and stickiness. This makes it easier to clear.

In chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD): a landmark 2014 RCT published in Chest (the PANTHEON trial, n=1,006, 1-year follow-up) found that 600mg NAC twice daily reduced COPD exacerbation rates by 22% compared to placebo in patients not on inhaled corticosteroids. Patients on ICS showed less benefit, suggesting NAC is particularly useful in the ICS-naive population.

For general respiratory health: the evidence is weaker but consistent with a modest benefit for mucociliary clearance — relevant for people prone to chest infections, those in polluted environments, or heavy smokers.

Inhaled NAC (nebulised) is used in cystic fibrosis and some ICU settings, though oral supplementation doesn't replicate this direct airway delivery.

Psychiatric Applications: The Surprising Area

The most intriguing emerging research on NAC concerns mental health — and it works through a different mechanism entirely. Beyond glutathione, NAC modulates glutamate transmission by affecting cystine-glutamate antiporter activity in glial cells. Glutamate is the brain's primary excitatory neurotransmitter, and dysregulated glutamate cycling underlies several psychiatric conditions.

OCD

A 2012 double-blind RCT in Journal of Clinical Psychopharmacology found that 2,400mg/day NAC added to SSRI therapy significantly reduced OCD symptom scores (Y-BOCS) compared to SSRI alone over 12 weeks. Effect sizes were modest but meaningful for a drug with negligible side effects.

Addiction and Compulsive Behaviours

NAC has been studied in cocaine, cannabis, nicotine, and gambling addiction with consistent signals across multiple small trials. A 2011 RCT in Biological Psychiatry (n=116) found NAC significantly reduced cocaine craving and use versus placebo in cocaine-dependent adults. A 2014 trial in JAMA Internal Medicine found NAC reduced cannabis use compared to placebo in adolescent users.

The mechanism: chronic drug use disrupts cystine-glutamate exchange, reducing extracellular glutamate and triggering compensatory upregulation — creating a persistent craving state. NAC normalises this exchange. The effect isn't "blunting pleasure" — it's restoring baseline glutamate homeostasis.

Depression and Bipolar

A 2008 RCT in Biological Psychiatry by Berk et al. (n=75) found that 2,000mg/day NAC over 24 weeks significantly reduced depressive symptoms (MADRS scale) in patients with bipolar disorder. Effect sizes were clinically meaningful (Cohen's d ~0.5 for depression subscale). Multiple subsequent trials have explored this, with mixed but generally positive signals, particularly for the depressive phase of bipolar.

The Caveat

Psychiatric NAC research is characterised by small sample sizes, short durations, and variable results. It's promising, not definitive. Don't replace prescribed psychiatric medication with NAC. The most evidence-supported use is as an adjunct — supplemental to, not instead of, standard care.

PCOS: The Insulin-Glutathione Connection

Polycystic ovary syndrome involves insulin resistance, androgen excess, and chronic low-grade inflammation — all conditions associated with oxidative stress. Several RCTs have compared NAC to metformin in PCOS with interesting results.

A 2007 meta-analysis in Fertility and Sterility reviewed 5 trials (n=411) comparing NAC to metformin for ovulation induction in PCOS. NAC showed comparable ovulation rates to metformin with a better side-effect profile (metformin causes significant GI distress in many users). The researchers noted NAC's antioxidant mechanism as complementary to metformin's insulin-sensitising pathway — different mechanisms, similar downstream effects on ovarian function.

For women with PCOS wanting to explore NAC, 600mg–1,800mg/day is the range used in trials. Discuss with an endocrinologist before substituting or adding to existing treatment.

Kidney Protection (Contrast Nephropathy)

NAC became standard premedication in many hospitals for patients receiving iodinated contrast dye (used in CT scans and angiography) with impaired kidney function. The concern: contrast agents can trigger acute kidney injury in susceptible patients by generating reactive oxygen species in renal tubular cells.

Early trials showed NAC premedication significantly reduced contrast-induced nephropathy. However, a large 2018 RCT (PRESERVE trial, n=5,177) found no benefit of NAC over sodium bicarbonate alone, raising questions about earlier positive findings. The debate continues — some centres still use it, others have moved away. The risk-benefit profile still favours NAC given its safety, but the evidence is weaker than once thought.

Tolerability

NAC is generally well-tolerated at standard supplement doses (600–1,200mg/day). Higher doses used in psychiatric trials occasionally cause nausea, diarrhoea, or headache — usually dose-dependent and manageable by splitting doses and taking with food.

One important note: NAC has a sulphurous smell. Some brands are worse than others. Enteric-coated capsules reduce GI irritation and smell. The effervescent powder form (common in European pharmacies) is bioavailable but strong-tasting.

Timing

No strong evidence for specific timing. Morning with food is conventional for most uses. If taking for sleep quality or anxiety (via glutamate modulation), some data favours evening dosing — though this is not well-established.

Drug Interactions

Nitroglycerin: NAC may enhance nitroglycerin's vasodilatory effect — caution in patients taking nitrates for angina.

Activated charcoal: Binds NAC; don't co-administer (relevant primarily in overdose settings).

Immunosuppressants: Theoretical concern that glutathione boosting could modify immune responses — clinically relevant data is sparse.

No clinically significant interactions with common supplements or most medications in normal supplementation doses.

The FDA Controversy and Availability

In 2020, the US FDA issued warning letters suggesting NAC may be excluded from the dietary supplement category because it was "first marketed as a drug" (as Mucomyst, an inhaled mucolytic). This triggered confusion and temporary removal of NAC products from Amazon and some US retailers.

The FDA has not enforced this position definitively, and NAC remains widely available in the US as of 2026. In the UK and EU, NAC is sold freely as a supplement in pharmacies and online. The regulatory situation is worth monitoring if you're in the US and sourcing NAC long-term.

Who Should Consider NAC

NAC is worth considering if you:

  • Drink alcohol regularly and want liver support
  • Have COPD or chronic bronchitis and get frequent exacerbations
  • Have PCOS and are looking for insulin-sensitising support with better GI tolerance than metformin
  • Struggle with OCD, compulsive behaviours, or addiction (as an adjunct to treatment — not a replacement)
  • Are over 50 and aware that glutathione synthesis declines with age
  • Train hard and want to support oxidative stress management (though high-dose antioxidants around training can theoretically blunt adaptations — more below)

The Training Adaptation Caveat

One nuance worth knowing: reactive oxygen species (ROS) generated during exercise are not purely harmful. They're signalling molecules that trigger mitochondrial biogenesis, upregulation of the body's own antioxidant defences, and muscle repair. High-dose antioxidant supplementation (vitamin C, E, and potentially NAC) around workouts may blunt these adaptations.

A 2014 study in Journal of Physiology found that high-dose vitamin C + E supplementation blunted VO2max and insulin sensitivity adaptations in exercising older adults. Whether NAC causes similar blunting is unknown — the data is not as direct — but the principle applies. If you train for performance, avoid taking high-dose NAC immediately pre or post-workout. Morning dosing on an empty stomach or with a late meal is a reasonable compromise.

Bottom Line

NAC is one of the most research-backed compounds available over the counter, with applications ranging from emergency medicine to liver support to psychiatric care — and a clean safety profile at standard doses. It's not a miracle supplement. It works by supporting a fundamental biochemical process (glutathione synthesis) that's critical across multiple organ systems.

For most people, 600mg once or twice daily provides meaningful antioxidant and liver-support benefits. For specific conditions (COPD, OCD adjunct, PCOS), higher doses in clinical ranges are where the evidence sits.

The irony: NAC is one of the cheapest compounds in supplement form, essential in every hospital, and barely discussed in mainstream health culture. Now you know it exists.

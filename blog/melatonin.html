Melatonin: Dosing Myths, Circadian Biology, and Why Less Is More

Most people take 5–10mg melatonin and wonder why it doesn't work well. The science says 0.3–0.5mg is physiologically appropriate for most adults. Here's the biology behind that, and what melatonin actually does — and doesn't do.

What Melatonin Actually Is

Melatonin is not a sedative. This is the most important thing to understand about it, and it's the thing most people get wrong.

Melatonin is a chronobiotic — a compound that signals time-of-day to your body. It's produced by the pineal gland in response to darkness and tells your brain and peripheral tissues that it's night. It doesn't knock you out. It doesn't increase sleep drive (that's adenosine). It shifts the timing of your biological clock.

The distinction matters enormously for how and when to use it — and why most people are using it incorrectly.

The Pineal Gland and Endogenous Production

Your pineal gland begins secreting melatonin 1–2 hours before your habitual sleep time, driven by the drop in environmental light reaching your retinal ganglion cells (ipRGCs — intrinsically photosensitive retinal ganglion cells that contain melanopsin and are particularly sensitive to short-wavelength blue light around 480nm).

Peak endogenous melatonin concentrations in healthy adults: 80–150 pg/mL (picograms per millilitre). These levels are reached around 2–3 AM and decline before waking.

Here's what most people don't know: a typical 5mg melatonin tablet produces plasma levels of 2,000–10,000 pg/mL — roughly 10–100× physiological levels. A 10mg tablet is even more disproportionate.

This supraphysiological dosing is not better. It's often worse.

The Dose-Response Problem

A landmark study by Zhdanova et al. (1995, Sleep) tested doses of 0.1mg, 0.3mg, and 1.0mg in young adults with normal sleep. All three doses reduced sleep onset latency similarly. The 0.3mg dose produced plasma melatonin levels closest to physiological night-time peaks.

A 2022 dose-finding study published in the Journal of Pineal Research (Voultsios et al.) confirmed the key finding: doses above 0.5mg don't produce meaningfully better sleep outcomes in most adults. What they do produce:

  • Next-morning grogginess ("melatonin hangover")
  • Daytime melatonin elevation, which can disrupt the body's natural circadian signal
  • Potential suppression of endogenous production with long-term use (though evidence is limited)
  • Vivid dreams and disrupted REM architecture in some users

The 5–10mg doses sold in US pharmacies and health stores were never calibrated to the biology. They reflect the typical minimum dose thresholds used in early clinical studies (which used 5mg as a starting point), not physiological appropriateness. UK and EU regulations cap OTC melatonin at 0.5mg (it's prescription-only above that in most of Europe) precisely because of this.

When Melatonin Actually Works: The Evidence

Jet Lag — Best Evidence

Melatonin's strongest evidence base is jet lag. A 2002 Cochrane Review (Herxheimer & Petrie) covering 10 trials found melatonin taken at destination bedtime significantly reduced jet lag severity compared to placebo. The effect was most pronounced for eastward travel (crossing more than 5 time zones).

Protocol for jet lag: 0.5mg at 10 PM local destination time on day of travel and for 3–5 days after. No need for higher doses.

Delayed Sleep Phase Syndrome (DSPS) and Chronotype Shifting

For people who naturally fall asleep late (2–4 AM) and can't shift earlier, melatonin can advance the circadian clock when taken several hours before desired sleep time.

The phase-shifting protocol: 0.5mg taken 5–6 hours before desired bedtime (not at bedtime). This is called the "phase advance" effect. Taking melatonin at bedtime doesn't shift the clock — it just slightly increases drowsiness at that moment.

This timing distinction is critical and largely ignored in consumer use. If you want to fall asleep at 11 PM instead of 1 AM, take 0.5mg at 5–6 PM for 1–2 weeks while simultaneously advancing light exposure in the morning.

Shift Workers

Meta-analyses show melatonin (0.5–3mg) helps shift workers sleep during daytime hours, with modest but consistent improvements in sleep duration and quality. Timing matters: take it immediately after arriving home from a night shift, in a darkened room.

Primary Insomnia — Limited Effect

For standard primary insomnia in people with normal circadian timing, melatonin's evidence is modest. A 2013 meta-analysis (Ferracioli-Oda et al., PLOS ONE, 19 RCTs) found melatonin reduced sleep onset latency by ~7 minutes and increased total sleep time by ~8 minutes compared to placebo. Statistically significant; clinically modest.

The effect is larger in circadian-related sleep problems (jet lag, DSPS, shift work) than in insomnia caused by anxiety, poor sleep hygiene, or other factors.

Honest conclusion: If you can't sleep because you're anxious, your phone is keeping you awake, or your bedroom is too warm, melatonin won't solve the problem. Address the root cause first.

Older Adults

Endogenous melatonin production declines with age — significantly so after 55–60. Older adults frequently have lower nocturnal melatonin peaks. Supplementation (0.5–2mg) shows more consistent benefit in this group than in young adults. Extended-release formulations may better mimic the natural nocturnal profile.

Melatonin and Children: A Caution

Melatonin is increasingly used in children, particularly those with ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, or neurodevelopmental conditions that affect sleep. Evidence from RCTs supports short-term use in these populations (with clinician supervision).

However, long-term use in children is a concern. Melatonin receptors are present throughout the body and have developmental roles. Sustained supraphysiological melatonin exposure during development could theoretically affect puberty timing and hormonal maturation. Studies are limited but precautionary guidelines exist in most European countries.

Practical guidance:

  • Short-term use for circadian rhythm adjustment: generally acceptable
  • Long-term daily use in children: discuss with a paediatrician
  • Doses should be significantly lower than adult doses — typically 0.5–1mg
  • Children should not take adult formulations without dose calculation

Exogenous Suppression: Does Supplementation Reduce Your Own Production?

This is a frequently asked question. The evidence is reassuring for short-term use and cautiously uncertain for very long-term use.

Short-term supplementation (days to weeks) does not appear to suppress endogenous pineal melatonin production. The pineal gland doesn't have a feedback mechanism to downregulate based on circulating melatonin levels in the way, say, testosterone feedback works on the HPG axis.

Long-term daily supplementation (months to years): limited evidence. There's theoretical concern from animal models, and some clinicians advise against continuous daily use exceeding 2–3 months without breaks. As a practical matter, if you're using melatonin nightly for sleep: take the lowest effective dose (0.3–0.5mg) and consider periodic 2–4 week breaks to maintain the circadian signal.

Light Exposure: The Foundation

No discussion of melatonin is complete without acknowledging what actually drives its production: light.

Morning light: 10–30 minutes of outdoor bright light within an hour of waking suppresses melatonin, advances the circadian clock, and sets the biological countdown to appropriate evening melatonin rise ~15 hours later. This single habit likely does more for sleep than any supplement.

Evening light: Blue-light exposure from screens after sunset delays melatonin onset. A 2014 paper in PNAS (Chang et al.) showed that an iPad used for 4 hours before bed suppressed melatonin by ~55% and delayed melatonin onset by 1.5 hours versus reading a print book. Blue-light blocking glasses (~50% mitigation) or switching screens to warm yellow/red tones after 8 PM are evidence-backed strategies.

If you sort your light environment, you may find you don't need melatonin supplements at all. Most sleep problems in young, healthy adults are lifestyle-driven, not melatonin deficiency-driven.

Interactions and Safety

Melatonin is generally very well tolerated. Known interactions and cautions:

  • Anticoagulants (warfarin, heparin): Melatonin may enhance anticoagulant effect — check INR more frequently if adding melatonin
  • Immunosuppressants: Melatonin has immune-modulatory effects; potentially problematic for transplant recipients or people on immunosuppression
  • Epilepsy: Some case reports of seizure threshold reduction; use caution
  • CYP1A2 substrates: Melatonin is metabolised by CYP1A2; fluvoxamine (CYP1A2 inhibitor) dramatically increases melatonin levels — avoid combination or reduce dose significantly
  • Pregnancy: Limited data; avoid unless specifically recommended by a clinician
  • Autoimmune conditions: Melatonin can stimulate immune function; theoretically problematic in autoimmune disease — discuss with your doctor

The common side effects (vivid dreams, grogginess, headache) are almost always dose-dependent and largely eliminated by using ≤0.5mg.

What Melatonin Won't Fix

Be clear-eyed about what melatonin cannot do:

  • Anxiety-driven insomnia: Melatonin doesn't calm a racing mind. CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioural Therapy for Insomnia) is first-line treatment for chronic insomnia and more effective than any supplement.
  • Sleep apnea: No effect on airway obstruction. Get tested if you snore heavily or wake unrefreshed.
  • Circadian chaos from inconsistent schedules: If you sleep at 11 PM one night and 2 AM the next, melatonin can't rescue that. Consistency of sleep timing is the single most powerful sleep intervention.
  • Afternoon energy crashes: Those are largely about adenosine accumulation and caffeine half-life, not melatonin.

The Bottom Line

Melatonin is a genuine, useful tool for circadian timing problems — jet lag, shift work, delayed sleep phase. Its evidence for primary insomnia is modest. Its optimal dose for most adults is 0.3–0.5mg — a fraction of what's sold OTC in the US.

If you're taking 5mg and finding melatonin either doesn't work or makes you groggy, try 0.5mg and focus on your light environment. That combination will outperform 10mg tablets and a dark room with a bright phone screen every time.

And if sleep is genuinely disrupted: address light first, timing second, and supplement third. Melatonin is a signal, not a sedative.

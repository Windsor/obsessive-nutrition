Longevity & NAD+

NMN and NR: The NAD+ Supplements, the Anti-Aging Claims, and What the Evidence Actually Shows

David Sinclair takes it. Peter Attia is cautious about it. The mouse studies are dramatic. The human trials are newer, smaller, and more complicated. Here's where NAD+ precursors actually stand.

March 2026 • 12 min read

Why NAD+ Matters

NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every living cell. It sits at the centre of cellular energy metabolism — shuttling electrons in the mitochondrial electron transport chain during oxidative phosphorylation — and it's also a required substrate for a family of enzymes called sirtuins (SIRT1–7), which regulate gene expression, DNA repair, and stress resistance.

The critical fact: NAD+ levels decline with age. Studies in rodents and humans show a 40–60% reduction in tissue NAD+ between young adulthood and middle age, with steeper declines thereafter. This decline is associated with mitochondrial dysfunction, reduced sirtuin activity, impaired DNA repair, and metabolic deterioration — essentially, many of the hallmarks of biological aging.

The hypothesis driving NMN and NR research: if declining NAD+ contributes to aging, restoring it should slow or reverse some aging processes. The mouse evidence supporting this hypothesis is genuinely compelling. The human evidence is more preliminary — but growing.

NMN vs NR: The Same Goal, Different Paths

Both NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) are NAD+ precursors — compounds the body converts into NAD+. They take slightly different biochemical routes:

A longstanding debate: does NMN actually get absorbed as NMN, or is it dephosphorylated to NR in the gut before absorption? A 2023 study in Cell Metabolism by the Imai lab showed that in humans, orally administered NMN raises blood NMN within 15 minutes — supporting direct absorption via a specific transporter (Slc12a8) identified in earlier mouse work. This partly resolves the debate in NMN's favour, though NR advocates note that blood NR also rises substantially, suggesting partial conversion still occurs.

Practical implication: Both compounds raise blood and tissue NAD+. The mechanism debate doesn't substantially change whether you should take one — but it affects which brand of evidence you're drawing on.

The Mouse Data: Why Everyone Got Excited

The animal research on NAD+ precursors is dramatic. A brief survey:

  • 2013 (Cell, Gomes et al.): NMN treatment reversed age-related decline in muscle oxidative metabolism in 22-month-old mice, restoring mitochondrial function to that of 6-month-old mice. Essentially: old mice became metabolically young. The authors described this as a "pseudo-rejuvenation" effect.
  • 2016 (Cell, Mills et al.): NMN supplementation in 12-month-old mice over 12 months improved energy metabolism, insulin sensitivity, eye function, bone density, immune function, and physical activity — without weight gain or toxic effects.
  • NR studies in rodents: Consistent improvements in mitochondrial biogenesis, NAD+ levels, exercise capacity, and metabolic markers. One study showed NR extended lifespan in a mouse model of Cockayne syndrome (a DNA repair disorder).

The catch: mice are not humans. Mouse lifespan can be extended by numerous interventions that fail in humans. Mice have higher metabolic rates, different NAD+ homeostasis, and don't share all human aging mechanisms. The translation problem in longevity research is notorious.

The Human Evidence: What We Actually Know

NR Human Trials

Trammell et al. 2016 (Nature Communications): First human pharmacokinetics study. n=12 healthy adults. 100mg, 300mg, or 1,000mg single NR dose raised blood NAD+ in a dose-dependent manner. Confirmed oral NR is bioavailable and raises NAD+ levels in humans. No significant side effects.

Martens et al. 2018 (Nature Communications): n=24 healthy middle-aged/older adults. 500mg NR twice daily (1,000mg/day) for 6 weeks. Results: blood NAD+ rose 60% vs placebo. Systolic blood pressure reduced by 3.9 mmHg and aortic stiffness markers improved — clinically meaningful cardiovascular signals. No improvement in maximal walking distance, muscle function, or cognitive measures.

Dollerup et al. 2018 (Nature Communications): n=40 obese men, 1,000mg NR/day for 12 weeks. Blood NAD+ rose significantly. No metabolic improvements: no changes in insulin sensitivity, body composition, liver fat, or mitochondrial function markers. Disappointing for the metabolic disease angle.

Elhassan et al. 2019 (Cell Reports): Skeletal muscle NAD+ confirmed elevated in biopsies after NR. But the expected downstream effects (mitochondrial biogenesis, sirtuin activation markers) were minimal in muscle tissue — suggesting NAD+ elevation doesn't automatically translate to functional improvements in all tissues.

NMN Human Trials

Yoshino et al. 2021 (Science): First major human NMN RCT. n=25 postmenopausal women with prediabetes or obesity. 250mg NMN/day for 10 weeks. Blood NAD+ elevated significantly. Key finding: improved muscle insulin sensitivity (as measured by glucose infusion rate during hyperinsulinemic-euglycemic clamp). No changes in body composition or overall insulin resistance. The 250mg dose appeared to specifically benefit skeletal muscle — an important signal for metabolic health.

Liao et al. 2021 (Frontiers in Aging): n=66 healthy elderly adults (65–80 years). 300, 600, or 900mg NMN/day for 60 days. All three doses significantly raised blood NAD+. Muscle strength (grip strength, walking speed) improved significantly at 300mg and 600mg. 900mg showed diminishing returns. This is one of the most clinically relevant findings in the human NMN literature.

Kim et al. 2022 (GeroScience): n=40 recreational runners, 300 or 600mg NMN/day for 6 weeks. Improved aerobic capacity (VO2max equivalent at submaximal exercise) and skeletal muscle adaptations vs placebo. The athletic performance angle — relevant for people who exercise.

What Human Trials Tell Us (So Far)

The honest summary of the human evidence through 2026:

✅ Confirmed: Both NR and NMN reliably raise blood and tissue NAD+ in humans. This is consistent across trials and dose-dependent.

✅ Supported: Some cardiovascular benefits (NR — blood pressure, arterial stiffness). Some skeletal muscle benefits (NMN — insulin sensitivity, strength in elderly). Some athletic performance signals (NMN — VO2max-adjacent markers).

⚠️ Mixed: Metabolic disease benefits. The Dollerup NR trial found no metabolic improvement in obese men. The Yoshino NMN trial found improved muscle-specific insulin sensitivity but not whole-body. The picture is tissue-specific, population-specific, and inconsistent.

❌ Not yet established: Anti-aging or longevity benefits in humans. No human trial has run long enough, or with enough participants, to show effects on aging biomarkers, disease incidence, or lifespan. The mouse-to-human translation for longevity specifically remains unproven.

Why NAD+ Doesn't Automatically Fix Everything

A key insight from the Elhassan 2019 study: raising tissue NAD+ doesn't automatically activate all the downstream pathways you'd expect. Sirtuin activity, mitochondrial biogenesis, and DNA repair responses don't necessarily follow linearly from NAD+ availability. There are rate-limiting factors beyond substrate concentration — protein levels, post-translational modifications, co-factors.

This is why some trials show blood NAD+ rising significantly while outcome measures (mitochondrial function, metabolic markers, physical performance) remain unchanged. NAD+ is necessary but not always sufficient for the hoped-for effects.

The analogy: adding more fuel to a car doesn't help if the engine has other problems. NAD+ is essential for healthy mitochondrial function, but aging involves many simultaneous deteriorations — and NAD+ supplementation addresses only one.

The Sinclair Controversy

David Sinclair's work at Harvard has been central to NMN's popularisation — he takes 1,000mg/day personally, has said so publicly, and his book Lifespan (2019) drove enormous commercial interest in NAD+ precursors. His research lab published much of the key animal work.

Critics note: Sinclair's enthusiasm sometimes runs ahead of the evidence; some of his pronouncements about reversing aging have been called premature. Reproducibility concerns about some sirtuin research exist (a controversy from the 2000s regarding resveratrol/sirtuin specificity). He has financial interests in longevity companies.

The nuanced view: Sinclair's underlying research contributions are legitimate and important. His public advocacy is more enthusiastic than the human evidence currently warrants. The truth is probably somewhere between "miracle anti-aging compound" and "useless" — somewhere that requires more time and larger trials to locate precisely.

Potential Concerns

Cancer Signalling

NAD+ is required for cell proliferation — cancer cells are voracious consumers of NAD+. Some researchers have raised theoretical concerns that raising systemic NAD+ could potentially support tumour growth. This concern is more theoretical than empirically established in humans. Rodent studies have been reassuring. But for individuals with active cancer or cancer history, caution and oncologist consultation is appropriate before supplementing.

Niacin Flush and Metabolites

Both NR and NMN can generate nicotinamide (niacinamide) as a metabolite. At high doses, this can cause mild flushing similar to (though less severe than) niacin. Some people find NMN causes light nausea or GI discomfort at higher doses. Splitting doses and taking with food addresses most GI issues.

Long-term Safety Data

NR has been used in human trials since 2016 — nearly a decade of safety monitoring across multiple trials and millions of consumers. No serious adverse events have emerged. NMN human trials began later (2020) but have similarly clean safety records. Both are considered safe in the doses studied, though "no observed adverse effects in trials lasting weeks to months" is not the same as confirmed long-term safety across decades.

NR vs NMN: Which to Choose?

If cost matters: NR is cheaper and has a longer human safety record. If you're specifically interested in skeletal muscle effects (strength, metabolic health, athletic performance), NMN has the more specific human data. The 2023 Cell Metabolism study confirming NMN's direct absorption is a point in NMN's favour mechanistically.

Most serious longevity researchers who supplement personally choose NMN — partly on the Sinclair influence and partly on the direct absorption data. For most people with a budget constraint, NR at 300–500mg/day delivers well-validated NAD+ elevation.

Timing

NAD+ is involved in circadian rhythm regulation (SIRT1 is a clock gene regulator). Some researchers suggest morning dosing to align with natural circadian NAD+ rhythms. Others find no practical difference. Morning with or without food is conventional; avoid late-night dosing if you find it activating or disruptive to sleep (some users report increased alertness).

Stacking Considerations

Resveratrol: Often combined with NMN (this is Sinclair's personal stack) as resveratrol activates sirtuin enzymes, while NMN provides the NAD+ substrate. The synergy is biologically plausible but not clearly demonstrated in human RCTs. If combining, take resveratrol with fat (it's fat-soluble) — yoghurt or olive oil works.

TMG (Trimethylglycine / Betaine): Some users add TMG alongside NMN/NR based on the reasoning that methylation demand increases with NAD+ synthesis (via NMN's methyl-group requirements). This is theoretically supported; no human trial has confirmed it's necessary. Low-cost and safe if you want to hedge.

CoQ10: Complementary rather than synergistic — CoQ10 works in the electron transport chain adjacent to NAD+, but different mechanism. Both are reasonable for mitochondrial support.

Who Should Consider NMN or NR

These compounds are worth considering if you are:

  • Over 40, particularly 50+, where NAD+ decline is most pronounced
  • Interested in metabolic health and muscle maintenance in ageing
  • Physically active and interested in the athletic performance signals
  • Cardiovascular health-focused (NR arterial stiffness data)
  • Willing to invest in longevity-oriented supplementation ahead of full confirmation from long-term trials

They are probably not necessary if you are under 35 with good metabolic health — NAD+ levels are naturally higher in youth and the benefit-to-cost ratio is less clear.

The Bottom Line

NMN and NR are among the most scientifically interesting supplements available. The biological rationale is sound: NAD+ does decline with age, NAD+ does matter for multiple age-associated processes, and both compounds do reliably raise NAD+ in humans. The key question — whether raising NAD+ translates into meaningful anti-aging benefits in humans — is genuinely open.

The honest answer in 2026: the human trials show real benefits in specific populations (elderly muscle function, arterial stiffness, metabolic improvement in prediabetic muscle). They don't yet show broad anti-aging or longevity benefits because no trial has run long enough or large enough to test that. The mouse data is suggestive. The human data is promising but preliminary.

If you're 50+, physically active, and interested in longevity — NMN or NR at 300–500mg/day represents a reasonable bet on a mechanism with solid biological plausibility and a clean safety record. It's not a certainty. It's not a fountain of youth. But it might be something. And the downside risk at sensible doses appears minimal.

That's the honest version. You can decide if that's good enough for your supplement budget.

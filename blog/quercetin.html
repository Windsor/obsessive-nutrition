Quercetin: Senolytic, Anti-Inflammatory, and What the Evidence Actually Shows

Quercetin is one of the most abundant and studied polyphenols in the human diet. It's been investigated for everything from seasonal allergies to longevity to COVID prevention. Here's what the evidence actually supports — and where it falls short.

Published March 2026 · 13 min read

What Is Quercetin?

Quercetin is a flavonoid — a type of polyphenol pigment — found in a wide range of plant foods. It gives the yellow-green colour to onions, apples, capers, and leafy greens. Humans have eaten quercetin throughout evolutionary history; it's part of the flavonoid family that epidemiological research consistently associates with reduced chronic disease risk.

It has multiple biological activities: antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, antihistamine, antiviral (at high concentrations in vitro), and — most recently and excitingly — senolytic. Each of these deserves a proper look, because the quality of evidence varies enormously across them.

The Bioavailability Problem

Before anything else: quercetin has a notorious bioavailability problem. In its basic aglycone form, it's poorly absorbed from the gut. Typical bioavailability from food ranges from 0–50% depending on the food matrix, gut microbiome, and individual variation.

In food, quercetin is usually present as glycosides (quercetin-3-glucoside, quercetin-3-rutinoside, etc.) — quercetin attached to sugar molecules. These forms have different absorption kinetics:

Form Absorption Notes
Quercetin aglyconeLowMost basic supplement form; poor gut absorption
Quercetin-3-glucoside (Q3G)~52% (Cmax 2–3× aglycone)Main form in onions; SGLT1 transporter involvement
Quercetin-3-rutinoside (rutin)Very low (colonic fermentation)Main form in buckwheat, tea; gut microbiome dependent
Quercetin phytosome (Quercefit)20× better than aglyconePhosphatidylcholine complex; used in better supplements
Quercetin + bromelainModestly enhancedCommon combination supplement; weak evidence for synergy

This matters enormously for supplement selection. Most cheap quercetin capsules use the aglycone form with essentially no bioavailability enhancement. Phytosome preparations (quercetin bound to phosphatidylcholine) like Quercefit (Quercevita) have the strongest human bioavailability data and should be the default choice for supplementing.

Taking quercetin with food — specifically with fat — also significantly improves absorption.

Anti-Inflammatory Mechanisms

Quercetin operates on multiple inflammatory pathways:

  • NF-κB inhibition: Like astaxanthin and curcumin, quercetin suppresses the master inflammatory transcription factor, reducing production of TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, and COX-2.
  • MAPK pathway modulation: Quercetin inhibits multiple mitogen-activated protein kinases involved in inflammatory signalling.
  • Mast cell stabilisation: Quercetin inhibits mast cell degranulation — the release of histamine and other inflammatory mediators. This is the mechanistic basis for its antihistamine effects.
  • Lipoxygenase inhibition: Reduces leukotriene production, another inflammatory pathway relevant to allergies and asthma.
  • PI3K/Akt/mTOR inhibition: At higher concentrations, quercetin inhibits pro-growth/pro-survival signalling that is also relevant to its senolytic effects.

Antihistamine Effects: Where the Evidence Is Solid

The antihistamine mechanism is one of the better-supported quercetin effects in humans. Mast cell degranulation — triggered by allergen-IgE binding — releases histamine, prostaglandins, and leukotrienes. Quercetin inhibits this degranulation by blocking calcium ion influx into mast cells and reducing phospholipase A2 activity.

In vitro and animal models consistently support this. Human evidence is more limited but suggestive:

  • Yamada et al. (2022) conducted a double-blind RCT in 66 Japanese adults with mild hay fever. Quercetin glycoside supplementation (100mg/day) for 8 weeks significantly reduced nasal symptom scores (rhinorrhea, sneezing) versus placebo.
  • Hirano et al. (2009) showed quercetin at 200mg/day for 8 weeks reduced subjective allergy symptoms in adults with moderate perennial allergic rhinitis.

It won't replace antihistamine drugs for acute severe allergies, but as a daily preventive during allergy season, the evidence supports use — particularly given the safety profile.

The Senolytic Story: Quercetin + Dasatinib

This is the most exciting and scientifically compelling area of quercetin research — and also the most overhyped in popular media.

What Are Senescent Cells?

Cellular senescence is a state where cells stop dividing but refuse to die. Young, healthy tissues have very few senescent cells — the immune system clears them efficiently. As we age, senescent cell burden increases because clearance becomes less efficient.

Senescent cells are actively harmful — they secrete a cocktail of inflammatory cytokines, proteases, and growth factors called the Senescence-Associated Secretory Phenotype (SASP). The SASP drives local and systemic inflammation, disrupts tissue function, promotes neighbouring cells to become senescent, and creates an environment that favours tumour growth.

Senolytics are drugs or compounds that selectively eliminate senescent cells. If you could safely clear senescent cells, the theory goes, you'd reduce inflammaging and potentially extend healthspan.

The Discovery of Quercetin as a Senolytic

Zhu et al. (2015) at the Mayo Clinic published a landmark Nature Medicine paper identifying the first senolytic compounds. Using a computational approach to identify drugs that might kill senescent cells by targeting their pro-survival pathways, they identified dasatinib (a cancer drug) and quercetin individually, and found them far more effective in combination.

The D+Q (Dasatinib + Quercetin) combination selectively kills senescent cells via:

  • Inhibiting BCL-2 and BCL-xL (anti-apoptotic proteins that senescent cells upregulate to survive)
  • Inhibiting PI3K/AKT/p21 pro-survival signalling
  • Quercetin specifically targets quercetin-sensitive senescent cell types (particularly human adipose-derived MSCs and endothelial cells)

In animal studies, a single course of D+Q treatment:

  • Extended median lifespan of naturally aged mice by 36%
  • Improved physical function in aged mice (grip strength, gait speed, rotarod performance)
  • Reduced frailty index scores significantly
  • Improved cardiac function in aged mice
  • Alleviated age-related osteoporosis and lung fibrosis

Human Trials: The IDiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis Study

Justice et al. (2019) conducted the first human senolytic trial using D+Q in patients with idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF) — a condition strongly associated with senescent cell accumulation in lung epithelium. 14 patients received intermittent D+Q (3 days on, 18 days off, × 3 cycles = 9 days of treatment total).

Results were encouraging:

  • Significant improvement in 6-minute walk distance (+44 metres, p<0.05)
  • Improved chair-stand test, gait speed, and stair climb speed
  • No significant changes in lung function tests (FVC, DLCO) — the primary clinical endpoint — which may reflect the pilot nature of the study

This was an open-label pilot study with no placebo control. Still, improvements in functional measures in a progressive, degenerative disease are striking.

The SToMP-AD Study (Alzheimer's Disease)

The Mayo Clinic's SToMP-AD trial (2021) — a phase 1 pilot study — enrolled 5 older adults with early symptomatic Alzheimer's disease and gave them D+Q for 12 weeks (intermittent dosing). The primary aim was safety/feasibility. Secondary measures found reductions in cerebrospinal fluid senescence markers (p21, phospho-p53) and preliminary improvements in some cognitive measures. Results were published as a feasibility study — far too small for clinical conclusions.

The Critical Caveat: Quercetin Alone vs D+Q

Here's what the longevity supplement industry frequently obscures: most of the dramatic senolytic evidence involves Dasatinib + Quercetin combined. Dasatinib is a prescription cancer drug with significant potential side effects (pleural effusion, cardiac toxicity, immunosuppression). It's not available OTC.

Quercetin alone has senolytic activity in some cell types but is far weaker than the combination. The cell-type specificity matters — quercetin senolytic activity is strongest in human adipose-derived MSCs and certain endothelial cells, while dasatinib clears senescent fat progenitor cells. They're complementary.

Fisetin — another flavonoid — has shown more promising senolytic activity as a solo compound in mouse studies. Human trials for fisetin as a standalone senolytic are ongoing (NCT02555228, NCT04711655).

The TORC1/mTOR Pathway

Quercetin inhibits mTOR (mechanistic target of rapamycin) signalling — specifically the TORC1 complex. mTOR is a master regulator of cell growth, protein synthesis, and autophagy. Chronic mTOR overactivation is associated with accelerated aging, and mTOR inhibition (like rapamycin or caloric restriction) extends lifespan in model organisms.

Quercetin's mTOR inhibition is weaker than rapamycin but pharmacologically real. Combined with its PI3K/Akt inhibition, this may contribute to its anti-cancer effects observed in cell culture models — though the concentrations typically required in vitro are often higher than what's achievable through supplementation.

Antiviral Activity: COVID-19 and Beyond

During the COVID-19 pandemic, quercetin attracted significant attention based on two mechanisms:

  1. Zinc ionophore activity: Quercetin may act as a zinc ionophore — facilitating zinc entry into cells. Intracellular zinc inhibits RNA-dependent RNA polymerase (RdRp), which coronaviruses need to replicate. This is the same mechanism proposed for hydroxychloroquine.
  2. Direct SARS-CoV-2 protease inhibition: Several in silico and in vitro studies showed quercetin binding to the main protease (Mpro) of SARS-CoV-2 with reasonable affinity.

The human evidence, however, is weak and inconsistent. A handful of small RCTs in early COVID-19 showed some benefit (reduced symptom duration, hospitalisation rate), but most were poorly controlled, small, and not blinded. Quercetin should not be positioned as a COVID treatment or preventive with current evidence.

The zinc ionophore mechanism remains interesting for general antiviral research, and quercetin combined with zinc as a preventive strategy is mechanistically plausible — but this is a hypothesis, not established clinical evidence.

Cardiovascular Effects

Several meta-analyses of quercetin RCTs have examined cardiovascular endpoints:

  • Blood pressure: A 2016 meta-analysis (Serban et al., Journal of the American Heart Association) of 7 trials found quercetin supplementation significantly reduced systolic BP by −3.04 mmHg (p<0.001) and diastolic BP by −2.63 mmHg (p<0.001). Effect was more pronounced in subjects with higher baseline BP (>142 mmHg SBP).
  • LDL cholesterol: Effect size is small and inconsistent across trials. Some studies show modest LDL reduction; others show no effect.
  • Endothelial function (FMD): Limited positive evidence. Quercetin appears to improve flow-mediated dilation at doses ≥150mg/day in some trials.

Exercise Performance

There's a body of work on quercetin and exercise performance, with mixed results:

A 2011 meta-analysis by Kressler et al. in the Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research pooled 11 studies on quercetin and VO2max/endurance performance. The pooled effect was significant but very small (ES=0.13, ~3.9% improvement) — and was predominantly driven by a few studies with high baseline quercetin depletion. The practical significance is modest.

Quercetin's mitochondrial biogenesis effects (via PGC-1α and SIRT1 activation) are real in cell culture — whether dietary or supplement doses achieve this in exercising humans is uncertain.

Cooking matters: Boiling onions reduces quercetin content by ~30% as it leaches into water. Roasting and frying are better for preserving quercetin. The outer skins of onions have the highest content — the reason onion soup (which includes the outer layers in the stock) is quercetin-rich.

Can you get enough from food? Average Western dietary intake is approximately 10–20mg/day. Clinical trials showing effects typically use 150–500mg/day of bioavailable forms. So yes, supplementation is necessary for dose-equivalent effects.

Timing and Co-factors

  • Always take with food (fat improves absorption significantly)
  • Vitamin C co-ingestion may help regenerate quercetin after it acts as an antioxidant
  • Bromelain is often combined; theoretical synergy, limited evidence for enhanced absorption
  • Split doses may improve total bioavailability vs single large dose
  • For senolytic protocols: intermittent dosing (3 consecutive days/month or every 3 months) is the pattern used in human trials, rather than daily continuous dosing

Drug Interactions and Safety

Quercetin is generally very safe at typical supplement doses:

  • No serious adverse effects in trials up to 1,000mg/day for 12 weeks
  • GRAS classification for food use

However, quercetin inhibits CYP3A4 and P-glycoprotein — two major drug metabolism pathways:

  • CYP3A4 substrates: Quercetin can increase blood levels of drugs metabolised by CYP3A4, including cyclosporine, certain statins, some antivirals, calcium channel blockers. This could amplify drug effects — sometimes dangerously.
  • P-gp inhibition: Similar potential for interactions with drugs transported by P-glycoprotein (digoxin, many cancer drugs).
  • Thyroid hormone: Quercetin inhibits thyroid peroxidase in vitro. At very high doses and/or with existing thyroid compromise, this is theoretically relevant. Not well-established at typical supplement doses.
  • Anticoagulants: Theoretical additive effect with warfarin (both affect platelet aggregation). Monitor INR if combining.

If you're on any prescription medication — especially cancer drugs, immunosuppressants, or anticoagulants — check with your prescriber before supplementing quercetin above 500mg/day.

The Bottom Line: What's Quercetin Actually Good For?

Let's be clear-eyed about the evidence hierarchy:

Well-supported:

  • Mast cell stabilisation / antihistamine effect — mechanistically solid, some human RCT support
  • Modest blood pressure reduction — meta-analysis supports ~3mmHg SBP reduction
  • Anti-inflammatory at the mechanistic level — NF-κB, COX-2, lipoxygenase inhibition

Promising but early:

  • Senolytic activity — exciting mouse data and pilot human trials, but human evidence is very early-stage; combined with dasatinib (prescription) in the strongest evidence
  • Neuroprotection and cognitive aging — mechanistic support, no strong human RCTs
  • Antiviral effects — mechanistically interesting, COVID trials inconclusive

Weak or overhyped:

  • Exercise performance — small effect sizes, likely not practically significant
  • Cancer treatment — in vitro cell culture data doesn't translate; supplementation doses don't achieve relevant concentrations
  • COVID prevention — not supported by current clinical evidence

Quercetin is a legitimate supplement for allergy management, anti-inflammatory support, and potentially as part of a longevity stack — but the bioavailability form matters enormously, the senolytic benefits are mostly from the dasatinib combination, and the "nature's senolytic" headline oversimplifies a much more nuanced picture.

For most people, 250–500mg/day of quercetin phytosome with meals is a reasonable daily protocol. For those following longevity-focused senolytic cycles, intermittent high-dose protocols (1,000mg for 3 consecutive days monthly) are used in the research context, though optimal human dosing remains an open question.

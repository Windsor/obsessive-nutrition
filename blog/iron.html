Iron: The World's Most Common Deficiency and Why Your Supplement Probably Isn't Working

March 2026 15 min read Micronutrients

Iron deficiency is the most common nutritional deficiency on the planet. The WHO estimates it affects approximately 2 billion people — roughly 25% of the global population. In women of reproductive age, prevalence reaches 30–40% in many countries. Even in wealthy nations with abundant food supplies, iron deficiency anaemia remains the leading cause of anaemia and a major driver of fatigue, cognitive impairment, reduced athletic performance, and impaired immune function.

Yet iron is also genuinely dangerous in excess. It is one of the few nutrients where toxicity is both real and relatively accessible. Iron metabolism is tightly regulated, and the system that controls it is more sophisticated than most people realise. Understanding it is the difference between effective supplementation and spending money on pills that either don't work or make you ill.

What Iron Does in the Body

Iron's most famous role is oxygen transport — as the core of haemoglobin in red blood cells, it binds oxygen in the lungs and delivers it to tissues. But haemoglobin is only part of the story. Iron is also:

  • The core of myoglobin: Myoglobin stores oxygen in muscle tissue. Athletes with depleted iron stores suffer performance losses even before haemoglobin levels fall below the clinical anaemia threshold.
  • Essential for mitochondrial function: Iron-sulphur clusters and haem-containing proteins (cytochromes) are central to the electron transport chain. Iron-deficient cells produce less ATP; this is why fatigue precedes full anaemia in iron depletion.
  • Required for dopamine synthesis: Iron is a cofactor for tyrosine hydroxylase, the rate-limiting enzyme in dopamine production. Iron deficiency causes mood disturbances and impairs motivation; restless legs syndrome is associated with brain iron deficiency specifically.
  • Critical for immune function: Both macrophage killing capacity and lymphocyte proliferation require adequate iron. However, iron also feeds pathogens — this creates a complex picture where acute illness drives hepcidin-mediated iron sequestration as a deliberate immune strategy.
  • Required for thyroid hormone production: Thyroid peroxidase is an iron-dependent enzyme. Iron deficiency impairs thyroid hormone synthesis, and co-existing iron and iodine deficiency produces worse outcomes than either alone.
  • Important for cognitive development: In foetuses and infants, iron is critical for myelination and hippocampal development. Iron deficiency during this window causes lasting cognitive impairments that are not fully reversed even with subsequent repletion.

The Three Stages of Iron Depletion

Iron deficiency develops in stages, and clinical practice often catches it too late:

  1. Iron depletion (Stage 1): Stored iron (ferritin) is reduced, but blood markers (haemoglobin, haematocrit, red cell indices) remain normal. Symptoms: fatigue, reduced exercise tolerance, poor concentration. Standard full blood count is normal — only ferritin testing reveals the problem.
  2. Iron-deficient erythropoiesis (Stage 2): Iron supply to bone marrow is insufficient for normal red blood cell production. Transferrin saturation falls. Red cells begin to become smaller (microcytic) and paler (hypochromic). Fatigue and symptoms worsen.
  3. Iron deficiency anaemia (Stage 3): Haemoglobin falls below threshold (below 120 g/L in women, 130 g/L in men per WHO criteria). This is what a standard FBC detects. At this point, weeks to months of depletion have already occurred.
The practical implication: if you wait for a standard blood count to diagnose iron deficiency, you are systematically missing Stage 1 — when symptoms are already present and intervention is most impactful. Always check ferritin, not just haemoglobin.

Who Is Most at Risk

  • Menstruating women: Each menstrual cycle removes 20–80 mg of iron. Women with heavy menstrual bleeding (HMB, defined as >80 mL/cycle) can lose 150–200 mg monthly — far exceeding what diet typically replaces. Iron deficiency without anaemia is the norm in this group, not the exception.
  • Pregnant women: Iron requirements rise to 27 mg/day during pregnancy (vs 18 mg/day in non-pregnant women of reproductive age). Expanded blood volume, foetal iron accretion, and placental needs drive this. Routine supplementation during pregnancy is universal guidance.
  • Endurance athletes: "Sports anaemia" is well-documented. Foot-strike haemolysis (destruction of red cells from repeated impact), increased GI losses during intense exercise, increased sweating losses, and high training demands create negative iron balance. Female endurance athletes are particularly vulnerable.
  • Vegans and vegetarians: Plant foods contain only non-haem iron, which has substantially lower bioavailability than haem iron from meat. The RDA for vegetarians is 1.8x the standard recommendation (approximately 32 mg/day for adult women) — impossible to achieve without careful food combining or supplementation.
  • People with gut issues: Coeliac disease, Crohn's disease, achlorhydria (low stomach acid), and gastric bypass surgery all impair iron absorption. H. pylori infection specifically depletes iron by competing for luminal iron.
  • Regular blood donors: Each whole blood donation removes approximately 250 mg of iron. Frequent donors who don't actively replace this can develop iron depletion over time.
  • Infants and toddlers: Rapid growth demands outpace intake, particularly in exclusively breastfed infants after 6 months (breast milk is low in iron) and in toddlers with high-dairy, low-meat diets ("milk displacement").

Haem vs Non-Haem Iron: The Absorption Gap

This distinction fundamentally determines how much iron from any given food actually enters your body.

Haem iron (from meat, poultry, fish) is absorbed at rates of 15–35%. It enters intestinal cells as an intact haem molecule via a dedicated transporter (HCP1/PCFT). Its absorption is relatively immune to dietary inhibitors and is only moderately influenced by iron status.

Non-haem iron (from plants, dairy, eggs, and most supplements) is absorbed at rates of 1–15%. It must first be converted to the ferrous (Fe²⁺) form before absorption. This conversion requires an acidic environment (stomach acid) and is dramatically influenced by co-consumed foods:

The research on ferrous bisglycinate is particularly interesting. A 2020 RCT in Nutrients (Szarfarc et al.) found bisglycinate achieved equivalent ferritin repletion at half the dose of ferrous sulphate, with significantly fewer GI side effects. The mechanism is the glycinate chelation — the iron is absorbed via the dipeptide/amino acid transporter alongside food rather than through the competitive DMT1 transporter, making it less affected by dietary inhibitors and less irritating to the gut mucosa.

The Hepcidin Problem

Here's something most iron supplement guidance completely ignores: hepcidin, the liver hormone that regulates iron absorption. When serum iron is adequate or elevated, hepcidin rises and blocks further absorption by degrading ferroportin (the only iron export channel) in intestinal cells. When iron is depleted, hepcidin falls and absorption increases.

This creates a counterintuitive implication for supplementation timing:

  • Twice-daily dosing is often inferior to once-daily. Oral iron rapidly raises serum iron, which within hours triggers hepcidin release that blocks absorption of a subsequent dose. Studies by Moretti et al. (2015, Blood) showed that alternate-day dosing achieves equivalent or better ferritin repletion than daily dosing with better tolerability.
  • The "split dose" advice is wrong for most people. Many clinicians still advise 200mg ferrous sulphate twice daily. The hepcidin data suggests this may be unnecessarily aggressive and counterproductive. Single daily dosing or alternate-day dosing is likely equivalent in total iron absorbed over weeks.
  • Acute illness suppresses iron absorption. During infections, inflammatory cytokines (especially IL-6) massively upregulate hepcidin. This is an evolved immune mechanism — pathogens need iron to replicate. It also means supplementing iron during active infection is largely futile and may theoretically feed pathogens. Pause supplementation during acute illness.

Convincing a GP of this can be frustrating — the "in range" ferritin trap catches many people.

Iron Overload: The Other Side of the Equation

The body has no regulated mechanism for excreting excess iron — losses occur only through menstruation, blood donation, cell shedding, and minor GI losses. Once absorbed, excess iron is stored or circulates. High circulating iron generates hydroxyl radicals via the Fenton reaction, causing oxidative damage to cells, DNA, and mitochondria.

Haemochromatosis — genetic iron overload — affects approximately 1 in 200 people of Northern European ancestry (homozygous C282Y mutation in HFE gene). Many are undiagnosed. Supplementing iron in these individuals can accelerate serious organ damage (liver cirrhosis, cardiomyopathy, diabetes, arthritis).

Men should not supplement iron routinely without evidence of deficiency. Men lose very little iron monthly and are at meaningful risk of accumulating excess iron, particularly as they age. Elevated ferritin in men is associated with increased cardiovascular disease risk and liver disease. Before supplementing iron, men should confirm deficiency with a ferritin test.

For women post-menopause, the same caution applies — menstruation-driven losses cease, so routine supplementation is no longer appropriate without testing.

Practical Supplementation Protocol

For confirmed iron deficiency (ferritin below 30 ng/mL with symptoms):

  1. Form: Ferrous bisglycinate 25–50 mg elemental iron, or ferrous sulphate 200 mg (65 mg elemental) if cost is the constraint
  2. Timing: On an empty stomach (or with a small amount of food if intolerance is severe), with 200 mg vitamin C or a glass of orange juice
  3. Avoid at the same time: Coffee, tea, calcium supplements, dairy, antacids, PPIs, other minerals (zinc competes with iron for the same transporter)
  4. Frequency: Once daily or alternate-day (not twice daily — hepcidin will block it)
  5. Duration: 3–6 months to replete stores (haemoglobin typically normalises in 4–8 weeks; ferritin takes longer)
  6. Retest: Check ferritin at 3 months; continue until ferritin is above 50 ng/mL and symptoms resolve
  7. Address the cause: If you're deficient without a clear explanation (not menstruating heavily, not vegan, not pregnant), investigate why — occult GI blood loss (bowel cancer screening) or coeliac disease should be considered

The Bottom Line

Iron deficiency is extremely common but routinely missed because standard blood tests only catch Stage 3. Ask for ferritin specifically. If you supplement, timing and form matter enormously — ferrous bisglycinate taken with vitamin C on an empty stomach is far more effective than ferrous sulphate swallowed with morning coffee. Alternate-day dosing beats twice-daily. And if you're male or post-menopausal, test before supplementing — you may not need it, and excess iron does real harm.

Vitamin K2: MK-4 vs MK-7, the Rotterdam Study, and Why K2 Is Not K1

By Obsessive Nutrition · March 2026 · ~2,800 words

Most people taking vitamin D are probably not taking K2. That's a problem — not a marketing problem, but a biochemical one. K2 activates the proteins that determine where calcium goes in the body. Without it, calcium absorbed in the gut may end up in arterial walls rather than bone. This is not theoretical. The Rotterdam Heart Study tracked it over 10 years in 4,807 people. Here's what it found, what K2 actually does, and how to use it correctly.

The Difference Between K1 and K2 (It's Not Marketing)

Vitamin K is a fat-soluble vitamin that exists in two main forms: K1 (phylloquinone) and K2 (menaquinone). They share a chemical backbone but behave very differently in the body.

Vitamin K1 is found primarily in leafy green vegetables — kale, spinach, broccoli. It functions mainly in the liver, where it activates clotting factors. If you take warfarin (a blood thinner that works by antagonising K1), you're told to limit leafy greens because they affect your INR. K1 is the vitamin your liver cares about.

Vitamin K2 is found primarily in fermented foods and animal products — particularly natto (fermented soybeans), hard cheeses, egg yolks, and liver. K2 functions primarily in bone and vascular tissue, where it activates two critical proteins:

  • Osteocalcin: produced by osteoblasts (bone-building cells), osteocalcin requires K2 activation (carboxylation) to bind calcium and incorporate it into bone matrix. Without K2, osteocalcin exists in the blood in an undercarboxylated, inactive form — unable to mineralise bone properly.
  • Matrix Gla Protein (MGP): produced by vascular smooth muscle cells, MGP is the body's most powerful inhibitor of arterial calcification. MGP requires K2 to become active. Without adequate K2, MGP remains undercarboxylated and arterial calcium deposits can accumulate.

This is why the K1/K2 distinction matters: K1 barely reaches bone and vascular tissue at meaningful concentrations. K2 does. They are not interchangeable for these purposes, despite both being called "vitamin K."

The Rotterdam Study: The Landmark Evidence

The Rotterdam Heart Study, published in 2004 in the Journal of Nutrition (Geleijnse et al.), followed 4,807 participants aged 55+ for a mean of 10 years. Dietary K2 intake was assessed via food frequency questionnaire and related to cardiovascular outcomes and all-cause mortality.

The key finding: The highest tertile of K2 intake (mean ~45 mcg/day) was associated with:

  • 57% lower risk of dying from cardiovascular disease (RR 0.43, 95% CI 0.24–0.77)
  • 52% lower risk of severe aortic calcification
  • 26% lower all-cause mortality

K1 intake showed no such association. The Rotterdam study found K2, not K1, was the cardioprotective form.

Important caveats: This is an observational study — dietary K2 intake is a proxy for other healthy dietary patterns (fermented foods, quality dairy). It cannot prove causation. The Rotterdam finding needs to be interpreted as hypothesis-generating, not definitive. However, the biological mechanism (MGP activation) is well-established, and the dietary data is consistent with a real effect.

A follow-up Prospect-EPIC cohort study from the Netherlands (Beulens et al., 2009, Atherosclerosis) confirmed the Rotterdam finding in a larger population: dietary K2 intake was inversely associated with coronary calcification.

MK-4 vs MK-7: What the Subtypes Mean

K2 comes in several subtypes called menaquinones, numbered by the length of their side chain: MK-4 through MK-13. The two most relevant for supplementation are MK-4 and MK-7.

For bone and cardiovascular applications: MK-7 is generally preferred in supplementation due to its longer half-life and superior ability to maintain carboxylation of osteocalcin and MGP at lower doses. Taking MK-7 once daily maintains steady plasma levels in a way that MK-4 — cleared within hours — does not.

MK-4 in Japanese research: High-dose MK-4 (1,500 mcg/day — 1.5 mg/day) has been used in Japanese clinical trials for osteoporosis treatment with significant results, including fracture risk reduction. This dose is far higher than typical MK-7 supplementation. The Shidara et al. 2006 trial in N Engl J Med correspondence, and the Inoue et al. 2009 JBMR meta-analysis, support high-dose MK-4 for bone in the Japanese clinical setting — but this is a pharmaceutical dose, not a dietary supplement dose, and the studies use MK-4 specifically.

Bone Density Evidence

The bone evidence for K2 is strong enough that Japan has approved MK-4 as a pharmaceutical agent (Glakay) for osteoporosis treatment.

Meta-analysis evidence (Cockayne et al., 2006, AORM): A systematic review of 13 randomised trials found supplemental vitamin K2 reduced bone loss significantly. Pooled data showed K2 reduced vertebral fracture risk by 60%, hip fracture risk by 77%, and non-vertebral fractures by 81% — though most studies were Japanese, using high-dose MK-4, and had methodological limitations.

A 2019 systematic review (van Ballegooijen et al., Nutrition): Examined MK-7 supplementation specifically. Doses of 90–360 mcg/day MK-7 improved osteocalcin carboxylation (a marker of K2 status) and reduced undercarboxylated osteocalcin. Some trials showed improvements in bone mineral density (BMD), particularly in postmenopausal women. Effect sizes were modest — K2 is not a substitute for adequate calcium, vitamin D, and weight-bearing exercise.

The Kaneki et al. mechanism work: Showed that the degree of osteocalcin carboxylation correlates with hip fracture risk in elderly Japanese women — providing a mechanistic link between K2 status and bone outcomes.

The D3 + K2 Synergy: Why This Matters

Vitamin D3 increases calcium absorption from the gut. This is its primary function and why D3 is so important for bone health. However, increased calcium absorption creates a question: where does that calcium go?

The answer depends on your K2 status. With adequate K2:

  • Osteocalcin is carboxylated → can bind calcium → deposits into bone matrix
  • MGP is carboxylated → actively inhibits arterial calcification

With inadequate K2:

  • Undercarboxylated osteocalcin cannot bind calcium effectively
  • Undercarboxylated MGP cannot prevent arterial calcium deposition
  • Absorbed calcium has nowhere to go in bone → may deposit in soft tissue and arteries

This is the basis of the "calcium paradox" — the observation in some studies that calcium supplementation increased cardiovascular events (meta-analysis by Bolland et al., 2010, BMJ: 1.3x increased MI risk). The hypothesis is that supplemental calcium without adequate K2 may increase arterial calcification risk, particularly in K2-deficient populations.

The practical implication: if you're taking high-dose vitamin D3 (which increases calcium absorption), K2 is the logical co-supplement. If you're supplementing calcium (especially above 500mg/day), K2 is even more important.

How K2-Deficient Are We?

Western diets are substantially lower in K2 than traditional Asian diets (particularly Japanese diets heavy in natto) or historical European diets (higher in fermented dairy). Multiple studies measuring undercarboxylated osteocalcin (ucOC — a marker of K2 insufficiency) in Western populations find:

  • 40–60% of healthy Western adults show elevated ucOC, indicating functional K2 insufficiency
  • Elderly populations: even higher prevalence (>60%)
  • Postmenopausal women: highest risk group

Dietary sources that provide meaningful K2:

  • Natto: 900–1,100 mcg MK-7 per 100g — by far the richest source, but acquired taste
  • Hard cheeses (Gouda, Edam): 20–75 mcg MK-4+MK-7 per 100g
  • Soft cheeses (Brie, Camembert): 5–15 mcg per 100g
  • Egg yolks: 15–30 mcg MK-4 per 100g
  • Chicken (dark meat): 10–15 mcg MK-4 per 100g
  • Butter/full-fat dairy: Low but meaningful contribution

Unless you eat natto regularly or consume significant quantities of hard cheese and egg yolks, meeting the Rotterdam threshold of ~45 mcg/day from diet alone is difficult for most Westerners.

What the Rotterdam Study Actually Found People Were Eating

The protective K2 intake in the Rotterdam Study came primarily from hard cheese (Gouda-type cheese is a significant source in the Netherlands) — not from supplements. The participants in the highest tertile were consuming roughly 45 mcg MK-7 equivalent daily from food. This is achievable in a Dutch diet but difficult in many other Western dietary patterns where fermented dairy intake is lower.

Dosing Protocol

No official RDA exists for K2 in most countries. The adequate intake (AI) for total vitamin K (primarily K1) is 90–120 mcg/day for adults. K2-specific recommendations are not established by government health bodies — the evidence base, while strong, is not considered sufficient for formal guidance.

Knapen et al. 2013 (Osteoporosis International): The landmark MK-7 trial — 244 postmenopausal women, 180 mcg MK-7/day vs placebo for 3 years. Result: significantly reduced bone loss at femoral neck and lumbar spine. Osteocalcin carboxylation significantly improved. The 180 mcg/day dose fully saturated the carboxylation status, whereas lower doses did not. This suggests 100 mcg/day may be adequate for many purposes but 180 mcg is more reliably effective for bone.

Safety Profile

Vitamin K2 has an excellent safety profile. There is no established tolerable upper intake level (UL) for K2 — meaning no toxicity level has been identified in clinical research even at supplemental doses significantly above dietary intake.

Warfarin interaction — important: All forms of vitamin K, including K2, interact with warfarin by competing with its mechanism of action. Anyone on warfarin or other vitamin K antagonists must consult their physician before taking K2 supplements. K2 is not contraindicated on warfarin but requires careful monitoring and dose adjustment — do not self-supplement.

No interaction with novel anticoagulants (DOACs) like rivaroxaban, apixaban, or dabigatran — these work via different mechanisms and are not affected by vitamin K intake.

Theoretical concern about hypercalcaemia at very high D3 + K2 doses has not materialised in clinical trials; the K2 + D3 combination appears safer than high D3 alone for this reason.

Practical Recommendations

Who should consider K2 supplementation:

  • Anyone taking vitamin D3 supplements at doses of 2,000 IU/day or higher
  • Postmenopausal women concerned about bone density
  • People with cardiovascular risk factors who want to address arterial calcification risk
  • Anyone supplementing calcium (especially above 500mg/day)
  • People with low dietary intake of fermented dairy, natto, and egg yolks
  • Elderly adults (where K2 insufficiency rates are highest)

Who doesn't need to supplement:

  • Regular natto consumers (getting 500–1,000+ mcg MK-7/day from food)
  • People eating substantial hard cheese daily with a varied diet (may be meeting thresholds from food)
  • Anyone on warfarin or K-antagonist anticoagulants without physician guidance

Optimal supplement form: MK-7, either standalone or in combination with D3. MK-7 derived from natto (the natural fermentation source) is the most studied form. Synthetic MK-7 is also available and appears bioequivalent. Take with a fat-containing meal — K2 is fat-soluble and absorption improves significantly with dietary fat.

Timing: Once daily is sufficient due to MK-7's 72-hour half-life. Morning with breakfast (containing some fat) is practical for most people.

The bottom line: If you're supplementing vitamin D3 and not taking K2, you're missing half the picture. The Rotterdam data is observational but the mechanism is solid. K2 is cheap, safe, and the dietary gap in most Western populations is real. At 100–200 mcg MK-7/day, this is one of the most defensible supplements in a thoughtful stack.

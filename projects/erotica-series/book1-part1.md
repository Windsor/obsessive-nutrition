# THE FIFTH FLOOR — Book One: THE SALON

## Part One: The Invitation

---

# Chapter One: What Lay Beneath

The fragment of the Virgin's robe came away like a whisper, like the last syllable of a prayer spoken by someone who had already stopped believing.

Vivienne Cross held her breath and pressed the cotton swab — damp with a solution of acetone and mineral spirits, the ratio calibrated by her own hand, because she trusted no one else's hand for this — against the surface of the azulejo tile, and beneath the thin crust of eighteenth-century overpainting, something appeared that should not have been there.

A nipple.

She set down the swab. She adjusted the magnifying lens on its articulated arm, swinging it closer until the tile filled her vision, every crackle and fissure in the glaze magnified into a landscape. Yes. Unmistakable. Beneath the pale blue drapery of the Madonna's cloak, which had been painted sometime around 1780 — she could date the pigment by its mineral composition, by the particular shade of cobalt they had used, the same shade that marked a dozen panels throughout the Museu Nacional do Azulejo — beneath that pious addition lay the original image, and the original image was a woman's bare breast.

Not just bare. Offered. The curve of the flesh had been rendered with extraordinary tenderness by the original painter, the aureole a warm terracotta against cream, the nipple drawn with a single, confident brushstroke that suggested the artist had been looking at an actual breast when he — or she, Vivienne thought, though she knew the odds — had set the pigment to the glaze. It was not an idealized breast, not the smooth porcelain orb of a Renaissance Venus. It was specific. Slightly asymmetrical. The kind of breast you knew because you had held it, or owned it, or wanted it with the kind of wanting that burned a permanent shape into the back of your eyes.

She pulled back from the magnifying lens and looked at the full panel. It was a standard religious scene — or it had been, once someone had painted over the original to make it so. Our Lady of the Conception, surrounded by angels, standing on a crescent moon. Two metres by one and a half, composed of approximately two hundred individual tiles, each fifteen centimetres square. Vivienne had been working on its restoration for three weeks. The museum had acquired it from a private collection in Évora, and the acquisition records described it simply as *Painel de Nossa Senhora da Conceição, c. 1740, artista desconhecido.*

Artist unknown. But the artist had known things. Had known, specifically, how a woman's body looked when it was not posed for devotion but for desire.

Vivienne's pulse was doing something it should not have been doing in a conservation laboratory at half past ten on a Tuesday morning. She was aware of it the way she was always aware of her body's inconvenient responses — with a kind of clinical displeasure, as though her skin were a garment that fit badly and she could not quite take off. She breathed through it. She was good at that. Breathing through things was, if not her greatest skill, then certainly her most practiced one.

She picked up the swab again.

Over the next two hours, working with the patience that had defined her career and the care that had ruined her marriage — Greg had once said, in one of their final arguments, that she treated seventeenth-century Portuguese tiles with more tenderness than she had ever shown him, and she had not been able to deny it, because it was true — Vivienne uncovered the rest.

The panel was not a religious scene. It had never been a religious scene. Beneath the madonnas and the angels and the crescent moons, the original painting depicted a garden. A garden full of figures. And the figures were fucking.

There was no other word for it, though Vivienne's mind, trained in the polite vocabulary of art history, tried several. *Engaged in erotic congress. Depicted in states of physical intimacy. Performing acts of —* No. They were fucking. A woman straddled a man on a stone bench, her head thrown back, her hair loose, her hands gripping his shoulders while his hands cupped her buttocks with unmistakable intent. To their left, two men embraced beneath an orange tree, one pressing the other against the trunk, their legs intertwined, one man's hand disappearing beneath the other's loosened breeches. In the foreground, a woman reclined alone on a chaise draped in silk, her skirts hiked to her waist, one hand between her thighs, her face turned toward the viewer with an expression that was neither shame nor invitation but something more unsettling: absorption. She was lost in herself. She did not care who watched.

Vivienne realised she had been staring at this last figure for a very long time.

She set down her tools. She removed her cotton gloves, folded them, placed them in the shallow ceramic dish beside her work station. She took off her magnifying visor and hung it on its hook. She pulled the light away from the panel and switched it off. And then she stood there, in the cool, climate-controlled silence of the conservation laboratory, and let the heat that had been building in her chest and her belly and the tender, treacherous space between her thighs simply exist without trying to argue it away.

The woman on the chaise had her eyes half closed and her lips parted and her fingers were buried in the soft shadow of herself, and the artist had painted her with love. Not lust — or not only lust. Love. The kind of attention that sees every detail and renders it faithfully, because the subject is sacred. Because the body is sacred.

Vivienne thought about Greg. About the way he had touched her — competently, efficiently, the way you might assemble a piece of furniture by following the instructions without ever looking at the picture on the box. She thought about the years she had spent lying beneath him, performing the sounds and movements she understood were expected, while the real Vivienne — the one with the vivid, sprawling, uncontrollable interior — masturbated in her mind to things she could never say aloud. Women. Women's mouths. Women's hands. The way a woman's back curved when she arched into pleasure. The way a woman sounded when she came.

She had never told Greg. She had never told anyone. It lived inside her like the original painting beneath the overpainting — visible, if you knew how to look, but buried under decades of careful concealment.

She locked the laboratory and went to find Luís.

---

Luís Ferreira was the kind of Portuguese man who made Vivienne reconsider, briefly and without conviction, her suspicion that she might not be attracted to men at all. He was fifty-three, silver-bearded, with hands scarred by decades of working with kilns and chemicals and the sharp edges of broken tiles. He was the museum's senior conservator and Vivienne's supervisor, and he had the rare quality of treating her expertise with respect rather than the faintly patronising deference she encountered from most European men when confronted with a young American woman who knew more about their country's artistic heritage than they did.

He was also, she had come to understand over the three months she had been in Lisbon, a man who noticed things. He noticed when she was tired, and brought her coffee without being asked. He noticed when she was frustrated, and left her alone. And today, when she found him in his office and told him about the panel — about the overpainting, about the original image, about the garden full of figures in various configurations of desire — he noticed something else.

He noticed that she was aroused.

He didn't say so. He was far too Portuguese for that. But his dark eyes lingered on her face a moment longer than usual, and when he spoke, his voice had a quality she had not heard before — a warmth that was also a question.

"Show me," he said.

She showed him. They stood together in the laboratory, and Luís examined the sections she had uncovered with professional concentration while Vivienne narrated her findings: the pigment analysis, the dating of the overpainting, her theory that the original commission had been private and the religious concealment applied later, perhaps when the panel changed hands, perhaps when the political or moral climate shifted.

"The Marquês de Pombal's era," Luís said, tracing a fingertip above the surface of the tiles without touching them, the conservator's instinctive restraint. "After the earthquake of 1755, there was a great deal of — how do you say — *moralização*. Moralizing. The Church said the earthquake was God's punishment for Lisbon's sins. Many private collections were... sanitized." He glanced at her. "The sins they were most concerned with were, of course, these ones."

He gestured at the panel. The woman on the chaise, half-uncovered, her fingers still hidden in shadow. The men beneath the orange tree. The couple on the bench, her body arched over his, frozen in mid-motion.

"It's extraordinary," Vivienne said, and her voice was steady, because she had made it steady, because she had thirty-two years of practice at making her voice do what she wanted rather than what she felt. "The quality of the original painting far exceeds the overpainting. Whoever did this — the original — they were a master. Look at the glazework. The flesh tones. The translucency."

"Yes," Luís said. He was looking at her again, not at the panel. "There is a tradition of this, you know. Erotic azulejos. Most are in private collections. Some in the palaces of the old aristocracy. There was a fashion for it in the mid-eighteenth century — Portuguese society was... more expansive in its appetites than the Church preferred."

"I'd like to research further. If there are comparable panels —"

"There are." He straightened. Rolled down his sleeves. "Some I can show you in the museum archives. Others..." He paused, and the pause had the quality of a door being held open. "Others you would need to see in their original context."

Vivienne waited.

"Come," Luís said. "I think we have earned a ginjinha."

---

They went to Alfama.

It was late afternoon, and the light in Lisbon was doing the thing it did at that hour — turning everything gold. Not the gold of postcards, though the postcards tried; this was deeper, more saturated, the light of a city that had been catching the Atlantic sun on its white walls and terracotta roofs for a thousand years and had absorbed it into its bones. Vivienne walked beside Luís through the narrow streets of the oldest bairro, the streets that had survived the great earthquake because Alfama sat on bedrock, on the bones of the Moorish city, streets so narrow that the buildings on either side almost touched and the sky above was a sliver of deepening blue, and she thought, as she always thought when she walked in Lisbon, that this city understood something about beauty that most cities had forgotten — that beauty was not perfection but accumulation, the layering of centuries, the crumbling stucco revealing the stone beneath, the azulejo tiles cracked and faded but still singing with colour, the bougainvillea exploding purple from a wrought-iron balcony, the sound of someone practicing fado in an upstairs room, the voice raw and true and full of the particular Portuguese ache that had its own word — *saudade* — because no other language had found a single word for longing-that-is-also-love-that-is-also-loss.

Luís took her to a bar she had not been to before, a tiny place on a street so steep it was almost a staircase, with a counter barely wide enough for two people and a woman behind it who greeted Luís by name and poured two glasses of ginjinha without being asked. The liqueur was thick, almost syrupy, the colour of dark garnets, and it burned sweetly going down, leaving a residue of sour cherry and alcohol that coated the inside of Vivienne's mouth like a second skin.

"Saúde," Luís said, raising his glass.

"Saúde." She drank. The warmth spread through her chest, loosening something that had been tight all day.

They talked about the panel. Luís told her about the tradition of erotic art in Portuguese decorative ceramics — the *azulejos eróticos* that had once adorned the private rooms of palaces and quintas throughout the country. Most had been destroyed or painted over during the various waves of moral panic that had swept through Portuguese history — the Inquisition, the post-earthquake piety, the Salazar dictatorship, which had been puritanical in public while its officials indulged in private. But some had survived. Some were still in the places they had been made for, hidden behind walls, beneath new panels, in locked rooms.

"There is one place," Luís said, and then he stopped.

Vivienne waited. She was good at waiting. Restoration was, fundamentally, an act of patience — you waited for the solvents to soften the surface, waited for the layers to reveal themselves, waited for the original to emerge. You could not force it. You could only create the conditions for revelation.

Luís turned his ginjinha glass slowly on the counter. The woman behind the bar was wiping glasses and not looking at them and very obviously listening.

"There is a place in Alfama," he said again, more quietly. "An old palácio. It has... a collection. Not a museum collection. A living collection. The tiles there are original, never overpainted, never hidden. They have been preserved exactly as they were intended to be seen." He looked at her, and in his dark eyes she saw something she could not immediately name — not desire, exactly, but a recognition. As though he were seeing in her something she had not yet seen in herself. "It is a private place. Not open to the public. But the people there — they understand about things that have been hidden. About things that deserve to be uncovered."

"How do I find it?"

He wrote an address on a napkin. Rua das Flores do Desejo. She did not recognise the street name, but she understood the Portuguese — Street of the Flowers of Desire — and she thought it sounded invented, the kind of name that belonged in a novel, not on a map.

"Go on a Friday evening," Luís said. "After ten. There is no sign. The door is unmarked. The building has azulejos on the façade — santos. Saints." He smiled, and the smile had an edge she had not seen before. "Do not let the saints discourage you."

He finished his ginjinha and changed the subject, and they talked about the museum's upcoming exhibition, and the difficulty of sourcing period-appropriate pigments, and the latest academic scandal involving a forged attribution, and Vivienne listened and responded and asked questions and laughed at the right moments, and all the while the napkin sat in her jacket pocket like a coal that would not cool.

---

She walked home alone through Alfama as the light faded. The streets emptied of tourists and filled with residents — women calling to each other from balconies, old men playing cards at a table outside a tascas, a boy kicking a football against a wall with rhythmic, meditative precision. From somewhere above, the sound of a guitar, the twelve-string *guitarra portuguesa* with its bright, yearning tone, and then a voice — a woman's voice, singing fado, and Vivienne stopped in the middle of the street and listened.

The song was about absence. She did not understand all the words — her Portuguese was functional but imperfect, built for reading art history texts rather than for parsing the elliptical poetry of fado lyrics — but she understood enough. The singer was singing about someone who had gone, and the space they had left behind, and the way that space was not empty but full of their absence, which was a kind of presence, which was a kind of pain that was also, somehow, a kind of pleasure, because to miss someone that much was proof that you had been capable of loving them, and to be capable of love was the only thing that made being alive bearable.

*Saudade.*

Vivienne thought about the woman on the azulejo panel. The one reclining alone on the chaise, touching herself with unselfconscious absorption. She thought about the way the artist had rendered the flesh of her inner thigh — warm, shadowed, the skin slightly flushed, the finest crackle in the glaze running across it like veins in marble. She thought about the woman's face. The half-closed eyes. The parted lips. The expression that said: *I am here, inside myself, and it is enough.*

She reached into her pocket and touched the napkin. The address. Rua das Flores do Desejo.

She kept walking. Past the Sé cathedral, massive and pale in the dusk. Past the miradors where couples sat on benches and looked out at the Tagus, the river enormous and silver in the fading light, the bridge a string of lights connecting one shore to the other, the statue of Christ on the far side with his arms spread wide as though embracing the whole sprawling, aching city.

In her apartment in Graça — a small, bright flat on the top floor of a building that tilted perceptibly toward the river, with a terrace the size of a prayer rug and a view that made her cry the first time she saw it — she ate a simple dinner of bread and cheese and *presunto* and olives, and drank a glass of vinho verde, and sat on her terrace and watched the lights come on across the river, and thought about what it would mean to walk through an unmarked door in Alfama and see what was on the other side.

She thought about the panel. The overpainting. The way someone had looked at that extraordinary, vivid, brave depiction of human desire and decided it needed to be hidden. Not destroyed — hidden. As though the person who painted over it had understood its beauty but believed that beauty was dangerous, that it needed to be contained, that it was better to see the Virgin Mary's blue cloak than the breast beneath it, even though the breast was real and the cloak was a lie.

She had been the overpainting her whole life. She had painted herself over so many times that she was not entirely sure what lay beneath.

She went to bed. She did not touch herself, though she wanted to. She lay on her back in the dark and felt the want like a physical presence in the room, heavy and warm and alive, and she let it be there without answering it, the way she always did, because answering it would mean acknowledging it, and acknowledging it would mean she was someone she was not ready to be.

She fell asleep with the napkin on her bedside table and the taste of ginjinha still on her tongue and the sound of fado drifting up from the street below, and in her dreams the woman on the azulejo chaise opened her eyes and looked directly at her and said, in Portuguese that Vivienne somehow perfectly understood: *Vem. Come.*

# Chapter Two: The Door on the Rua das Flores do Desejo

Friday came slowly, the way Fridays do when you are waiting for them.

Vivienne worked on the panel. She uncovered more of the original painting, centimetre by painstaking centimetre, working with swabs and solvents and the finest scalpels, peeling back the pious overpainting to reveal the garden beneath. Each new section confirmed what the first discovery had promised: the original was a masterwork. The figures were rendered with an anatomical precision that suggested direct observation — these were not the idealised bodies of classical painting but real bodies, with real weight and texture, soft bellies and strong thighs and the creases that flesh makes when it folds and presses against other flesh. The garden setting was lush, the vegetation painted with a botanical accuracy that Vivienne could almost identify — pomegranate and fig and orange trees, the traditional fruits of Portuguese gardens, laden and ripe.

She documented everything with the methodical care her training required — photographs at each stage, detailed notes on pigment composition and brushwork, measurements, condition reports. But the professional notation could not quite contain what she was seeing. In the margins of her notebook, she found herself writing things that were not part of any standard conservation report. *The quality of light on the woman's inner arm — golden, warm, the glaze technique almost identical to Josefa de Óbidos but 50 years later.* And: *The man's hand on the other man's hip — the pressure of the fingers. You can see the dimple where the thumb presses into flesh. The artist knew what it felt like to hold someone like this.* And, on Thursday evening, when she uncovered a new section that showed a woman kneeling between another woman's spread thighs, her face buried in the soft darkness there, both their faces invisible but their bodies speaking a language that needed no translation: *God.*

She did not show Luís these notes.

On Friday morning, she woke early and lay in bed and watched the light strengthen on the ceiling and told herself she was not going. She told herself this while choosing what to wear, standing in front of her small wardrobe in her underwear, holding up dress after dress and rejecting each one, until she understood that she was not selecting an outfit for a woman who was staying home. She chose a black dress — simple, well-cut, a dress she had bought in New York three years ago for an exhibition opening and had worn exactly twice. It was the kind of dress that made her feel like someone other than herself, which, she thought, might be the point.

She spent the day at the museum. She could not concentrate. Her hands, usually so steady, trembled when she picked up her finest brush, and she set it down again and stared at the panel and thought about the evening ahead with a nauseating mix of anticipation and terror that sat in her stomach like a stone dropped into deep water — she could feel it sinking, slowly, pulling everything else down with it.

At six o'clock she left the museum. She went home. She showered, spending too long under the water, the heat softening her muscles and loosening the knots in her shoulders and back. She did not allow herself to think about why she was shaving her legs so carefully, drawing the razor up the long line of her calf, over the curve of her knee, along the sensitive skin of her inner thigh. She did not allow herself to think about anything at all. She dried her hair. She put on the black dress. She looked at herself in the mirror.

The woman in the mirror looked frightened. She also looked, Vivienne had to admit, beautiful — not beautiful in the way that Greg had called her beautiful, which had always felt like a compliment paid to a photograph rather than a person, but beautiful in the way that a thing is beautiful when it is about to change, when it stands at the edge of itself, trembling.

She put on lipstick. She took it off. She put it on again.

At ten o'clock she left the apartment and walked down the hill toward Alfama.

---

The night was warm. Lisbon in early October still held the memory of summer, the stones of the buildings radiating the heat they had absorbed during the day, the air soft and fragrant with jasmine and the salt tang of the river. Vivienne walked through Graça and then down through the winding streets toward the Castelo de São Jorge, the fortress lit up on the hilltop like a crown, and then down further, into the dense, tangled web of Alfama.

She had looked up the address. Rua das Flores do Desejo did exist, though it was not on Google Maps — she had found it eventually on an old city plan archived at the municipal library, a pre-earthquake map that showed the street as a narrow lane running between the Rua de São Miguel and the Rua de São Pedro, parallel to the river, in the oldest part of the bairro. The fact that it did not appear on modern maps should have been a warning, she thought. Or a sign. She was not sure which.

She found it almost by accident. She had been walking in circles — the streets of Alfama doubled back on themselves, spiralling inward like the chambers of a shell, and she kept emerging into the same small square with the same fountain, dry and covered in blue and white tiles depicting fish — when a narrow passage opened to her left, barely wider than her shoulders, unlit except for the glow from a window high above, and she turned into it because she had nowhere else to turn, and after twenty steps the passage opened into a tiny courtyard, and there it was.

The palácio.

It did not look like a palace. It looked like every other building in Alfama — four or five storeys tall, the façade of crumbling plaster and azulejo tiles, a heavy wooden door, a wrought-iron balcony on the second floor trailing bougainvillea. But the tiles on the façade were not the standard blue-and-white decorative patterns that covered half the buildings in Lisbon. They depicted saints. Rows of saints, their eyes cast upward in various attitudes of ecstasy and torment, their hands clasped in prayer or raised in blessing, their bodies wrapped in robes that seemed, Vivienne noticed with a restorer's eye, to cling and drape in ways that were not quite devotional. Saint Sebastian, especially — bound to his tree, his body pierced with arrows, his head thrown back, his lips parted — looked less like a man being martyred and more like a man in the grip of something he had stopped trying to resist.

*Do not let the saints discourage you,* Luís had said.

Vivienne stood in the courtyard and looked at the door and almost turned around.

It was not fear, exactly. Or it was fear, but fear of a particular kind — not the fear of danger but the fear of recognition. The fear that she would walk through that door and find something she had been looking for without knowing she was looking for it, and that finding it would mean she could never go back to not knowing it existed.

She thought about the panel. The woman alone on the chaise, touching herself with that expression of perfect, unselfconscious absorption.

She thought about every night she had lain in her bed beside Greg, performing stillness while her mind roamed through landscapes of desire he would never see.

She thought about the overpainting. About how someone had decided that truth was too dangerous to display, and had covered it with something acceptable, something safe, something *appropriate,* and how for two hundred and forty years that decision had held, until Vivienne had pressed a damp cotton swab against the surface and the lie had begun to dissolve.

She walked to the door. There was no sign, no nameplate, no bell — only a brass knocker in the shape of a hand, a woman's hand, the fingers curled as though beckoning. She lifted it and let it fall.

The sound was deeper than she expected, resonant, the kind of sound that travels through stone.

The door opened.

A man stood on the other side — young, perhaps twenty-five, with dark curly hair and the kind of face that belonged on one of the azulejo saints outside. He wore a simple white shirt and dark trousers and he smiled at her with an expression of warmth so genuine that Vivienne felt her shoulders drop an inch.

"Boa noite," he said. "Bem-vinda." *Good evening. Welcome.*

"Boa noite," Vivienne said. Her Portuguese felt clumsy in her mouth. "I was — Luís Ferreira suggested I —"

"Yes," the young man said, as though this were all the explanation required. "Please. Come in."

---

The hallway beyond the door was narrow and long and lit by candles — real candles, not the electric imitations, actual flames burning in sconces on the walls, the light flickering against more azulejo tiles, these ones depicting not saints but flowers, cascades of flowers in blue and white and yellow and green, roses and lilies and jasmine and others she could not name, climbing the walls from floor to ceiling as though the corridor were a pathway through an impossibly vertical garden.

The young man led her forward. The floor was tiled too, a geometric pattern in black and white that made her think of the cloisters she had visited at the Jerónimos Monastery in Belém, the same sense of sacred geometry, of a space designed to guide the eye and the body toward something beyond itself.

At the end of the corridor, a staircase rose to the left, curving upward and out of sight. Ahead, a pair of double doors stood open, and from beyond them came the sound of conversation and music — a guitar, the twelve-string *guitarra portuguesa*, being played softly and with great skill — and the warm amber glow of more candlelight, and the scent of something rich and sweet that she identified after a moment as ginjinha.

"O Salão," the young man said, gesturing toward the open doors. *The Salon.* "Please, make yourself comfortable. Someone will bring you a drink."

He disappeared back down the corridor, and Vivienne was alone.

She stepped through the doors.

The room took her breath. Not because it was large — it was, in fact, surprisingly intimate, perhaps ten metres by fifteen — but because every surface had been designed to be beautiful and the cumulative effect was overwhelming, the way a cathedral overwhelms, not through any single element but through the totality of care. The walls were covered in azulejo panels, and these panels — Vivienne understood immediately — were the real thing. Not the overpainted, sanitized versions she spent her days uncovering. These were originals, preserved in their full, unabashed glory.

The tiles depicted scenes of pleasure. The same garden motif she had found beneath the museum panel, but larger, more elaborate, an entire world painted in the exquisite blue-and-white tradition of Portuguese tilework. Men and women, women and women, men and men, alone and in pairs and in groups, in gardens and bedrooms and bathhouses, their bodies rendered with that same loving anatomical precision she had admired in the museum panel. Every position, every configuration, every shade of human desire laid out across the walls in glazed ceramic, the images flowing from one to the next without interruption, a continuous panorama of the body's possibilities.

Vivienne stood in the doorway and stared.

The rest of the room was furnished with an elegance that belonged to another century but did not feel anachronistic — low sofas upholstered in deep jewel tones, velvet and silk, scattered with cushions; small tables of dark polished wood bearing candelabras and crystal decanters; thick rugs on the tiled floor; curtains of heavy brocade framing tall windows that must have overlooked the courtyard. The lighting was entirely candle-flame — dozens of candles, perhaps hundreds, on every surface, in sconces, in a chandelier of wrought iron and crystal that hung from the vaulted ceiling. The effect was warm, intimate, tremulous — a room that breathed.

And the people. There were perhaps twenty people in the room, seated on the sofas and standing in small groups, and they were, Vivienne realised, the most beautiful people she had ever seen. Not uniformly — not the manufactured beauty of fashion or film but the beauty of variety, of specificity, of people who inhabited their bodies with total ease. A woman in her fifties with silver hair piled loosely on her head, wearing a green silk dress, laughing at something a younger man had said. A couple — two men, both dark-haired, one tall and one compact — standing by the window with their hands intertwined, speaking in low Portuguese. A woman sitting alone, reading — actually reading — a book, a glass of something amber on the table beside her, her legs tucked beneath her. All of them clothed, all of them composed, all of them radiating a quality that Vivienne could only describe as *presence* — they were here, fully, in their bodies and their attention.

A woman appeared at her elbow — another Keeper, Vivienne would later learn — with a tray bearing a single crystal glass of ginjinha. "Bem-vinda," she said softly, and placed the glass in Vivienne's hand, and was gone.

Vivienne drank. The ginjinha was different from the one she and Luís had drunk in the Alfama bar — richer, deeper, with a complexity that suggested age and care, cherries that had steeped for years in alcohol and sugar and time. It burned going down and then spread warmth through her chest and her belly, and she thought: *This is a place where everything is real. The candles are real. The tiles are real. The drink is real. Nothing here is pretending to be something else.*

She moved further into the room. Found a place on a sofa near the wall where she could sit with her back protected and watch. Watching was what she was good at. Watching was what she had always done — at parties, at gatherings, in her marriage. She was the woman at the edge of the room, the one with the seeing eyes and the still hands, the one who noticed everything and participated in nothing.

The guitarist was playing in the corner — a man of sixty or so, his face weathered and kind, his fingers moving over the strings with the casual virtuosity of someone who has been playing for so long that the music comes from the same place as breathing. He was playing a fado melody, something Vivienne half-recognised, and a woman nearby was humming along, her eyes closed, swaying slightly, and the music filled the room the way the candlelight filled it, golden and warm and alive.

Vivienne watched. She watched the woman with the silver hair lean forward and whisper something in the young man's ear that made him blush and grin. She watched the two men by the window, the taller one lifting the shorter one's hand to his lips and kissing the knuckles one by one. She watched the reading woman turn a page without looking up, completely self-contained, and envied her.

And then she saw Mariana.

She did not know, yet, that the woman's name was Mariana. She did not know anything about her except what her eyes told her, which was: *This. This is what you came here for.*

The woman was standing near the far wall, beneath a panel depicting two women in a bathhouse, their bodies intertwined in water that the artist had rendered as flowing ribbons of blue and white. She was talking to a man — tall, silver at the temples, with the stillness of a priest or a predator — but as Vivienne looked, the woman turned her head and met Vivienne's eyes across the room, and something happened.

It was not dramatic. No thunderbolt, no cinematic slow-motion. It was quieter than that, and more devastating. The woman looked at Vivienne and Vivienne looked at the woman and something in Vivienne's chest cracked open — not broke, cracked, the way the glaze on an azulejo tile cracks after centuries, fine lines spreading from a central point, a pattern that is itself a kind of beauty, the beauty of a thing that has endured long enough to show its age.

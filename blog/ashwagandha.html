Ashwagandha: The Stress and Cortisol Science Behind the World's Most Popular Adaptogen

KSM-66, Sensoril, and standard root extract — what the randomised controlled trials actually show about cortisol, testosterone, sleep, and thyroid, and when the evidence gets overhyped.

Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera) has been used in Ayurvedic medicine for over 3,000 years. It now sells in the hundreds of millions of dollars annually as the Western supplement market's favourite "stress adaptogen." Whether the evidence supports the marketing depends entirely on which claim you are evaluating — because ashwagandha's effects on cortisol and stress are reasonably well-supported, while several other popular claims are much shakier.

This is what the science actually shows.

What Is an Adaptogen — and Is That a Useful Concept?

The term "adaptogen" was coined in Soviet pharmacology in the 1940s to describe substances that increase the body's non-specific resistance to stress. The concept is not well-defined in modern pharmacology — it describes a functional outcome (stress resistance) rather than a mechanism — but it has proven commercially durable because it maps onto something real: some compounds do appear to modulate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis, which governs the body's stress response.

Ashwagandha's active compounds are primarily withanolides — steroidal lactones unique to the Withania somnifera plant. The specific withanolides believed to be most active include withaferin A, withanolide D, and withanosides. The root extract is the most studied; leaf extracts have a different withanolide profile and somewhat different research support.

Extract Types: KSM-66 vs Sensoril vs Standard Root

This distinction matters more than most supplement guides acknowledge:

Extract Type Source Withanolide % Typical Dose Research Support
KSM-66 Root only ≥5% 300–600mg/day Strongest — most RCTs used this extract
Sensoril Root + leaf ≥10% 125–250mg/day Good — several RCTs, lower dose effective
Shoden Root + leaf ≥35% 120mg/day Limited — promising but fewer trials
Standard root powder Root 1–5% (variable) 3,000–6,000mg/day Some RCTs — inconsistent quality control
Unspecified extract Variable Unknown Variable Cannot evaluate

The key practical point: a supplement label that says "ashwagandha 300mg" with no extract specification tells you very little. KSM-66 and Sensoril are standardised, third-party tested, and have the most consistent trial evidence. They cost more. That cost difference reflects genuine quality variation.

Cortisol and Stress: The Strongest Evidence

This is where ashwagandha's evidence base is most solid.

A well-conducted 2019 RCT in Medicine (Chandrasekhar et al., building on their earlier 2012 work) gave 240mg/day of KSM-66 to chronically stressed adults for 60 days versus placebo. The ashwagandha group showed a 23% reduction in serum cortisol versus 8% in the placebo group (p<0.001), along with significant reductions in Perceived Stress Scale scores, anxiety (DASS-21), and self-reported food cravings under stress.

A 2021 systematic review and meta-analysis in PLOS ONE (Pratte et al.) pooled 5 RCTs (n=400) examining ashwagandha's effect on stress and anxiety. The pooled analysis found a significant reduction in stress scores (SMD −1.05, 95% CI −1.56 to −0.55) with a medium-to-large effect size and low heterogeneity. The quality of the included studies was rated moderate to high.

Mechanism: Withanolides appear to act as partial agonists at GABA-A receptors (similar to but weaker than anxiolytics) and modulate cortisol production by acting on the adrenal cortex and hypothalamus. They may also reduce the inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α) that activate the stress axis.

Effect size in context: The cortisol reduction is real but should be contextualised. Ashwagandha does not reduce cortisol below normal ranges in healthy individuals — it appears to normalise elevated cortisol in chronically stressed people. It is not a cortisol suppressor in the way pharmaceutical interventions are. For someone with genuinely elevated cortisol due to chronic stress, the effect may be meaningful. For someone with normal cortisol, the effect is smaller.

Sleep: Better Evidence Than Expected

Multiple RCTs have shown ashwagandha improves sleep quality, particularly sleep onset and non-restorative sleep, with the effect most pronounced in anxious individuals:

  • A 2021 trial in PLOS ONE (Langade et al.) using 300mg KSM-66 twice daily for 8 weeks found significant improvements in sleep efficiency (p=0.011), sleep onset latency (p=0.02), and total sleep time (p=0.04) versus placebo in adults with insomnia criteria
  • A 2019 study using 120mg/day of Shoden extract showed similar improvements in sleep quality over 8 weeks

The sleep benefit likely flows from the cortisol and anxiolytic effects — reduced evening cortisol levels facilitate easier sleep onset. It is not a sedative. It will not make you drowsy. People who report "better sleep" on ashwagandha typically describe waking less, falling asleep faster, and feeling more rested — not sedation.

Testosterone and Male Fertility

This is one of ashwagandha's more overhyped areas. Here is what the evidence actually shows:

A 2010 study (Mahdi et al.) in 46 men with oligospermia found 675mg/day of ashwagandha root over 3 months increased testosterone by 17% and improved semen parameters (sperm count +167%, motility +57%). This is often cited as evidence that ashwagandha "boosts testosterone." The limitations: small sample, subfertile men only, unblinded, not replicated at scale.

A 2019 RCT in Medicine (Wankhede et al.) gave 300mg KSM-66 twice daily to healthy men undergoing resistance training for 8 weeks. Testosterone increased by ~15% versus ~2.6% in the placebo group (p=0.004). Muscle mass and strength also increased more in the ashwagandha group.

The honest interpretation: Ashwagandha appears to increase testosterone in men with suppressed testosterone (due to stress, overtraining, or poor sleep) by reducing cortisol — which is catabolic to testosterone at chronically elevated levels. This is a cortisol-mediated recovery effect, not a direct androgenic effect. It is not meaningfully ergogenic for men with normal testosterone levels. The effect is modest and unlikely to be perceptible in the absence of a stress/cortisol problem.

For women: the testosterone and fertility data apply to women in a different context — there is some evidence for ashwagandha improving sexual function in women (FSFI scores) via stress reduction, not testosterone elevation.

Thyroid Function

Some studies have reported that ashwagandha increases T3 and T4 levels, making it popular in hypothyroidism communities. This claim needs careful handling:

  • A 2017 study (Sharma et al.) found 600mg/day of ashwagandha root extract for 8 weeks significantly increased T3 (+19.6%) and T4 (+9.3%) versus placebo in adults with subclinical hypothyroidism. TSH also normalised in more participants
  • The mechanism is unclear — possibly via cortisol reduction improving conversion of T4 to T3

Critical warning: Anyone taking thyroid medication should not use ashwagandha without medical supervision. The interaction could cause hyperthyroidism. Those with Hashimoto's thyroiditis (autoimmune thyroid disease) should be particularly cautious — ashwagandha is an immune modulator, and its effects on autoimmune thyroid disease are not well studied. The thyroid effect, while real in some populations, is not a safe home-remedy for hypothyroidism.

Cognitive Function

Two good RCTs support ashwagandha's effect on memory and cognitive function:

  • A 2017 study in the Journal of Dietary Supplements found KSM-66 (300mg twice daily, 8 weeks) improved immediate and general memory, executive function, attention, and information processing speed in healthy adults versus placebo
  • A 2021 study using 600mg KSM-66 daily for 8 weeks found improvements in attention, memory, and psychomotor speed in stressed adults

The cognitive effect likely involves cortisol reduction (high cortisol impairs hippocampal function) plus possibly direct neuroprotective effects via withanolide-A, which has shown neurogenesis-promoting activity in animal models. The human cognitive evidence is preliminary but consistent.

Physical Performance

Beyond the testosterone/resistance training study above, a 2015 trial (Journal of the International Society of Sports Nutrition) found KSM-66 (300mg twice daily, 8 weeks) in healthy male athletes significantly improved VO2 max (6% increase), time to exhaustion, and markers of cardiorespiratory endurance compared to placebo.

A meta-analysis published in 2021 pooled 12 RCTs and found significant improvements in muscle strength and recovery with ashwagandha supplementation, particularly relevant in resistance training contexts. Effect sizes were modest but consistent.

Safety Profile

Ashwagandha has a strong safety record in trials up to 3 months at standard doses. Key considerations:

  • Generally well tolerated: The most commonly reported side effects are mild GI discomfort (nausea, loose stools) — reduced by taking with food
  • Rare liver injury reports: As of 2023, there are approximately 20 published case reports of ashwagandha-associated liver injury, mostly with high doses or extended use. These are rare but notable. Avoid if you have existing liver disease
  • Pregnancy: Contraindicated — withaferin A has abortifacient properties in animal models
  • Thyroid medication interactions: As above — significant interaction risk
  • Immunosuppressants: Ashwagandha has immunomodulatory effects that could interfere with immunosuppressive therapy
  • Sedative medications: Additive CNS-depressant effects possible

Who Should Consider It — and Who Shouldn't

Reasonable candidates:

  • Adults with chronic, documented stress and associated sleep issues
  • Those with elevated cortisol symptoms (poor sleep, weight gain particularly around the abdomen, difficulty recovering from training)
  • Men with stress-related testosterone suppression who are also resistance training
  • Those specifically seeking a non-sedating aid to sleep onset

Poor fit / caution advised:

  • Anyone on thyroid medication
  • Anyone with autoimmune conditions (immunomodulatory effects are unpredictable)
  • Pregnant women
  • Anyone seeking a performance enhancer in the absence of a cortisol/stress problem — the effect is small
  • Anyone with liver disease or elevated liver enzymes

Dosing Protocol

Goal Extract Dose Timing Duration
Stress / cortisol reduction KSM-66 300mg twice daily With meals (morning + evening) 8–12 weeks, then reassess
Sleep improvement KSM-66 or Sensoril 300–500mg 30–60 min before bed 8 weeks minimum
Resistance training / performance KSM-66 600mg/day (split) Morning + pre-workout 8 weeks minimum
Budget option Sensoril 125–250mg/day With food 8–12 weeks

Cycling: The trials typically run 8–12 weeks. There is no strong evidence that continuous use loses efficacy, but given the limited long-term safety data, taking 4–8 week breaks every 2–3 months is a reasonable approach. This is conservative rather than evidence-based.

Bottom Line

Ashwagandha earns its place in the evidence-based supplement stack for people dealing with chronic stress. The cortisol and sleep data from standardised extracts (KSM-66, Sensoril) is reasonably robust — multiple RCTs, consistent effect direction, plausible mechanism. The testosterone and physical performance effects are real but depend heavily on context: they are most meaningful for people who are cortisol-suppressed, poorly recovered, or genuinely overtrained.

What ashwagandha is not: a productivity drug, a testosterone replacement, an anabolic agent, or a thyroid treatment. The gap between the evidence and the marketing copy is significant. Use the standardised extracts, use them for an appropriate period, and expect stress and sleep improvements — not dramatic body composition changes.

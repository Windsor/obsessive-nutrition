# Education in Portugal: Complete Guide for Expat Families 2026

Moving to Portugal with children? Understanding the education system is crucial for a smooth transition. This guide covers everything from public schools and private international options to university education and homeschooling in Portugal.

## The Portuguese Education System

Portugal's education system is divided into several stages:

- **Pre-school (Pré-escolar)**: Ages 3-6, optional but widely available
- **Basic Education (Ensino Básico)**: Ages 6-15, compulsory
  - 1st Cycle (1º Ciclo): Ages 6-10, grades 1-4
  - 2nd Cycle (2º Ciclo): Ages 10-12, grades 5-6
  - 3rd Cycle (3º Ciclo): Ages 12-15, grades 7-9
- **Secondary Education (Ensino Secundário)**: Ages 15-18, grades 10-12
- **Higher Education (Ensino Superior)**: Universities and polytechnics

Education is compulsory from age 6 to 18 (or completion of secondary school).

## Public Schools (Escolas Públicas)

### Overview

Portuguese public schools are free for all residents, including expat children. While the system has improved significantly in recent decades (Portugal now scores above OECD average in PISA), quality varies by region and school.

### Enrollment

To enroll your child in a public school:

1. **Get your NIF** (tax number) and register as a resident
2. **Visit the local school** or agrupamento de escolas (school cluster)
3. **Required documents**: Passport, proof of address, previous school records, vaccination records, NIF
4. **Enrollment period**: Usually April-June for the following September
5. **Late arrivals**: Schools accept mid-year enrollments, subject to availability

### What to Expect

- **Language**: All instruction is in Portuguese — no exceptions
- **School year**: Mid-September to mid-June
- **Hours**: Typically 9am-3:30pm (1st cycle) or 8:30am-5pm (2nd/3rd cycle and secondary)
- **Holidays**: Christmas (2 weeks), Carnival (3 days), Easter (2 weeks), summer (mid-June to mid-September)
- **Class size**: 20-26 students per class
- **Curriculum**: National curriculum set by Ministry of Education
- **Grading**: 1-5 scale (basic), 0-20 scale (secondary and university)

### Language Support

Many schools in areas with significant expat populations offer **Portuguese as a Non-Native Language (PLNM)** classes. These help children learn Portuguese while following the regular curriculum. However:

- PLNM availability varies by school and area
- Large cities (Lisbon, Porto) and the Algarve have better support
- Smaller towns may have limited or no PLNM provision
- Younger children (under 8-9) typically acquire Portuguese within 6-12 months through immersion
- Older children may need more structured support

### Pros and Cons of Public Schools

**Pros:**
- Free education
- Full immersion in Portuguese language and culture
- Children integrate with local community
- Generally safe and caring environments
- Good foundation in core subjects

**Cons:**
- Language barrier initially challenging
- Teaching style can be more traditional/lecture-based
- Facilities vary significantly between schools
- Limited extracurricular activities compared to private schools
- Parent-teacher communication may require Portuguese

## Private Schools (Escolas Privadas)

### Portuguese Private Schools

Portuguese-curriculum private schools offer smaller class sizes, better facilities, and sometimes bilingual programs. They follow the national curriculum but with more resources.

- **Cost**: €300-700/month depending on level and school
- **Language**: Portuguese, sometimes with enhanced English programs
- **Advantages**: Smaller classes (15-20), better facilities, more extracurriculars
- **Examples**: Colégio Efanor (Porto), Colégio Moderno (Lisbon)

### International Schools

International schools follow foreign curricula (British, American, French, German, IB) and teach primarily in English or another foreign language. They're the most popular choice for expat families, especially those who may not stay permanently.

#### Lisbon Area

- **St. Julian's School**: British curriculum + IB, ages 3-18. One of Portugal's oldest international schools. €8,000-18,000/year
- **Carlucci American International School of Lisbon (CAISL)**: American/IB curriculum, ages 3-18. €12,000-22,000/year
- **St. Dominic's International School**: IB curriculum, ages 3-18. €10,000-19,000/year
- **Deutsche Schule Lissabon**: German curriculum, ages 3-18. €5,000-8,000/year
- **Lycée Français Charles Lepierre**: French curriculum, ages 3-18. €4,000-7,000/year
- **Prime School International**: IB/Cambridge, ages 3-18. €7,000-14,000/year
- **TASIS Portugal**: American/IB, ages 3-18. €14,000-24,000/year (newer school)

#### Porto Area

- **Oporto British School**: British curriculum, ages 3-18. €6,000-12,000/year
- **CLIP (Colégio Luso-Internacional do Porto)**: IB curriculum, ages 3-18. €8,000-15,000/year
- **Deutsche Schule zu Porto**: German curriculum, ages 3-18. €4,000-7,000/year
- **Lycée Français International de Porto**: French, ages 3-18. €4,000-6,500/year

#### Algarve

- **Nobel International School**: British/IB, ages 3-18. €7,000-14,000/year
- **Colégio Santiago International School**: British, ages 3-16. €5,000-10,000/year
- **International School of the Algarve (Porches)**: British, ages 3-11. €5,000-8,000/year

#### Other Regions

- **International School of Coimbra**: Ages 3-18
- **International School of Palmela**: Near Setúbal, ages 3-18

### Choosing Between Public and International

| Factor | Public School | International School |
|--------|--------------|---------------------|
| Cost | Free | €5,000-24,000/year |
| Language | Portuguese only | English/bilingual |
| Integration | Deep local integration | International community |
| Curriculum | Portuguese national | British/American/IB |
| Class size | 20-26 | 12-20 |
| University prep | Portuguese universities | Global universities |
| Best for | Long-term/permanent moves | Short-term or uncertain stays |

## Pre-School and Childcare

### Public Pre-school (Jardim de Infância)

- Ages 3-6, free at public institutions
- Hours: Typically 9am-3:30pm, with extended hours available (ATL)
- High demand — apply early
- Portuguese language only

### Private Pre-school and Nurseries

- **Creche** (nursery): Ages 0-3, €250-600/month
- **Jardim de Infância** (pre-school): Ages 3-6, €200-500/month
- Many offer bilingual programs (Portuguese-English)
- Look for IPSS institutions (social solidarity) — subsidized rates based on income

### Childcare Tips

- Waiting lists for good creches can be 1+ year in Lisbon/Porto
- IPSS institutions charge on a sliding scale — can be very affordable
- Au pairs are legal in Portugal and increasingly popular with expat families
- Babysitting costs €6-10/hour in cities

## University and Higher Education

### Portuguese Universities

Portugal has excellent universities at very affordable prices:

- **University of Lisbon**: Portugal's largest, ranked in global top 200
- **University of Porto**: Strong in engineering, medicine, sciences
- **University of Coimbra**: One of the world's oldest (1290), UNESCO World Heritage
- **NOVA University Lisbon**: Strong in business and social sciences
- **Católica**: Private, strong in law, business, and economics

### Tuition Fees

- **Public universities (Portuguese/EU students)**: €697/year (fixed by law)
- **Public universities (non-EU students)**: €1,500-7,000/year
- **Private universities**: €3,000-12,000/year
- **MBA programs**: €10,000-30,000 total

### English-Taught Programs

An increasing number of programs are taught in English, especially:

- Master's and MBA programs
- Engineering and technology courses
- Some bachelor's programs at NOVA, Católica, and international universities

### Admission

- **Portuguese students**: National exam results + school grades
- **International students**: Application process varies; many accept IB, A-Levels, AP scores
- **Erasmus**: Portugal is a top Erasmus destination — 15,000+ exchange students/year

## Homeschooling in Portugal

Homeschooling (ensino doméstico) is legal in Portugal but regulated:

### Requirements

- **Registration**: Must register with the local school cluster (agrupamento)
- **Curriculum**: Must follow Portuguese national curriculum objectives
- **Assessment**: Children are assessed annually by the registered school
- **Qualified supervisor**: The teaching parent should demonstrate ability to teach
- **Records**: Must maintain portfolio of work

### Practical Considerations

- Authorities are generally supportive if you follow the process
- Growing homeschooling community, especially among expats
- Facebook groups and co-ops exist in Lisbon, Porto, and Algarve
- Some families combine homeschooling with part-time international school attendance

## Special Education Needs (SEN)

Portugal has an inclusive education policy:

- All schools must accept children with special needs
- **Decree-Law 54/2018** guarantees inclusive education and individual support plans
- Public schools have SEN coordinators and support teams
- Services include speech therapy, occupational therapy, and psychological support
- Quality varies — specialized private schools exist for significant needs
- **CRI (Centro de Recursos para a Inclusão)**: Community resources providing therapy and support

## Extracurricular Activities

Portuguese schools offer fewer extracurriculars than some countries, but options exist:

- **ATL (Atividades de Tempos Livres)**: After-school programs, common in primary schools
- **Sports clubs**: Football, swimming, tennis, padel, martial arts
- **Music schools**: Municipal music schools (conservatórios) offer affordable lessons
- **Language schools**: British Council, Cambridge schools, Alliance Française
- **Scouting**: CNE (Corpo Nacional de Escutas) is popular
- **Coding/STEM**: Growing number of after-school programs in cities

## Practical Tips for Expat Families

### Before You Move

1. Research schools in your target area early — international schools have waiting lists
2. Get all school records apostilled/translated
3. Contact target schools directly for admission requirements
4. Budget for education costs if choosing private/international
5. Consider your timeline — are you in Portugal permanently?

### First Year

1. Be patient with language acquisition — it takes 6-18 months for fluency
2. Connect with other expat families through school parent groups
3. Supplement with Portuguese lessons if in international school
4. Encourage local friendships outside the expat bubble
5. Attend school events even if you don't speak Portuguese fluently

### Language Transition Tips

- Children under 7-8 typically adapt fastest through immersion
- Ages 8-12 may need PLNM support and patience
- Teenagers (13+) face the hardest transition — consider international school if staying short-term
- Private Portuguese tutoring (€15-25/hour) accelerates learning
- Portuguese cartoons, games, and social activities help younger children

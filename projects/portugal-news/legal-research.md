## 1. Portuguese Press & Media Law (ERC)

### The Regulatory Body
The **Entidade Reguladora para a Comunicação Social (ERC)** is Portugal's media regulator, established by Law No. 53/2005 of 8 November 2005. It regulates and supervises all organisations carrying out media activities in Portugal. Address: Av. 24 de Julho, 58, 1200-869 Lisboa.
- Website: www.erc.pt
- Online registration portal: erc.pt (área de Registo OCS)

### The Key Law: Lei de Imprensa (Press Law)
**Lei n.º 2/99, de 13 de Janeiro** is the main Press Law governing publications in Portugal. It applies to both print AND online publications. Key points:

**Does an online news site need to register with ERC?**  
**YES — if it is a periodic publication open to the general public.** Per Article 13 of Decreto Regulamentar n.º 8/99, de 9 de Junho (as amended by Decreto Regulamentar n.º 7/2021, de 6 de dezembro):
> Electronic publications are subject to registration because the Press Law applies to them equally, regardless of technological support.

Registration is required for any "publicação periódica" (periodic publication) that:
- Has a fixed frequency/periodicity
- Is intended to continue indefinitely
- Contains doctrinal or informational content (text or images)
- Is available to the general public

### What Needs to Register
Per the ERC, the following must register:
- Periodic publications (publicações periódicas)
- Journalistic companies (empresas jornalísticas)
- News agencies (empresas noticiosas)
- Radio/TV operators and services
- Internet-only radio/TV services
- On-demand audiovisual services
- Video-sharing platforms

**A news/newsletter website would fall under "publicação periódica electrónica"** and registration is required.

### Registration Process (Step by Step)

**Step 1: Provisional Registration**
Submit to ERC (online via Portal dos Registos, by post, or in person):
- Completed registration form
- **Estatuto editorial** (editorial statute) — explains: subject matter, type of information, expected page count, distribution area, projected circulation, and editorial policy
- A natural-size copy of the publication's logo/title
- Declaration of acceptance of the role by the **Director** (see below)
- Declaration of the contractual relationship between editor and owner (if separate legal entities)
- Company incorporation documents / commercial register certificate (Certidão Permanente), or access code

**Step 2: Conversion to Definitive Registration**
Within 90 days of provisional registration notification, submit **proof of first publication/edition**. Must also deposit the editorial statute (for informative publications). If not done within 90 days, the registration expires.

**Step 3: Ongoing Obligations**
- Report any changes to registration within 30 days
- Maintain publication at registered frequency (or notify ERC of suspension)
- Send ERC a paper copy containing the editorial statute and audited accounts in the first 6 months of each year (if registered as journalistic company with organised accounts)

### The Director Requirement
Every periodic publication must have a named **Director (Diretor)** who:
- Is formally accepted via declaration
- Bears editorial responsibility
- Must be a resident person with full legal capacity
- Does NOT need to be a licensed journalist (the Lei de Imprensa does not restrict ownership or directorship to journalists)

> Per ERC FAQ: "A Lei nº.2/99, de 13 de Janeiro (Lei de Imprensa) não estabelece qualquer restrição quanto à sua fundação de jornais e outra publicações, as quais podem ser propriedade de qualquer pessoa singular ou colectiva." (There are no restrictions on who can found newspapers or publications — any natural or legal person can do so.)

### Article 15 Obligations (Publication Imprint)
Every periodic publication must display on the first page/header of each issue:
- Publication title
- Date
- Frequency/periodicity
- Name of the Director
- Unit price (if applicable)
- Owner's name and registered address
- Registration number

For online publications, this translates to the mandatory display of this information on the website (typically in a dedicated "About" or footer section).

### Foreign-Owned Publications
No restrictions on foreign ownership of Portuguese publications. A UK, US, or any other nationality individual or company may own and operate a Portuguese-registered publication.

### Is Registration Mandatory or Just Recommended?
Registration is legally required for periodic publications open to the public. However, enforcement against small online publications is inconsistent in practice. That said:
- Operating without registration exposes you to regulatory risk
- ERC can compel registration and impose fines
- Being registered confers legitimacy and may help with press credentials
- Some platforms and PR contacts may ask for your ERC registration number

### Registration Fees
Fees are published on the ERC website (erc.pt/pt/fs/taxas). These are modest regulatory fees, typically in the range of tens to a few hundred euros per year depending on publication type and size.

### Periodicity Suspension Rules
If you need to pause publication, you must notify ERC. Suspension limits by periodicity:
- Daily publications: up to 2 months/year
- Up to monthly: up to 4 months/year
- Up to quarterly: up to 6 months/year
- Up to semi-annual: up to 1 year
- Up to annual: up to 2 years

Exceeding the suspension period can result in automatic registration cancellation.

## 2. Business Structure

### Options for Running the Publication

#### Option A: Freelancer / Recibos Verdes (Sole Trader — Trabalhador Independente)
- Simplest to set up — register with the Tax Authority (AT) as self-employed
- Pay IRS (personal income tax) on income
- No separate legal entity — you are personally liable for all obligations
- Best for: very early stage, pre-revenue, testing the concept
- **Limitation:** If the business grows significantly, unlimited personal liability becomes a risk. Also may not be taken as seriously for media partnerships.

#### Option B: Empresário em Nome Individual (ENI — Sole Proprietorship)
- A formal sole proprietorship business structure
- Still personally liable (no liability shield)
- Slightly more formal than recibos verdes
- Same tax treatment as freelancer

#### Option C: Sociedade Unipessoal por Quotas (Unipessoal Lda.)
- **Single-member limited liability company** — the most recommended structure for a solo founder
- Limited liability — your personal assets are protected
- Minimum share capital: **€1** (though having more is practical)
- Separate legal entity from the founder
- Can register for ERC as a legal person
- Corporate tax (IRC) applies to company profits
- Setup cost: ~€360 via "Empresa na Hora" service (instant setup) or via notary/lawyer
- Best for: once you're taking income from the business, have subscribers/advertisers, or want credibility

#### Option D: Sociedade por Quotas (Lda. — Standard Private Limited Company)
- Multi-shareholder version of the above
- Minimum capital: **€1 per shareholder**
- Appropriate if launching with co-founders/partners
- Same liability protection as Unipessoal Lda.

### Special Requirements for Media Companies
Under Lei de Imprensa (Art. 2/99), there are **no special legal requirements for ownership** of a publication beyond general company law. However, **Lei n.º 78/2015, de 29 de julho** requires transparency about media company ownership, management, equity, and funding — information that must be disclosed to ERC.

**Companies with multiple shareholders** that have as their **primary activity** the editing of periodic publications must also register as **"empresas jornalísticas"** with ERC.

### Recommended Path for a Solo Expat Founder
1. Start as **Trabalhador Independente** (recibos verdes) to test the concept
2. Once generating revenue, incorporate a **Sociedade Unipessoal por Quotas (Unipessoal Lda.)**
3. Register the publication with ERC
4. Hire a local **Contabilista Certificado (TOC)** for accounting — required for Lda.

### Setting Up in Portugal as a Non-Resident
Non-EU citizens will need a valid Portuguese residency visa (e.g., D7, digital nomad visa, etc.) to operate a business in Portugal. EU citizens can establish themselves freely. A **NIF (número de identificação fiscal)** is required for all business activities.

## 3. Copyright & Fair Use

### Portugal Has No "Fair Use" — But Has Exceptions
Portugal operates under **civil law**, not common law. There is **no "fair use" doctrine** as in the US or UK. Copyright is governed by:
- **Código do Direito de Autor e dos Direitos Conexos (CDADC)** — Decree-Law n.º 63/85, de 14 de março, substantially amended multiple times, most recently by Decreto-Lei n.º 47/2023.
- **EU Directive 2001/29/EC** (InfoSoc Directive) — implemented in Portugal
- **EU Directive 2019/790** (DSM Copyright Directive) — implemented in Portugal via amendments to the CDADC

### What You CAN Do: Permitted Uses for News Reporting

**1. Quotation for Commentary/Criticism (Art. 75 CDADC)**
Quoting short excerpts from protected works is permitted when:
- The quotation is for the purpose of criticism, commentary, education, or information
- The source and author are clearly credited
- The amount quoted is proportionate to the purpose (not wholesale reproduction)
- The work has been lawfully published

**2. Reporting Current Events (Art. 75(2) CDADC)**
Reproduction of short excerpts of works for the purpose of reporting current events is permitted, provided:
- The reproduction is to the extent justified by the informational purpose
- The source is clearly identified

**3. News Summaries / Paraphrasing**
*Facts cannot be copyrighted.* You can freely:
- Report on the facts contained in a Portuguese news article
- Translate facts and news events into English
- Summarize what has happened
- Quote short extracts with attribution

What you CANNOT do without permission:
- Copy entire articles verbatim
- Republish large portions of protected text
- Republish photos/images without license (unless in the public domain or under Creative Commons)
- Create a "translation" that is simply a word-for-word rendering of a copyrighted article

### The EU Copyright Directive (DSM Directive 2019/790) — The "Press Publishers' Right"

**Article 15 (formerly Article 11)** — "The Press Publishers' Right" — is the most important piece of EU copyright law to understand for aggregation/translation.

**What it creates:**
A new 2-year neighbouring right for **press publishers** (professional news organisations that invest in content production) against **Information Society Service Providers (ISSPs)** — primarily large tech platforms like Google News, Bing News, etc.

**Key nuances for your publication:**

1. **It protects Portuguese news publishers FROM aggregators, not FROM you.** As a publisher reporting on their content, you are not an "ISSP aggregating their content at scale." You are a journalist/commentator engaging with the news.

2. **Individual words and very short extracts are explicitly exempted.** Per the Directive, using individual words or "very short extracts" does not trigger the press publishers' right.

3. **Private or non-commercial uses by individual users are exempt.** If you're writing a newsletter as a solo creator, you're closer to an individual user commenting on news than a tech platform systematically scraping content.

4. **Portugal's implementation note:** Portugal's transposition of the DSM Directive into the CDADC (via Article 176) adds press publications as a category of protected works — but enforcement has focused on tech platforms, not small English-language commentary publications.

5. **Translation:** Translating an article from Portuguese to English is technically a "derivative work" requiring the original rightsholder's permission. However, translating *the facts* (not the expression) is not infringement. In practice: **translate and transform — don't copy and paste verbatim** (even in translation).

### Practical Guidelines for Your Publication

**Safe Practices:**
- Write your own original reporting based on multiple Portuguese sources
- Credit all sources explicitly (link back to originals)
- Quote only short extracts (1-3 sentences maximum)
- Always add original commentary or analysis
- Take your own photos or use licensed/royalty-free stock photos
- For translated content: substantially transform and add value, don't just translate verbatim
- Consider reaching out to Portuguese publications for a formal content partnership/license

**Riskier Practices (avoid without permission):**
- Publishing full-length translations of articles
- Using images scraped from Portuguese news sites
- "Aggregating" large amounts of content without transformation
- Presenting Portuguese news content as your own without attribution

### Images
Images from Portuguese news sites are fully copyrighted. Use:
- Your own original photography
- Creative Commons licensed images (with correct attribution)
- Stock photo services (Getty, Shutterstock, Unsplash, Pexels)
- Publicly available government/official press releases (often Crown-equivalent public domain)

## 4. GDPR Compliance

### Overview
The **General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR — Regulation 2016/679)** applies across the EU including Portugal. Portugal's national implementing authority is the **CNPD (Comissão Nacional de Proteção de Dados)**.

For a newsletter-based publication collecting email addresses from subscribers, GDPR compliance is essential. Fines can reach €20 million or 4% of annual global turnover.

### Email Newsletter: Legal Basis for Data Processing

For marketing newsletters, **explicit consent (Article 6(1)(a) GDPR)** is the standard legal basis. Requirements:

**Valid Consent Must Be:**
- **Freely given** — not a condition of accessing content (no forced subscribe)
- **Specific** — the subscriber knows exactly what they're signing up for
- **Informed** — you explain clearly what you'll send and how often
- **Unambiguous** — requires a clear affirmative action (pre-ticked boxes are INVALID)
- **Revocable** — easy unsubscribe mechanism in every email

### Practical Newsletter Compliance Checklist

**Sign-up form must include:**
- [ ] Clear description of what emails will contain
- [ ] Explicit consent checkbox (not pre-ticked)
- [ ] Link to Privacy Policy
- [ ] Confirmation email with double opt-in (strongly recommended)

**Each email must include:**
- [ ] Your full business name and address
- [ ] Easy, functional unsubscribe link
- [ ] Information about how data is used

**Your website must have:**
- [ ] **Privacy Policy** covering: data controller identity, what data is collected, legal basis, retention periods, data subject rights (access, erasure, portability, rectification), right to complain to CNPD, third-party processors
- [ ] **Cookie Policy** (if using tracking cookies/analytics)
- [ ] **Cookie Consent Banner** for non-essential cookies (analytics, ads)

**Backend requirements:**
- [ ] Record of consent (timestamp, IP, checkbox version) — keep this for 3+ years
- [ ] Data Processing Agreements with email service providers (e.g., Mailchimp, Mailerlite, Beehiiv, Substack)
- [ ] Process for handling Subject Access Requests (DSAR) within 30 days
- [ ] Process for handling unsubscribe requests promptly (immediately if possible, max 10 business days)

### ePrivacy Directive (Cookie Law)
In addition to GDPR, Portugal implements the ePrivacy Directive (Directive 2002/58/EC). This requires:
- Cookie consent for non-essential cookies (analytics, advertising)
- Clear, granular consent options
- No pre-ticked consent boxes

### Data Subject Rights Under GDPR
Subscribers have the right to:
- **Access** their data
- **Rectify** incorrect data
- **Erasure** ("right to be forgotten") — delete from your lists on request
- **Restrict processing**
- **Data portability**
- **Object** to processing
- **Withdraw consent** at any time

### CNPD Registration
Small publishers do not generally need to register with CNPD unless processing special categories of data. However, you must maintain a Record of Processing Activities (ROPA) internally if you have 250+ employees (you won't — but good practice anyway).

### Tools for Compliance
- **Email platforms:** Mailchimp, Mailerlite, Beehiiv, Substack — all have GDPR features built in; use their EU-based processing options
- **Cookie consent:** Cookieyes, Cookiebot, or similar
- **Privacy policy generator:** Use a professional tool or lawyer — do not rely solely on free generators for a commercial publication

## 5. Tax Implications

### VAT (IVA) on Digital Subscriptions

**VAT Rates in Portugal (2025/2026):**
- Standard rate: **23%** (mainland Portugal)
- First reduced rate: **13%**
- Second reduced rate: **6%**

**Key rule:** Newspapers, books (print and electronic), and similar publications are taxed at the **reduced 6% VAT rate** in Portugal. This includes digital news subscriptions.

However, the exact classification matters:
- A **digital news subscription** (access to editorial content) likely qualifies for the 6% rate, similar to e-books and newspapers
- **Generic "newsletter subscriptions"** that are more service-like could be treated as standard services at 23%
- Advertising revenue is standard-rated at 23%

**Always confirm your specific product classification with a Portuguese tax accountant (TOC).**

### VAT Registration Threshold
- From 2025: threshold is **€15,000 annual turnover**
- Below this threshold, you do not need to charge/collect VAT
- Once you exceed it, mandatory VAT registration with the **Autoridade Tributária e Aduaneira (AT)**
- Can register online at eportal.gov.pt

### VAT on Digital Services to EU Customers (OSS)
If you're selling subscriptions to customers in OTHER EU countries (not just Portugal), you must consider the **VAT One Stop Shop (OSS)** rules:
- If cross-border digital service revenue exceeds **€10,000/year**, you must charge VAT in the customer's country (or use OSS to file centrally)
- Substack/Ghost/Beehiiv may handle this automatically for platform-based subscriptions
- For your own website with direct subscriptions, consult a tax advisor

### Income Tax

**As a Sole Trader (Recibos Verdes / Trabalhador Independente):**
Progressive IRS rates apply (2025):
| Income Bracket | Rate |
|----------------|------|
| Up to €7,703 | 13% |
| €7,703–€11,623 | 18% |
| €11,623–€16,472 | 23% |
| €16,472–€21,321 | 26% |
| €21,321–€27,146 | 32.75% |
| €27,146–€39,791 | 37% |
| €39,791–€51,997 | 43.5% |
| €51,997–€81,199 | 45% |
| Over €81,199 | 48% |

For freelancers under the **simplified regime (regime simplificado)**, business income from services is taxed on 75% of gross income (25% is deemed costs). Minimum income for Social Security contributions applies.

**As a Lda. (Company):**
Corporate tax (IRC) in Portugal (2025): **20%** standard rate
- Reduced rate for SMEs: **17%** on first €50,000 of taxable income (as of 2025 budget)
- Municipal surcharge (Derrama): up to 1.5% of taxable income
- State surcharge on profits over €1.5 million: 5-9%
- Note: IRC is gradually being reduced to 17% by 2028 per recent government plans

### Social Security Contributions
As a self-employed person (trabalhador independente) in Portugal:
- Social security contributions: 21.4% on 70% of gross income (or 20% if registered as other categories)
- First 12 months of self-employment: may be exempt from SS contributions (check current rules)
- These cover healthcare, pension contributions

### NHR (Non-Habitual Resident) Status
The original NHR tax regime (flat 20% on Portugal-source income, foreign income exemptions) was **discontinued from January 1, 2024** for new applicants. It has been replaced with the **IFICI (Incentivo Fiscal à Investigação Científica e Inovação)** — "NHR 2.0" — which is more restrictive and targeted at specific highly-skilled sectors. Media/journalism is unlikely to qualify unless working in a tech/research capacity.

If you applied for NHR before the cutoff and were approved, you continue to benefit from the old regime for your 10-year period.

### Annual Tax Filing
- Personal income tax (IRS): file by **June 30** each year for the previous year
- Corporate tax (IRC): companies must file the periodic income declaration (Declaração Periódica de Rendimentos — Model 22) by **May 31** each year
- VAT returns: monthly (if turnover ≥ €650k) or quarterly (if less)

## 6. Existing Competitors

### Major English-Language Portugal News Publications

#### 1. The Portugal News (theportugalnews.com)
- **Founded:** 1977 — the oldest and longest-running English-language newspaper in Portugal
- **Model:** Print (weekly, every Friday) + website + multilingual online content
- **Reach:** Claims 400,000 readers/week; audited print circulation ~20,000+ copies/week
- **Content:** National news, politics, property, lifestyle, sports — general interest
- **Revenue:** Advertising (print and digital), some subscription
- **Website:** 155,000+ page views/week (2019 figures — likely higher now)
- **Coverage:** National, though historically Algarve-focused
- **Tone:** Established, mainstream, somewhat general
- **Gap:** Limited political depth, analysis, or accountability journalism

#### 2. Portugal Resident (portugalresident.com)
- **Founded:** 1989
- **Owner:** Open Media Group (also publishes Essential Algarve, Essential Madeira)
- **Model:** Print tabloid + website; free in hotels/cafés, newsagents
- **Coverage:** Primarily Algarve-oriented English expat community
- **Content:** News, real estate, business, lifestyle, sports
- **Tone:** Tabloid style — often described as "translations of Portuguese news" without much original reporting
- **Revenue:** Advertising-heavy (property ads prominent)
- **Gap:** Light on political analysis, Lisbon/Porto coverage, intellectual depth

#### 3. Algarve Daily News (algarverealestate.com / algarvedailynews.com)
- **Model:** Online-only
- **Focus:** Regional news for the Algarve specifically
- **Content:** Local news, tourism, property, expat lifestyle
- **Revenue:** Advertising, property listings

#### 4. Expatica Portugal (expatica.com/pt)
- **Parent:** International Expatica network (Netherlands-based)
- **Model:** Free website, content aggregation + guides
- **Content:** How-to guides for expats (visas, banking, healthcare), not primarily news
- **Revenue:** Advertising, affiliate links
- **Gap:** Not a news publication — more of a relocation guide

#### 5. Portugalist (portugalist.com)
- **Model:** Independent content site, primarily SEO-driven
- **Content:** Moving to Portugal guides, lifestyle, cost of living — NOT political news
- **Revenue:** Affiliate marketing, advertising
- **Audience:** People considering moving to Portugal, not already-established expats

#### 6. Portuguese American Journal
- **Model:** US-based, covering Portuguese diaspora in the USA
- **Content:** Cultural, community news for Portuguese-Americans
- Not directly competitive for Portugal-based expat news

#### 7. Substack Newsletter: "Expat in Portugal" (expatinportugal.substack.com)
- **Model:** Personal Substack with "thousands of subscribers"
- **Content:** Personal/lifestyle content about moving to Portugal
- Not a news publication

### What's MISSING in the Market
A significant gap exists for:
- **Political analysis and accountability journalism** in English (covering Assembleia da República, government policy, corruption)
- **Curated, quality translation** of significant Portuguese news with expert commentary
- **Portugal-wide** (not Algarve-centric) English-language publication
- **Newsletter format** with paid subscriber model (no one is doing this well)
- **Data journalism** on Portugal (immigration stats, housing data, tax policy, etc.)
- **Content that treats expats as engaged citizens** rather than just lifestyle consumers

## 7. Revenue Models

### For a Niche English-Language Expat News Publication

#### Model 1: Substack / Newsletter-First
**Platform:** Substack (substack.com)
- Free to start; Substack takes **10%** of paid subscription revenue + Stripe fees (~2.9% + $0.30/transaction)
- Hosts newsletter, website, podcast all in one
- Built-in discovery network and recommendation system
- Adds sponsorship/advertising beta (as of late 2025)
- **Best for:** Solo creator, starting out, building audience first
- **Typical conversion:** 5-10% of free subscribers convert to paid
- **Price points:** €5-10/month or €50-100/year are standard for niche newsletters
- **Upside:** No platform costs until you earn. Built-in community features.
- **Downside:** 10% cut is significant at scale; Substack owns the platform relationship

**Alternative newsletter platforms:**
- **Ghost** (ghost.org) — self-hosted or managed, 0% commission on subscriptions, flat monthly fee (~$9–$99/month). Best for higher-revenue operations.
- **Beehiiv** — newsletter platform with no commission, strong analytics, supports ads
- **Mailchimp / Mailerlite** — email sending only; pair with Stripe for payments

#### Model 2: Advertising
- Display advertising (Google AdSense, direct banner ads)
- Sponsorships from relevant brands targeting Portugal expats: property agencies, immigration lawyers, language schools, relocation services, banks (Atlantico, Millennium BCP), insurance, etc.
- Estimated CPMs for niche expat audience: €5-15 CPM for display; sponsored posts can command €200-2,000 depending on audience size
- **Minimum viable audience for meaningful ad revenue:** 10,000+ monthly unique visitors

#### Model 3: Subscriptions
- **Freemium model:** Free access to most content; paid subscribers get deeper analysis, weekly briefings, archives, Q&A access
- Target price: **€7-10/month** or **€70-100/year**
- Portuguese expat community is affluent and accustomed to paying for quality content
- Reference: The Portugal News does not have a paywall; there's a clear gap for a quality paid product

#### Model 4: Sponsorships / Partnerships
Ideal sponsors for Portugal expat news:
- Real estate agencies (Engel & Völkers, JLL, Coldwell Banker Portugal)
- Immigration lawyers (lots of English-speaking firms)
- Relocation consultancies
- Language schools
- Expat banks and fintech (Wise, Revolut, Millennium BCP expat accounts)
- Healthcare providers (private insurance for expats)
- Portugal tourism-related businesses

Typical sponsored newsletter/article rates for 1,000–5,000 subscribers: **€100–500 per send**; scales linearly.

#### Model 5: Events
- Virtual Q&As or webinars (e.g., "Understanding Portuguese politics" with guest speakers)
- In-person meetups/events for expat community
- Conference model (longer term)

#### Model 6: Affiliate Revenue
- Refer readers to lawyers, accountants, relocation services (earning referral fees)
- Financial products (bank accounts, insurance, VPNs, etc.)
- Course platforms for learning Portuguese

### Recommended Revenue Stack for a New Publication
**Phase 1 (0-1 year, 0-500 subscribers):**
- Free newsletter via Substack or Beehiiv
- Soft launch of paid tier at reasonable price
- Focus on audience building; no ads yet

**Phase 2 (1-2 years, 500-5,000 subscribers):**
- Paid subscription tier (€7-10/month)
- First sponsorships from targeted Portugal expat brands
- Consider transitioning to Ghost for lower fees

**Phase 3 (2+ years, 5,000+ subscribers):**
- Full advertising programme
- Events
- Potential print run or partnership with established outlet
- Staff/contributor expansion

## 8. Domain & Naming Considerations

### Using "Portugal" or "Portuguese" in the Name

**Good news:** There are **no specific legal restrictions** on using "Portugal" or "Portuguese" in a publication name in Portugal's Press Law. Unlike some countries where geographic terms in company names require official approval, the Lei de Imprensa places no such restriction on publication names.

**However, consider:**

1. **Company name approval:** If incorporating an Lda. or SA, your company name must be approved by the **Instituto dos Registos e do Notariado (IRN)** and cannot be identical or confusingly similar to an existing registered company name. There are already companies with "Portugal" in their media names — check for conflicts.

2. **Trademark conflicts:** The Portugal News (theportugalnews.com) and Portugal Resident (portugalresident.com) are established trademarks. Do not use names confusingly similar to these. Check INPI (Instituto Nacional da Propriedade Industrial) at inpi.pt for registered trademarks before choosing a name.

3. **Domain considerations:**
   - **.pt domains** are managed by **DNS.pt** (dns.pt)
   - To register a .pt domain, you need: a NIF (tax number), OR proof of trademark registration in Portugal, OR proof of established legal entity
   - .pt domains are more restrictive than .com — require Portuguese entity or trademark
   - **Recommendation:** Register both a **.com** and **.pt** version if possible
   - ".eu" domains are available to EU residents and businesses

4. **Geographic descriptors:** Using "Portugal" as a descriptor (e.g., "Portugal Daily Briefing", "The Lisbon Letter", "Atlantic Brief") is generally fine and less legally complex than implying official status (e.g., "Official Portugal News").

5. **Avoid state-resembling names:** Do not use names that could imply official government affiliation (e.g., "Portugal Government News" or "Official Portuguese Press").

### Trademark Registration (Optional but Recommended)
Once you've chosen a name, consider registering it as a trademark with **INPI Portugal** (inpi.pt). Benefits:
- Protects your brand from imitators
- Gives legal basis for .pt domain registration challenges
- Required for some advertising partnerships and press credential applications
- Cost: ~€185 for national trademark registration (1 class)

### Naming Strategy
Consider names that:
- Are distinctive and not just generic geographic descriptors
- Suggest quality, intelligence, insider knowledge (for the expat audience)
- Are easy for English speakers to type and remember
- Are available as .com and .pt domains

Examples of distinctive possible directions:
- "The Lisbon Dispatch" / "Lisbon Letter" (city-specific but not geographically limiting)
- "Portugal Unlocked" / "Portugal Decoded" (concept-based)
- "The Atlantic Brief" (evokes Portugal's location, not trademarked)
- "Cais" or other evocative Portuguese-but-accessible names

## 9. Liability & Defamation Law

### Constitutional Framework
Article 37 of the **Constituição da República Portuguesa** guarantees freedom of expression and information. Article 38 specifically guarantees press freedom. These are fundamental rights that Portuguese courts balance against individual rights to honour, reputation, and privacy.

### Civil Defamation (Difamação Civil)

**Key law:** Article 484 of the **Código Civil** states:
> "Anyone who affirms or spreads a fact that may damage the credit or good name of any natural or legal person is liable for damage caused to said person."

Compensation is awarded to restore the plaintiff to the position they would have been in without the violation. Damages reflect:
- Actual losses suffered
- Profits the plaintiff was prevented from realising
- Non-pecuniary damages (moral damages) for damage to reputation

Historically, Portuguese courts have been relatively restrained in awarding large defamation damages (compared to UK, for example), but awards are increasing.

### Criminal Defamation (Difamação Criminal)

**Código Penal, Articles 180–189** govern "Crimes Against Honour":

**Difamação (Defamation) — Article 180:**
Conduct of someone who:
1. Makes a factual allegation about another person (even expressed as suspicion) that offends their honour or reputation
2. Expresses an opinion about someone that offends their honour or reputation
3. Repeats such an allegation or opinion

*Penalty:* Up to **6 months imprisonment or 240 days' fine**

*Aggravated penalty (Article 183):*
- Where the defamation is committed in a way that facilitates public disclosure (e.g., media): **minimum 120 days' fine, maximum 2 years imprisonment**
- Where the defamer knew the facts were false: penalties increased by one-third

*If committed via media outlet:* Courts treat this as seriously aggravated. Prison sentences (even if suspended) are not uncommon.

**Injúria (Insult) — Article 181:**
Directing offensive statements directly at a person.
- Penalty: Up to 3 months imprisonment or 120 days' fine

**Statute of limitations:** 6 months from when the complainant first became aware of the facts and identity of the responsible party. (Very short — complaints must be filed quickly.)

### Defences Available to Publishers (Article 180 CP)
**Difamação is NOT punishable if:**
1. The allegation was made **in pursuit of a legitimate interest** (e.g., journalistic reporting of public interest facts within freedom of the press)
2. The allegation is **proved to be true**
3. There was a **strong and serious reason to believe, in good faith**, that the allegation was true

**But even with these defences:** If the statement relates to the private or family life of the person (rather than their public role), the publisher may still be found guilty even if the information is true.

### Right of Reply (Direito de Resposta)

**Constitutional basis:** Article 37(4) of the Constitution guarantees the right of reply and rectification.

**Under Lei de Imprensa (Art. 2/99):**
Any person who has been the subject of references in a periodic publication that may affect their reputation or good name — or that are erroneous or false — has the **right of reply and rectification**.

**Procedural requirements:**
- Right must be exercised within **30 days** (for daily or weekly publications) or **60 days** (for less frequent publications) of publication
- The invocation must be delivered in a way that proves the publication received it
- The response text must have a direct and relevant connection to what prompted it

**Publisher obligations:**
- Must publish the right of reply/rectification text **free of charge**
- Must publish it in the next edition after receipt
- Must give it the **same prominence** as the original reference

For online publications, this means the response must be published in a visible place on the website, with the same prominence as the original article.

### Privacy Considerations (GDPR + Civil Code)
Be careful about:
- Publishing personal data without consent
- "Doxxing" private individuals (addresses, personal details)
- Publishing information from private communications
- Writing about people who have not voluntarily entered public life

**Public figures (politicians, public officials, celebrities)** have reduced privacy expectations regarding their public roles, but retain privacy rights for their private/family life.

### Practical Liability Management for Your Publication
1. **Verify before publishing** — have evidence for factual claims
2. **Distinguish fact from opinion** — clearly label commentary as opinion
3. **Report on public interest matters** — the strongest legal defence
4. **Attribute all claims** — "According to X..." is safer than stating as absolute fact
5. **Have a right of reply mechanism** — publish it clearly on your site
6. **Consult a Portuguese media lawyer** before publishing anything investigative or accusatory
7. **Carry media liability insurance** — consider professional indemnity/media insurance
8. **Don't publish unverified allegations** about private individuals

### Journalists' Professional Card (Cartão de Jornalista)
Portugal has a formal journalist accreditation system administered by the **Comissão da Carteira Profissional de Jornalista (CCPJ)**. The professional journalist card provides certain legal protections (source confidentiality, etc.) but is not required to publish a media outlet or run a website. However, having accredited journalists on staff or writing for your outlet may strengthen your editorial credibility and legal defences.

## 10. EU Digital Services Act (DSA)

### Overview
The **Digital Services Act (Regulation 2022/2065)** entered into force in November 2022. It applies to all "online intermediary services" operating in the EU. The majority of provisions became applicable from **17 February 2024**.

### Does it Apply to a Small News Website?

**The key question:** Is a news website an "online intermediary service" under the DSA?

The DSA primarily targets:
- **Hosting services** (cloud, web hosting)
- **Online platforms** (social media, marketplaces, app stores)
- **Very Large Online Platforms (VLOPs)** and **Very Large Online Search Engines (VLOSEs)**
- Video-sharing platforms

**A news publication/editorial website is NOT an "online intermediary service"** in the traditional DSA sense — it is a content creator/publisher, not a platform for third-party content. Therefore, the DSA's primary obligations (moderation transparency reports, algorithm audits, etc.) do **not apply** to a pure editorial publication.

**However, some DSA obligations may apply if you:**
- Run a **comment section** (hosting user-generated content)
- Operate a **forum or community features**
- Allow **user uploads** of content

In such cases, you are acting as a hosting provider for that user-generated content.

### The Proportionality Principle
The DSA follows a "tiered" approach:

| Entity Size | DSA Classification | Obligations |
|---|---|---|
| Micro (<10 employees, <€2M turnover) | Micro enterprise | **Exempt from most obligations** |
| Small (<50 employees, <€10M turnover) | Small enterprise | **Exempt from most obligations** |
| Medium (≥50 employees OR ≥€10M) | Intermediary service | Standard DSA obligations apply |
| 45M+ EU monthly active users | VLOP/VLOSE | Full DSA + additional obligations |

**A new news publication will almost certainly qualify as a micro or small enterprise and be EXEMPT from most DSA obligations.**

### Minimum DSA Requirements (Even for Small Publishers)
Even if exempt from most obligations, some baseline requirements apply to all online services:
- Provide a **single point of contact** for authorities (an email address is sufficient)
- Be accessible to the **Digital Services Coordinator** (DSA national authority — in Portugal: the communications regulator ANACOM is the DSC)
- Comply with **GDPR** (separate from DSA but related)
- For any comment/UGC hosting: have a mechanism to receive and act on **illegal content notifications**

### If You Have a Comment Section
If you enable comments, you become a "hosting service provider" under the DSA. Minimum obligations for micro/small entities with comments:
- Have a way for users to **report illegal content** (e.g., CSAM, hate speech, terrorism)
- Act on clearly illegal content when notified
- Keep basic records of moderation decisions

**Practical recommendation:** Start without a comment section. Use social media or Substack's "discussion" feature instead. Add comments later once you understand the regulatory burden.

### E-Commerce Directive Context
The DSA replaces certain parts of the old **E-Commerce Directive (2000/31/EC)**. For editorial publishers with no UGC, the old "mere conduit" and hosting safe harbours are largely academic.

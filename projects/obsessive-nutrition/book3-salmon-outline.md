## Chapter 2: What Salmon Actually Is (~2,000 words)
### Species
- Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar) — the farmed standard
- Pacific species: Chinook/King (fattiest, most omega-3s), Sockeye (deepest red, high astaxanthin), Coho (balanced), Pink (most common canned), Chum (lowest omega-3s), Steelhead (technically a trout)
- Key nutritional differences between species — not all salmon are equal

### Composition per 100g (wild Atlantic, cooked)
- Protein: 25g (complete protein, all essential amino acids)
- Fat: 13g (predominantly MUFA and PUFA)
- Omega-3s: 2.2g (EPA + DHA combined) — among the highest of any commonly eaten fish
- Omega-6:Omega-3 ratio: approximately 0.3:1 (very favourable)
- Vitamin D: 526 IU (one of the richest food sources)
- Vitamin B12: 3.2μg (134% RDA)
- Selenium: 36μg (65% RDA)
- Astaxanthin: 0.5-3mg (wild) — the carotenoid responsible for pink colour and significant antioxidant activity

### Wild vs Farmed: The Nutritional Comparison
- Fat content: farmed is fattier (18g vs 13g per 100g) — more total omega-3s in absolute terms
- Omega-3 quality: wild has better EPA:DHA ratio; farmed fed fish meal/oil has declining omega-3 content as feed formulation shifts to plant-based
- Contaminants: farmed has higher PCBs, dioxins historically (improving with feed changes); wild Pacific has lower contaminant load than wild Atlantic
- Astaxanthin: wild get it from krill (natural); farmed get synthetic astaxanthin added to feed
- The honest verdict: both are excellent; wild Pacific (especially Sockeye) is the gold standard; farmed Norwegian is nutritionally solid and far preferable to most other protein sources

## Chapter 3: The Research — What We Know (~5,000 words)

### Cardiovascular Health (strongest evidence base)
- The Greenland Inuit studies (Bang & Dyerberg, 1970s) that launched omega-3 research
- EPA/DHA mechanisms: triglyceride reduction (RCTs show 20-30% reduction at therapeutic doses), anti-inflammatory eicosanoids, platelet aggregation, endothelial function
- GISSI-Prevenzione trial (1999, n=11,324): 15% reduction in cardiovascular mortality
- JELIS trial (2007, Japan, n=18,645): 19% reduction in major cardiovascular events
- ASCEND and VITAL trials (2018-2019): more modest effects in primary prevention — important nuance
- Current position: strong for secondary prevention (post-heart attack), more modest for primary prevention in low-risk populations
- Triglycerides: most robust individual endpoint — consistent 15-30% reduction in hypertriglyceridaemic patients

### Inflammation and Immune Function
- EPA and DHA as precursors to anti-inflammatory resolvins and protectins
- CRP and IL-6 reduction in meta-analyses of omega-3 supplementation
- Relevance to chronic disease: inflammation as common pathway in CVD, T2DM, cancer, neurodegeneration
- Rheumatoid arthritis: one of strongest non-cardiovascular evidence bases — consistent reduction in joint pain and morning stiffness in RCTs, reduced NSAID use

### Brain Health and Cognition
- DHA structural role: 60% of brain fat is DHA; concentrated in synaptic membranes and photoreceptors
- Depression: meta-analyses suggest benefit, particularly EPA-dominant formulations; effect size modest but comparable to some antidepressants in mild-moderate depression
- Cognitive decline: observational studies strongly positive; RCT evidence mixed (AREDS2, MAPT — weaker effects in prevention of decline)
- ADHD: growing evidence for benefit in children; several RCTs showing modest improvement in attention scores
- Dementia/Alzheimer's: DHA levels lower in Alzheimer's brain tissue; supplementation studies disappointing in established disease but promising in prevention phase

### Metabolic Health
- Insulin sensitivity: modest improvements in insulin resistance in RCTs — mechanisms include adiponectin upregulation, inflammatory pathway modulation
- NAFLD (non-alcoholic fatty liver disease): consistent improvement in liver fat, ALT/AST markers in multiple RCTs — one of the better-supported metabolic indications
- Type 2 diabetes: no direct preventive effect shown; but omega-3s reduce cardiovascular risk in diabetics significantly

### Cancer
- Most data observational; RCT evidence limited and mixed
- Colorectal cancer: most consistent signal — omega-3s reduce polyp recurrence in some trials
- Breast cancer: inverse association in observational data; mechanism unclear
- Prostate cancer: controversial — some studies suggest possible promotion at high doses; requires nuance
- General note: promising but not established; do not overclaim

### Vitamin D (salmon as a delivery vehicle)
- Vitamin D deficiency: affects ~40% of European adults
- 100g salmon provides 50-130% of RDA depending on species and preparation
- Vitamin D's roles: bone health (established), immune function (strong), cancer prevention (emerging), cardiovascular (mixed), mental health (emerging)
- Salmon is among the best dietary sources; particularly valuable in low-sunlight northern latitudes

### Astaxanthin
- Carotenoid with potent antioxidant activity (claimed to be 6,000x stronger than vitamin C — this requires context)
- Human RCT evidence: skin health (reduced UV damage, improved elasticity), exercise recovery (reduced muscle damage markers), eye health (reduced eye strain)
- Anti-inflammatory properties in vitro and animal models — human trial evidence building but not yet conclusive
- Emerging area: cognitive protection, mitochondrial function

## Chapter 4: What We Don't Know (~1,500 words)
- Optimal dose: how much EPA+DHA per day? Studies use 0.5g to 4g — response is likely non-linear
- EPA vs DHA: separate roles becoming clearer but still incompletely understood
- Farmed salmon omega-3 decline: as feed shifts from fish meal to plant-based, omega-3 content dropping 50% since 1990s — will continue
- Form: fish vs supplement — is there a matrix effect from eating whole fish?
- Interaction with omega-6 intake: the ratio may matter as much as absolute omega-3 intake
- Contaminants: long-term effects of low-level PCB exposure from farmed salmon remain debated
- Individual response variation: genetics (FADS1/FADS2 polymorphisms) affect omega-3 conversion and utilisation substantially

## Chapter 5: How Much, How Often, What Form (~2,000 words)
- WHO/FAO recommendation: 250-500mg EPA+DHA/day for general health
- For cardiovascular benefit: 1-2g EPA+DHA/day
- For triglyceride reduction: 3-4g/day (prescription omega-3 territory)
- Practical: 2-3 servings of fatty fish per week achieves 250-500mg/day target
- One 150g serving of salmon provides approximately 3g EPA+DHA — already above most recommendations
- Cooking impact: baking/poaching preserves omega-3s well; frying at high heat oxidises them
- Supplements: when food is insufficient or impractical — triglyceride form (Nordic Naturals, Carlson) superior to ethyl ester form for absorption; keep refrigerated; check IFOS certification for purity
- Pregnancy: particularly important — 200mg DHA/day minimum; avoid high-mercury species; salmon is safe at 2-3 servings/week
- Drug interactions: high-dose omega-3s potentiate anticoagulants (warfarin, aspirin) — relevant at doses >3g/day

## Chapter 6: Buying, Storing, Using (~1,500 words)
### Buying
- Wild Pacific vs farmed: preference for wild (Sockeye, Chinook) when budget allows; Norwegian farmed is the best farmed option
- "Atlantic salmon" in most supermarkets = farmed; "Pacific" or named species = likely wild
- Canned: pink salmon is wild Pacific and dramatically cheaper than fresh; sockeye canned is excellent
- Labels to look for: ASC (Aquaculture Stewardship Council) for farmed; MSC (Marine Stewardship Council) for wild
- Fresh smell: should smell oceanic, not "fishy" — strong fishy smell = oxidised omega-3s = lower quality

### Storing
- Fresh: use within 2 days; store at 0-2°C (coldest part of fridge)
- Frozen: excellent option — IQF (individually quick frozen) wild salmon retains nutrients well; use within 3 months
- Smoked: refrigerate; use within the use-by date; lower omega-3 retention than fresh/frozen

### Cooking
- Best methods for omega-3 preservation: baking (180°C, 12-15 min), poaching, steaming, sous vide
- Avoid: deep frying (oxidises fats); overcooking (dry salmon = degraded omega-3s)
- The 10-minute rule: 10 minutes per inch of thickness at 180°C; salmon is done when it flakes easily and is slightly translucent in the centre
- 5 recipes: miso-glazed baked salmon, poached salmon with herb oil, salmon poke bowl, smoked salmon scrambled eggs, canned salmon fishcakes

## Chapter 7: The Verdict (~500 words)
- Evidence strength: 9/10 for cardiovascular (secondary prevention); 7/10 for inflammation; 7/10 for brain health; 8/10 for vitamin D delivery
- Who benefits most: those with high triglycerides, cardiovascular risk, inflammatory conditions, depression, or living at northern latitudes (vitamin D)
- Red flags: none significant at food intake levels; high-dose supplements warrant medical supervision
- Our verdict: salmon deserves its superfood status more than almost any other food — the evidence base is unusually strong, the nutritional profile is exceptional, and the risks at normal dietary intake are minimal. Eat it twice a week.
